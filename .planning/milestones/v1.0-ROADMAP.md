# Roadmap: D&O Liability Underwriting Worksheet System

## Overview

This roadmap transforms a stock ticker into a comprehensive D&O underwriting worksheet through 12 phases that mirror the 7-stage pipeline architecture (RESOLVE, ACQUIRE, EXTRACT, ANALYZE, SCORE, BENCHMARK, RENDER) plus knowledge management, market intelligence, interactive UX, and actuarial pricing. Each phase delivers an independently testable capability: Phase 1 establishes the project skeleton and domain knowledge foundation, Phases 2-3 build the data acquisition and extraction pipeline, Phases 4-5 implement the analytical sections (market/governance/litigation), Phase 6 builds the scoring engine, Phase 7 adds peer benchmarking and the executive summary, Phase 8 renders the final Word/PDF/Markdown documents, and Phase 9 migrates domain knowledge from flat JSON files to a queryable, versionable knowledge store. The predecessor's 1.5MB of validated domain knowledge (359 checks, 10-factor scoring model, 17 composite patterns) is carried forward starting in Phase 1 and activated in Phase 6.

### Phase 9: Knowledge Store & Domain Intelligence
**Goal**: The system's domain knowledge (checks, patterns, scoring rules, sector baselines, red flags, and accumulated underwriting notes) is migrated from flat JSON config files to a queryable knowledge store with full check lifecycle management (incubation → development → active → deprecated), provenance tracking, learning/feedback loops, check optimization, and **industry-specialized analysis playbooks** -- deeply tailored expertise per industry vertical (biotech clinical trial risk, fintech regulatory exposure, energy environmental liability, etc.) with industry-specific checks, risk patterns, claim theories, and underwriting questions that make the system dramatically more useful for specialized underwriters.
**Depends on**: Phase 8 (system is functionally complete; this is a knowledge infrastructure improvement)
**Requirements**: None (new capability not in original requirements)
**Success Criteria** (what must be TRUE):
  1. All 359+ checks are stored with provenance metadata: origin (BRAIN/ migration, user-added, AI-generated), validation status (validated, provisional, deprecated), creation date, last modified date, and modification history
  2. Checks can be queried by any combination of: sector, factor, section, severity, status, allegation theory, and free-text search -- without grepping JSON files
  3. Each check has explicit traceability: data source dependencies (what ACQUIRE needs), evaluation logic (what ANALYZE does), output mapping (where in the worksheet it appears), and scoring impact (which factor it hits). Adding a new check surfaces any missing links in this chain.
  4. Check lifecycle is a first-class concept: INCUBATING (raw idea captured) → DEVELOPING (working out data/evaluation/output chain) → ACTIVE (all links complete, executes in pipeline) → DEPRECATED (preserved with reason). Underwriting ideas are captured immediately and developed into checks over time.
  5. Underwriting notes, additional knowledge, and synthesis (handwritten observations, calibration notes, edge case learnings) are stored alongside structured checks with full-text search capability
  6. When a check is modified or deprecated, the previous version is preserved with a reason for the change -- the system never silently loses knowledge
  7. Learning infrastructure: outcome tracking (did flagged risks materialize?), check effectiveness metrics (fire rate, false positive rate, correlation with claims), and check redundancy analysis (which checks always co-fire and could be consolidated or composed into narratives)
  8. Narrative composition: checks can be grouped into risk stories (e.g., "aggressive revenue recognition + insider selling + auditor change = restatement risk narrative") that are more useful to underwriters than individual signals
  9. Document ingestion: the system accepts external documents (short seller reports, claims studies, underwriter notes, industry analyses) and processes them into knowledge store artifacts -- spawning incubating checks, adjusting scoring parameters, or adding context to existing checks
  10. The scoring engine (Phase 6) and all downstream consumers read from the knowledge store instead of flat JSON files, with zero functional regression (all existing tests pass)
  11. Industry-specialized playbooks exist for at least 5 high-priority verticals (e.g., Technology/SaaS, Biotech/Pharma, Financial Services, Energy/Utilities, Healthcare). Each playbook contains: industry-specific checks (beyond generic), tailored risk patterns, industry claim theories with historical examples, sector-specific meeting prep questions, and industry-specific scoring weight adjustments
  12. Industry playbooks are activated automatically based on SIC/NAICS classification during RESOLVE, and the worksheet output includes an "Industry-Specific Risk Factors" section tailored to the company's vertical

### Phase 10.1: Pricing Model Enhancement *(INSERTED)*
**Goal**: The pricing database schema is reworked to reflect how D&O insurance market intelligence actually arrives in the real world — partial data is the norm, not the exception. An underwriter might know only a renewal date, or just one layer's pricing, or the broker but not the terms. The enhanced model supports: partial/incomplete program records that are still valuable, year-over-year tracking showing how programs evolve (carrier rotations, broker changes, pricing movement), broker and brokerage capture with contact information, proper policy year management, and flexible data entry where any combination of fields is useful. Critically, the system can ingest real-world artifacts — quota share binders, policies, program summaries, Excel tower spreadsheets — and extract pricing intelligence from them. The analytics engine is updated to work with this richer, more realistic data model.
**Depends on**: Phase 10 (existing pricing infrastructure to enhance)
**Requirements**: None (refinement of Phase 10 capability)
**Success Criteria** (what must be TRUE):
  1. A program record can be created with just an anniversary date and ticker — every additional field (broker, limits, layers, pricing) adds value but none are required beyond the minimum
  2. Year-over-year tracking: for a given company, the system shows how the D&O program has evolved — which carriers rotated in/out, how pricing moved, whether the broker changed, how limits/retention changed

**Problem**: v1 has ~55% field coverage across the worksheet. Financial data (XBRL) is HIGH quality. Everything else -- governance, litigation, compensation, AI risk -- ranges from LOW to GARBAGE. ### Phase 34: Living Knowledge & Continuous Learning
**Goal**: The knowledge system becomes a living analytical framework that can ingest new information and evolve. When a new article, claim, regulatory action, or industry report arrives, the system can: evaluate whether existing checks cover it, identify knowledge gaps, extract new patterns, propose new checks or threshold adjustments, and validate retroactively against the portfolio. The system gets meaningfully smarter with every company analyzed and every external input ingested.
**Depends on**: Phase 33 (question-driven architecture ensures pipeline answers the right questions before making it learn)
**Requirements**: ARCH-09, ARCH-10, SECT7-06, SECT7-07, SECT7-11
**Success Criteria** (what must be TRUE):
  1. **External document ingestion**: Articles, claims reports, short seller reports, regulatory actions, and industry analyses can be ingested and mapped against the knowledge model. The system parses: what company, what happened, what's the D&O implication
  2. **Knowledge gap identification**: When new information arrives, the system evaluates: "Do our checks cover this scenario? Would our pipeline have caught this?" If not: what check is missing, what data source would we need, what extraction logic, what threshold?
  3. **Pattern extraction**: New information is analyzed for patterns — "This $500M SPAC settlement followed: projections during roadshow → missed post-merger → stock drop → 10b-5 suit." Is this a known pattern? A variant? Something new?
  4. **Check proposal generation**: The system proposes new check definitions (with full lifecycle metadata) based on ingested information. Proposals enter INCUBATING status and require human approval to activate
  5. **Retroactive validation**: New checks can be tested against cached state files from prior runs — "If we had this check 6 months ago, would it have fired for companies that later had claims?"
  6. **Baseline self-calibration**: Industry base rates, severity baselines, and scoring parameters update based on accumulated results with human approval gates. Calibration drift is tracked and auditable
  7. **Underwriter feedback integration**: Annotations ("this check was wrong", "you missed X", "this threshold is too sensitive") feed back into the knowledge model as calibration signals
  8. **Compounding intelligence**: Company #50 gets a meaningfully better analysis than Company #1 — more refined thresholds, more patterns detected, better peer context, fewer false positives
  9. **Pricing model distinction preserved**: The actuarial pricing model (empirical settlement data → what should it cost) and real-world market intelligence (actual quotes/towers → what IS the market charging) remain architecturally separate. The GAP between them is the insight. They inform each other but do not converge into a single model

### Phase 36: Pricing Model Calibration & Market Intelligence
**Goal**: Two architecturally separate pricing capabilities: (1) the actuarial pricing model produces credible loss-based premium indications calibrated against empirical settlement data — "what should this cost based on risk?"; (2) the market intelligence system ingests real-world quotes, towers, and program data — "what IS the market actually charging?" The GAP between actuarial indication and market reality is a first-class analytical output: it tells the underwriter whether the market is pricing risk correctly, where opportunities exist, and where to be cautious.
**Depends on**: Phase 35 (clean display layer shows pricing data correctly)
**Success Criteria** (what must be TRUE):
  1. **Actuarial model calibrated**: Expected loss, severity, and premium indications validated against historical settlement data for at least 10 company archetypes. Model assumptions transparent and auditable
  2. **Market intelligence ingestion**: Real D&O program structures ingested via document pipeline (quota binders, policy docs, tower spreadsheets). At least 5 real programs captured
  3. **Market positioning works**: "For companies with this risk profile, the market prices at $X" produces meaningful answers with confidence based on data volume
  4. **Gap analysis as insight**: Worksheet shows actuarial indication, market reality, and the gap — with interpretation. "Our model says $X, market charges $Y, gap of Z% suggests [opportunity/caution]"
  5. **Architecturally separate**: Actuarial model and market intelligence are separate modules, separate data stores, separate display sections. They inform each other but do NOT converge. The actuarial model never uses market quotes as inputs to its loss model; the market intelligence never uses actuarial outputs to adjust observed prices
  6. **Prospecting capability**: The pricing database supports queries beyond single-company underwriting: "Show me all companies in this sector with renewals in Q2", "Which accounts have premium above model indication?", "Where is the market hardening/softening?" — enabling the system to serve prospecting and portfolio management, not just referral underwriting
  7. **Continuous accumulation**: Every real-world pricing event captured (even partial data — just an anniversary date, just one layer's price) makes the database more valuable. The system is designed to run these databases continuously, accumulating market intelligence over time as a competitive moat. Real-world events (articles, market moves, stack information) feed both the knowledge system (Phase 34) AND the pricing database
