# D&O Underwriting Worksheet: Comprehensive Gaps Analysis

## Executive Summary

After extensive research across D&O underwriting best practices, academic literature, SEC data sources, industry reports (Allianz 2026, Woodruff Sawyer 2026, Cornerstone Research, Aon), and emerging claims trends, this analysis identifies **19 major gaps** and **numerous sub-gaps** in the proposed worksheet structure. The gaps range from entirely missing risk dimensions (M&A activity, capital markets exposure, workforce/employment risk) to missing data points within existing sections, to structural/analytical gaps in how the worksheet synthesizes information.

The findings are organized into three tiers:
1. **Missing Risk Dimensions** -- entire categories of risk that have no home in the current structure
2. **Missing Data Points Within Existing Sections** -- specific signals and analyses that should be added to sections 1-6
3. **Structural & Analytical Gaps** -- missing framework elements for how the worksheet operates

---

## TIER 1: MISSING RISK DIMENSIONS (Need New Sections or Major Subsections)

---

### GAP 1: Capital Markets Activity & Securities Offering Exposure

**What is missing:** A dedicated analysis of the company's capital markets activity, including recent and planned IPOs, secondary/follow-on equity offerings (SPOs), debt issuances (bonds, convertible notes), shelf registrations (S-3), and at-the-market (ATM) programs. This should include a timeline of all offerings, the specific statutory exposure each creates, and an assessment of disclosure quality in offering documents.

**Why it matters for D&O:** Capital markets transactions are the single most direct driver of Section 11 (Securities Act of 1933) liability. Unlike Section 10(b) claims under the Exchange Act (which require scienter), Section 11 is essentially a strict liability statute -- plaintiffs need only show a material misstatement or omission in a registration statement, with no requirement to prove intent or even negligence. This dramatically lowers the litigation bar. Every equity or debt offering creates a new "window" of Section 11 exposure. Cornerstone Research data shows that cases involving IPOs or SPOs settle for significantly higher amounts. Recent IPO companies face the highest D&O claim frequency of any cohort.

**Where the data comes from:**
- SEC EDGAR: S-1, S-3, S-4 (merger), F-1 (foreign), 424B (prospectus supplements) filings
- SEC EDGAR: Registration statements on file (effective shelf registrations)
- Company 10-K: Discussion of capital structure and financing activities
- Underwriting agreements filed as exhibits to registration statements
- EDGAR XBRL data on debt issuances and equity transactions

**Which section it should go in:** This needs its own section or a prominent subsection, possibly within a new "Capital Structure & Transaction Risk" section (see Gap 2 on M&A). Alternatively, a new **Section 7: Capital Markets & Transaction Activity**.

**Importance: CRITICAL**

### GAP 2: M&A Activity as a Standalone Risk Dimension

**What is missing:** A comprehensive M&A risk analysis covering:
- **Historical M&A activity:** Completed acquisitions (size, frequency, integration success/failure, goodwill impairments post-deal), divestitures, joint ventures
- **Pending/announced transactions:** Deal terms, regulatory approval status, shareholder vote requirements, appraisal rights exposure
- **M&A-related litigation history:** Merger objection suits, appraisal actions, "disclosure-only" settlements, buyer's remorse suits
- **Acquisition integration risk:** Track record of hitting synergy targets, write-downs, executive departures post-acquisition
- **Going-private/take-private exposure:** Management buyout conflicts, special committee process quality
- **De-SPAC transaction analysis:** For companies that went public via SPAC, the entire de-SPAC process, projections used, and earnout structures

**Why it matters for D&O:** M&A is one of the highest-frequency triggers for D&O claims. Virtually every public company merger attracts litigation -- at one point, over 90% of deals valued above $100M drew lawsuits. While "merger tax" litigation has shifted somewhat after the Trulia decision in Delaware, deal litigation remains prolific, particularly appraisal actions and disclosure claims. Academic research identifies "vigorous acquisition policy" as a primary predictor of D&O litigation. Goodwill impairments following acquisitions are frequently the basis for securities fraud claims alleging the company overpaid and knew (or should have known) the deal was value-destroying. The Purdue Pharma decision has heightened personal liability exposure for directors in distressed M&A contexts.

**Where the data comes from:**
- SEC EDGAR: S-4 (merger proxy), DEFM14A (merger proxy), SC 13E-3 (going private), SC TO (tender offers), 8-K Item 1.01 (entry into material definitive agreement), 8-K Item 2.01 (completion of acquisition/disposition)
- SEC EDGAR: Goodwill and intangible asset balances and impairment disclosures in 10-K
- Court dockets (PACER) for merger objection suits
- Press releases and 8-K filings on deal announcements
- Bloomberg/Capital IQ transaction databases (commercial)

**Which section it should go in:** New subsection within a **Section 7: Capital Markets & Transaction Activity**, or its own section entirely.

**Importance: CRITICAL**

### GAP 3: Individual Director & Officer Risk Profiles

**What is missing:** A person-by-person risk assessment of each director and named executive officer, covering:
- **Personal litigation history:** Prior securities class actions, derivative suits, regulatory enforcement actions, or criminal proceedings involving each individual at current or prior companies
- **Board interlocks:** Other public company boards each director sits on, and the D&O risk profile of those companies (a director on multiple troubled boards is a contagion risk)
- **Prior company failures:** Whether any director or officer was previously associated with companies that went through bankruptcy, restatements, SEC enforcement, or significant securities litigation
- **Tenure and experience:** How long each D/O has been in role, relevant industry experience, financial literacy (especially for audit committee members)
- **Concentration of new directors:** A sudden influx of new board members can signal governance instability or an attempt to "clean house"
- **"Repeat player" plaintiffs' targets:** Some executives have track records of being individually named in litigation

**Why it matters for D&O:** The D&O questionnaire that insurance applicants must complete asks specifically about each director's and officer's personal litigation history, prior company associations, and conflicts of interest -- because individual risk profiles are material to underwriting. Board interlocks create contagion pathways: a director embroiled in a fraud at Company A may create derivative scrutiny at Companies B and C where they also serve. Academic research on "tainted directors" shows that governance quality and stock price effects follow individuals across boards. The Cornerstone Research settlement data shows that the personal characteristics of defendants (particularly CFOs and CEOs) influence claim outcomes.

**Where the data comes from:**
- SEC EDGAR: DEF 14A (proxy statement) -- director biographies, other board memberships, related party transactions, compensation
- SEC EDGAR: Form 3/4/5 -- individual insider ownership and trading
- Stanford Securities Class Action Clearinghouse -- search by individual defendant name
- PACER / court dockets -- personal litigation history
- SEC AAER database -- prior enforcement actions against individuals
- LinkedIn / public biographical sources
- ISS / Glass Lewis director profiles (commercial)

**Which section it should go in:** This should be embedded within **Section 4 (Governance & Leadership)** as a major new subsection: "Individual D&O Risk Profiles."

**Importance: CRITICAL**

### GAP 4: Workforce, Employment Practices & Human Capital Risk

**What is missing:** Analysis of employment-related D&O exposure:
- **EEOC complaints and charges:** Volume, trends, types of discrimination alleged
- **Wage and hour class actions:** Pending or historical (these are among the highest-severity employment claims)
- **Workforce reduction (RIF) risk:** Layoff history, WARN Act compliance, severance practices
- **Whistleblower and qui tam activity:** SEC whistleblower tips filed against the company, False Claims Act qui tam actions, internal whistleblower retaliation claims
- **Human capital disclosures:** New SEC-mandated human capital disclosures in 10-K (Item 1), including turnover rates, diversity statistics, safety metrics
- **Labor relations:** Union presence, collective bargaining agreement expirations, strike history
- **#MeToo / workplace culture risk:** History of sexual harassment claims against executives, culture-related investigations
- **Contractor vs. employee classification risk:** Gig economy exposure, misclassification litigation

**Why it matters for D&O:** Employment practices claims are the #1 source of D&O claims for private companies and a growing exposure for public companies. The EEOC received 88,531 discrimination charges in FY2024 (up 9.4% YoY). Wage and hour class actions can generate hundreds of millions in exposure. Whistleblower activity is a leading indicator of deeper problems -- the SEC whistleblower program has generated over $1.5 billion in awards, and tips often precede enforcement actions. The SEC's human capital disclosure requirements (adopted 2020) mean that misstatements about workforce metrics now create securities fraud exposure. Layoffs, particularly poorly managed ones, generate both WARN Act liability and derivative suits alleging fiduciary breach. Recent EEOC enforcement and the expansion of EPL-related D&O claims make this a material gap.

**Where the data comes from:**
- SEC EDGAR 10-K: Item 1 Human Capital disclosures (mandated since 2020)
- EEOC public records and litigation database
- OSHA enforcement records (for workplace safety)
- WARN Act notices (state-level databases)
- Court dockets (PACER) for employment class actions
- SEC whistleblower program statistics (aggregate; individual tips are confidential)
- Glassdoor and similar platforms for employee sentiment (supplementary)
- Company proxy statement (DEF 14A) for workforce diversity disclosures

**Which section it should go in:** New subsection, potentially within an expanded **Section 5 (Litigation & Regulatory Exposure)** or as a standalone section given its breadth.

**Importance: CRITICAL**

### GAP 5: International & Jurisdictional Exposure

**What is missing:** A comprehensive geographic risk assessment:
- **Operational footprint:** Countries where the company has material operations, subsidiaries, joint ventures
- **High-risk jurisdiction exposure:** Operations in countries with elevated corruption, sanctions, or regulatory risk (OFAC-sanctioned countries, FCPA high-risk zones, countries with emerging securities litigation regimes)
- **Cross-border regulatory exposure:** EU GDPR, UK Bribery Act, EU corporate sustainability due diligence, foreign antitrust regimes, emerging non-US class action mechanisms
- **Non-US securities litigation exposure:** Companies listed on non-US exchanges (or with ADR programs) face securities litigation risk in multiple jurisdictions simultaneously (UK, Netherlands, Australia, Germany all have expanding collective action mechanisms)
- **Sanctions compliance posture:** OFAC compliance program quality, screening procedures, prior sanctions-related incidents
- **Transfer pricing and international tax risk:** Exposure to international tax disputes, BEPS (Base Erosion and Profit Shifting), Pillar Two global minimum tax compliance
- **Conflict minerals (Form SD) and supply chain due diligence:** Required filings under Dodd-Frank Section 1502

**Why it matters for D&O:** International exposure is a growing and frequently underestimated D&O risk dimension. There has been a marked increase in shareholder claims being pursued across multiple jurisdictions simultaneously. Non-US issuers with ADRs trading in the US remain targets of US securities class actions. FCPA enforcement is identified as a "hot topic" in the Woodruff Sawyer 2026 D&O guide. OFAC violations can result in fines (e.g., PURE insurance's $466,200 fine in 2023) and create derivative suit exposure against directors who failed to establish adequate compliance programs (Caremark liability). The EU is rapidly expanding its litigation mechanisms, and the EU Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive creates personal director liability for supply chain failures. A common D&O coverage misconception is that a policy marked "worldwide" will respond to claims in all jurisdictions -- it often will not.

**Where the data comes from:**
- SEC EDGAR 10-K: Item 1 (geographic segments), Item 1A (risk factors mentioning specific countries), Notes to Financial Statements (segment reporting, geographic revenue breakdown)
- SEC EDGAR Form SD (conflict minerals annual filing)
- OFAC SDN List and enforcement actions database
- DOJ FCPA enforcement action database
- EU enforcement databases (varies by jurisdiction)
- Company sustainability reports and ESG disclosures
- State Department travel advisories and Transparency International CPI scores

**Which section it should go in:** New subsection within **Section 1 (Company Profile & Industry Context)** or a standalone section. Given the breadth of the analysis, a sub-framework within Section 1 covering "Geographic Risk Profile" is recommended, with forward-looking jurisdictional risk woven into Section 6.

**Importance: CRITICAL**

### GAP 6: Environmental, Social & Sustainability Risk (Beyond Governance)

**What is missing:** The current structure covers the "G" of ESG in Section 4 (Governance) but is largely silent on the "E" and "S":
- **Environmental liability exposure:** Pending environmental litigation, Superfund site involvement, EPA enforcement actions, state-level environmental claims, PFAS exposure, carbon emissions disclosure accuracy
- **Climate disclosure risk:** Compliance with SEC climate disclosure rules (phased in for large accelerated filers starting FY2025), state-level climate disclosure mandates (California SB 253/SB 261), EU CSRD
- **Greenwashing risk:** Discrepancies between ESG commitments/marketing and actual performance, vulnerability to "greenwashing" securities claims
- **Social / community impact:** Community opposition to operations, NIMBY litigation, indigenous rights claims
- **Product safety and mass tort exposure:** Pending or potential mass tort litigation (opioids, PFAS, talc, etc.) that could trigger D&O claims when stock drops on adverse verdicts or settlements
- **ESG ratings divergence:** Material gaps between company ESG self-reporting and third-party ESG ratings

**Why it matters for D&O:** Environmental and social risks have emerged as major D&O claim drivers. Post-Purdue Pharma, mass tort liability creates personal D&O exposure that can no longer be easily discharged in bankruptcy. WTW research shows the risk of a securities class action increases from 5% to 68% following a substantial environmental or safety incident. The SEC's climate disclosure rules create new securities fraud exposure for climate-related misstatements. "Greenwashing" claims are a growth area in both regulatory enforcement and private litigation. D&O policies may contain pollution exclusions that could limit coverage for climate-related claims. The Allianz 2026 report identifies ESG as a key claims driver. Conversely, companies with strong ESG programs generally obtain more favorable D&O insurance terms and pricing, making this assessment relevant to both risk identification and underwriting positioning.

**Where the data comes from:**
- SEC EDGAR 10-K: Item 1A risk factors (environmental disclosures), Notes to Financial Statements (environmental contingencies and reserves)
- EPA Enforcement and Compliance History Online (ECHO) database
- EPA Superfund site database (CERCLIS)
- State environmental agency enforcement records
- Company sustainability/ESG reports (voluntary but increasingly standard)
- Third-party ESG ratings (MSCI, Sustainalytics, ISS ESG -- commercial)
- CDP (formerly Carbon Disclosure Project) filings
- SEC climate disclosure filings (beginning 2026 for FY2025)
- California Air Resources Board reporting (SB 253)

**Which section it should go in:** New subsection or standalone section. Could be integrated as part of an expanded "ESG Risk Profile" that includes the governance elements currently in Section 4 plus new E&S coverage.

**Importance: CRITICAL**

### GAP 7: The Company's Own D&O Insurance Program Structure

**What is missing:** Analysis of the target company's existing D&O insurance program:
- **Tower structure:** Primary carrier, excess layers, total limits of liability purchased
- **Side A / Side B / Side C coverage:** Allocation across insuring agreements
- **Side A DIC (Difference in Conditions) coverage:** Whether dedicated Side A DIC exists
- **Retention/deductible levels:** Self-insured retention amounts for Side B and C
- **Policy terms and conditions:** Key exclusions, definition of "claim," definition of "insured," coverage territory, prior acts date
- **Claims history under the D&O program:** Frequency and severity of prior claims, reserves, payments
- **Adequacy assessment:** Whether current limits are adequate relative to market cap, peer benchmarks, and risk profile
- **Renewal timing and market dynamics:** When the program renews, market conditions

**Why it matters for D&O:** For an underwriter evaluating whether to participate in a D&O insurance tower, understanding the existing program structure is fundamental. The position in the tower (primary vs. excess), the quality of co-insurers, and the adequacy of total limits all affect risk. A company that buys inadequate D&O limits relative to its market cap may signal a governance or risk management deficiency. Conversely, a company that has dramatically increased its limits may be anticipating trouble. Prior claims frequency and severity under the D&O program is the single most direct measure of the insured risk's loss history. Side A DIC coverage is critical for individual director/officer protection -- its absence may deter qualified independent directors, itself a governance red flag. The Kevin LaCroix (D&O Diary) analysis emphasizes that program structure concerns are among the most important considerations for D&O insurance buyers and underwriters alike.

**Where the data comes from:**
- Company's D&O insurance application and renewal submission
- Broker-provided market summaries and placement slips
- 10-K disclosures (some companies disclose D&O insurance limits and retentions)
- DEF 14A proxy statement (indemnification and insurance disclosures)
- Prior loss runs from the incumbent carrier(s)
- Industry benchmarking data (Advisen, Aon, Marsh, WTW -- commercial)

**Which section it should go in:** This is underwriting-specific context that doesn't fit neatly into a risk assessment section. It should be a **standalone section** -- perhaps **Section 0 or Section 8: Insurance Program Structure & History** -- that frames the underwriting decision itself.

**Importance: CRITICAL**

### GAP 8: Corporate Structure Complexity (Section 1)

**What is missing:**
- **Subsidiary count and structure:** Total number of subsidiaries (reported in Exhibit 21 to the 10-K), jurisdictions of incorporation, VIE (variable interest entity) structures
- **Off-balance-sheet entities and SPV usage:** Special purpose vehicles, securitization structures, unconsolidated entities
- **Dual-class share structure:** Whether the company has multiple share classes with differential voting rights (supervoting shares)
- **Controlled company status:** Whether a single shareholder, family, or founder group holds majority voting control
- **Holding company vs. operating company structure:** Whether the public entity is a holding company with operating subsidiaries

**Why it matters for D&O:** Complex corporate structures increase the opacity of financial reporting and the potential for related-party transactions, tunneling, and disclosure failures. Dual-class structures are a red flag for governance quality -- proxy advisory firms (ISS, Glass Lewis) flag these as high-risk. Controlled companies are exempt from certain exchange listing requirements (e.g., majority independent board), creating governance gaps. VIE structures, common in Chinese ADR issuers, create unique disclosure risks. Exhibit 21 subsidiary lists are available on EDGAR and can reveal previously unknown jurisdictional exposure.

**Data source:** SEC EDGAR Exhibit 21 (subsidiaries), 10-K Notes (VIEs), DEF 14A (voting structure), Articles of Incorporation

**Section:** Section 1 (Company Profile & Industry Context)

**Importance: IMPORTANT**

### GAP 9: Anti-Takeover Provisions & Shareholder Rights (Section 4)

**What is missing:**
- **Anti-takeover provisions inventory:** Classified/staggered board, poison pill (shareholder rights plan), supermajority voting requirements, blank-check preferred stock, limitations on calling special meetings or acting by written consent
- **Forum selection provisions:** Exclusive forum bylaws (Delaware Chancery Court for fiduciary claims, federal court for Securities Act claims), federal forum provisions
- **State of incorporation analysis depth:** The current worksheet mentions "state of incorporation implications" but needs much deeper analysis of Delaware SB 21 (2025 amendments limiting controller transaction liability), Nevada's weaker fiduciary duty regime, potential reincorporation trends
- **Shareholder rights plan (poison pill) status:** Whether one is currently in effect, its terms, and whether it was board-adopted or shareholder-approved

**Why it matters for D&O:** Anti-takeover provisions directly affect D&O litigation risk. Classified boards reduce the risk of proxy fights but may entrench management (increasing Caremark/oversight liability risk). Forum selection clauses are a critical litigation cost control mechanism -- federal forum provisions can eliminate state-court Section 11 claims. Delaware's SB 21 amendments (effective August 2025) fundamentally changed the liability landscape for controlled transactions, potentially reducing D&O risk for Delaware-incorporated controlled companies. The D&O Diary notes that poison pills generate defense-cost exposure by attracting shareholder scrutiny of director loyalty.

**Data source:** DEF 14A proxy statement, Articles/Certificate of Incorporation, Bylaws (all filed on EDGAR), ISS governance scores, SharkRepellent database (commercial)

**Section:** Section 4 (Governance & Leadership)

**Importance: IMPORTANT**

### GAP 10: Shareholder Activism & Proxy Fight Exposure (Section 4 or 5)

**What is missing:**
- **Active activist campaigns:** Whether any activist investor currently holds a position and has filed a 13D or engaged the company
- **Proxy fight history:** Prior proxy contests, board seats won by dissidents, settlement agreements
- **Vulnerability scoring:** Characteristics that make the company an attractive activist target (underperformance vs. peers, excess cash, conglomerate discount, governance deficiencies)
- **Activist investor surveillance:** 13D/13G filings showing accumulation by known activists (schedule 13D is required when someone acquires >5% with an "activist intent")
- **Say-on-pay and shareholder proposal results:** Failed say-on-pay votes, high-support shareholder proposals on governance reforms

**Why it matters for D&O:** U.S. shareholder activism was up 6% YoY in 2024 to 115 campaigns. A record 27 CEOs resigned from activist-targeted companies in 2024. Activist campaigns generate substantial D&O defense costs, board disruption, and often trigger follow-on derivative litigation. Many D&O insurers now specifically include activism as a covered peril. Companies targeted by activists face elevated securities litigation risk because the activist's public campaign often highlights alleged governance failures or mismanagement that plaintiffs' firms then use as the basis for securities claims. 13D filings are freely available on EDGAR and provide early warning.

**Data source:** SEC EDGAR Schedule 13D/13G filings, DEF 14A (shareholder proposals, voting results), activist campaign databases (13D Monitor, Insightia -- commercial), ISS voting analytics

**Section:** Section 4 (Governance & Leadership) -- new subsection on "Shareholder Activism & Engagement"

**Importance: IMPORTANT**

### GAP 11: SEC Comment Letters & Staff Review Activity (Section 2 or 5)

**What is missing:**
- **SEC comment letter history:** Whether the company has received SEC Division of Corporation Finance comment letters, the subject matter, frequency, and resolution status
- **Trends in comment letter topics:** Whether comments focus on revenue recognition, segment reporting, non-GAAP measures, MD&A, risk factor disclosures, or other areas
- **Filing amendments prompted by SEC review:** Whether the company has had to amend filings (10-K/A, 10-Q/A) in response to SEC comments
- **SEC review cycle timing:** When the company was last subject to a comprehensive review (the Sarbanes-Oxley Act requires SEC review of every company at least once every three years)

**Why it matters for D&O:** SEC comment letters are a publicly available, underutilized risk signal. Nearly half of all SEC comments involve accounting and disclosure issues, and more than 17% of cases result in amended filings. Academic research shows that SEC comment letters are associated with subsequent restatements and enforcement actions. A company receiving repeated or escalating SEC comments on the same topic (e.g., revenue recognition) is at elevated risk of a restatement, which is one of the most powerful predictors of securities class action filing. Comment letters and responses are publicly posted on EDGAR with a delay.

**Data source:** SEC EDGAR CORRESP filings (comment letter correspondence), SEC EDGAR full-text search, Audit Analytics comment letter database (commercial)

**Section:** Section 2 (Financial Health) or Section 5 (Litigation & Regulatory Exposure)

**Importance: IMPORTANT**

### GAP 12: Tax Aggressiveness & Tax Litigation Risk (Section 2)

**What is missing:**
- **Effective tax rate analysis:** Cash effective tax rate vs. statutory rate, vs. industry peers
- **Unrecognized tax benefits (UTB):** Size and trend of UTB balances (disclosed in 10-K tax footnote), UTB-related settlements
- **Tax shelter participation:** Disclosure of aggressive tax strategies (income shifting, transfer pricing structures, inversions)
- **Tax-related litigation:** IRS disputes, Tax Court proceedings, state tax litigation
- **International tax structure complexity:** Use of tax haven jurisdictions, intellectual property holding structures

**Why it matters for D&O:** Academic research (Donelson, Glenn & Yust, published in Review of Accounting Studies) directly demonstrates that D&O insurers view tax aggressiveness as increasing litigation risk, and D&O premiums increase as unrecognized tax benefits increase. Tax aggressiveness is also correlated with accounting fraud (Lennox et al., Journal of Accounting Research). Tax-related derivative suits and securities class actions are a meaningful source of D&O claims. Furthermore, high UTB balances may signal broader management willingness to push accounting and disclosure boundaries, making tax aggressiveness a useful proxy for overall reporting risk.

**Data source:** SEC EDGAR 10-K: Income tax footnote (UTBs, rate reconciliation, deferred tax detail), MD&A discussion of tax matters, Item 1A risk factors (tax-related risks), company tax policy disclosures

**Section:** Section 2 (Financial Health) -- new subsection on "Tax Risk Profile"

**Importance: IMPORTANT**

### GAP 13: Cybersecurity Governance & Data Breach History (Sections 4 and 5)

**What is missing:**
- **SEC cybersecurity disclosures:** Item 1C of Form 10-K (mandated by SEC's July 2023 cybersecurity rules) -- risk management, strategy, and governance related to cybersecurity, including board oversight
- **Material cybersecurity incidents:** Item 1.05 of Form 8-K -- required disclosure of material cybersecurity incidents within four business days
- **Data breach history:** Prior breaches, their scale, regulatory fines, litigation generated
- **Cybersecurity governance depth:** Whether the board has a designated cyber-expert director, frequency of cyber briefings, CISO reporting structure
- **Third-party cybersecurity ratings:** Bitsight, SecurityScorecard or similar ratings as an external signal of cyber hygiene
- **Cyber insurance program:** Whether the company maintains standalone cyber insurance (and its adequacy), which can affect the severity of D&O claims arising from cyber incidents

**Why it matters for D&O:** WTW research shows a dramatic increase in securities class action risk from 5% to 68% following a substantial cyber incident. The SEC's 2023 cybersecurity disclosure rules (effective December 2023 for large accelerated filers) created new, mandatory disclosure obligations that -- if violated -- create straightforward securities fraud claims. Board-level cyber governance is now part of D&O underwriting, with carriers looking beyond IT controls to scrutinize governance frameworks. The Allianz 2026 report identifies cyber liability as one of the sharpest rising D&O risk areas. Moody's (in partnership with Bitsight) now offers D&O underwriters cyber risk assessment tools. A cyber incident that leads to both direct costs AND a stock drop creates overlapping D&O and cyber insurance claims, raising coverage coordination issues.

**Data source:** SEC EDGAR 10-K Item 1C (cybersecurity disclosures), 8-K Item 1.05 (incident disclosures), HHS breach notification database (healthcare), state attorney general breach notification databases, Bitsight/SecurityScorecard (commercial), news monitoring

**Section:** Cybersecurity governance in **Section 4**; breach history and cyber litigation in **Section 5**; forward-looking cyber risk in **Section 6**

**Importance: CRITICAL**

### GAP 14: Customer & Supplier Concentration Risk (Section 1 or 2)

**What is missing:**
- **Customer concentration:** Revenue dependence on major customers (any customer representing >10% of revenue must be disclosed per ASC 280)
- **Supplier concentration:** Dependence on single-source suppliers for critical inputs
- **Contract renewal risk:** Timing of major customer/supplier contract renewals or expirations
- **Supply chain disruption exposure:** Vulnerability to geopolitical, climate, or pandemic-driven supply chain disruptions
- **Channel partner risk:** Dependence on key distribution partners or platforms

**Why it matters for D&O:** Academic research demonstrates that customer concentration is associated with stock price crash risk and corporate risk-taking. Companies with concentrated customer bases are incentivized to withhold bad news, delay negative disclosures, and manage earnings upward to maintain key relationships -- all behaviors that increase securities fraud risk. Loss of a major customer can trigger precipitous stock declines and securities litigation. Supply chain disruptions (as seen during COVID and with geopolitical tensions) create disclosure risk when companies fail to timely disclose the impact. The loss of a 10%+ customer that was known but not timely disclosed is a textbook Section 10(b) fact pattern.

**Data source:** SEC EDGAR 10-K: Segment reporting footnote (major customer disclosures), Item 1A risk factors (supply chain / customer concentration), MD&A (discussion of key customers), 8-K filings (material contract terminations)

**Section:** Section 1 (Company Profile & Industry Context) or Section 2 (Financial Health)

**Importance: IMPORTANT**

### GAP 15: Intellectual Property, Antitrust & Product Liability Dimensions (Section 5)

**What is missing:**
- **Significant IP litigation:** Patent infringement suits (as plaintiff or defendant), trade secret misappropriation claims, Markman hearing outcomes
- **Antitrust exposure:** FTC or DOJ antitrust investigations, private antitrust class actions, market dominance creating regulatory risk
- **Product liability / mass tort exposure:** Pending or potential mass tort claims (PFAS, opioids, talc, etc.) that could trigger secondary D&O claims upon stock drops
- **Regulatory product approvals at risk:** FDA, FAA, or other agency approvals that, if revoked or denied, would trigger disclosure-based securities claims

**Why it matters for D&O:** Event-driven securities litigation -- where an operational event (product recall, adverse verdict, regulatory denial) causes a stock drop and triggers a follow-on securities claim -- has been a growth area. Plaintiffs allege that directors knew of the underlying risk (e.g., product defect, antitrust exposure) and failed to disclose it adequately. The Allianz 2026 report identifies "event-driven litigation" as a key claims category. While D&O policies typically exclude direct IP claims against the entity, the securities fraud derivative claims arising from these events are fully covered. Antitrust investigations by the DOJ or FTC often lead to securities class actions when disclosed.

**Data source:** SEC EDGAR 10-K Item 3 (Legal Proceedings), Item 1A (risk factors), Notes to Financial Statements (contingent liabilities), PACER court dockets, FDA/FTC/DOJ enforcement databases, ITC investigations database

**Section:** Section 5 (Litigation & Regulatory Exposure) -- expanded to include these dimensions explicitly

**Importance: IMPORTANT**

### GAP 16: Political Spending, Lobbying & Reputational Risk (Section 4 or 6)

**What is missing:**
- **Corporate political spending:** PAC contributions, lobbying expenditures, trade association memberships funding political activity
- **Lobbying disclosure:** Federal and state lobbying registration and expenditure reports
- **Misalignment risk:** Whether political spending aligns with stated corporate values and ESG commitments (misalignment creates "greenwashing"-style reputational claims)
- **Board oversight of political spending:** Whether the board reviews and approves political spending policies
- **Shareholder proposals on political spending:** History and support levels for shareholder proposals demanding political spending transparency

**Why it matters for D&O:** Harvard Law School Forum research warns that companies face "significantly greater risks than in the past" from political spending, particularly when contributions fund groups whose actions conflict with public commitments. Misaligned political spending creates reputational harm, stakeholder backlash, and derivative suit exposure alleging director breach of fiduciary duty. Shareholder proposals on political spending disclosure consistently receive significant support (some receiving majority votes). In a polarized political environment, corporate political spending creates asymmetric downside risk. Companies that contribute to candidates whose views contradict company values face brand harm that can translate to stock price impact and securities claims.

**Data source:** FEC (Federal Election Commission) database, Senate/House lobbying disclosure databases, OpenSecrets.org, company proxy statements (shareholder proposal voting results), company voluntary political spending disclosures

**Section:** Section 4 (Governance & Leadership) or Section 6 (Forward-Looking Risk)

**Importance: NICE TO HAVE (but increasingly important)**

### GAP 17: Bankruptcy / Insolvency Proximity Analysis (Section 2)

**What is missing:**
- **Zone of insolvency analysis:** Beyond Altman Z-Score (which is included), a more nuanced analysis of whether the company is approaching the "zone of insolvency" where fiduciary duties shift
- **Debt maturity wall:** Near-term debt maturities that could trigger liquidity crises
- **Covenant compliance:** Proximity to financial covenant violations in credit agreements
- **Going concern language tracking:** Whether auditors have included going concern language (or considered and rejected it)
- **Creditor composition:** Who are the major creditors, and are they sophisticated enough to pursue D&O claims in bankruptcy
- **Deepening insolvency risk:** Whether continued operations are deepening creditor losses (creating personal D&O liability)

**Why it matters for D&O:** Global business insolvencies are expected to rise 6% in 2025 and 5% in 2026, reaching record highs 24% above pre-pandemic levels (Allianz 2026). Insolvency is the single most powerful trigger for D&O claims -- bankruptcy trustees and creditor committees routinely pursue breach of fiduciary duty claims against former directors and officers. The "zone of insolvency" doctrine (though narrowed in Delaware after North American Catholic Educational Programming Foundation v. Gheewalla) creates a legal gray zone where directors must begin considering creditor interests. The Allianz report notes that D&O underwriters are applying "more underwriting discipline around companies' financials and how they're servicing their debt, prioritizing detailed financial data, including debt maturities, covenant compliance and relationships with lenders." The current worksheet includes Z-Score and M-Score but doesn't capture the full bankruptcy proximity picture.

**Data source:** SEC EDGAR 10-K: Debt maturity schedule (in notes), going concern language (auditor's report), covenant terms (credit agreement filed as exhibit), MD&A (liquidity discussion), 8-K Item 1.03 (bankruptcy filing), 8-K Item 2.04 (triggering events from material impairment)

**Section:** Section 2 (Financial Health) -- enhanced subsection on "Insolvency Proximity & Creditor Exposure"

**Importance: CRITICAL**

### GAP 18: Accounting Policy & Disclosure Quality Deep Dive (Section 2)

**What is missing (beyond what is in the current financial section):**
- **Revenue recognition policy analysis:** Specific revenue recognition methods used, ASC 606 implementation, areas of judgment in revenue timing
- **Non-GAAP metrics usage:** Which non-GAAP measures the company emphasizes, how large the GAAP-to-non-GAAP adjustments are, SEC scrutiny of non-GAAP practices
- **Critical accounting estimates:** Identification of the most judgment-heavy estimates (goodwill impairment testing, loan loss reserves, insurance reserves, warranty reserves, long-lived asset impairment, fair value Level 3 inputs) and assessment of aggressiveness
- **Segment reporting granularity:** Whether the company's segment reporting is sufficiently granular or whether it may be aggregating segments to obscure underperformance (a frequent SEC comment letter topic)
- **Related party transaction depth:** Beyond listing them, assessing their economic substance, arms-length pricing, and disclosure adequacy (the SEC has brought enforcement actions against Walt Disney, Eagle Bancorp, and others for RPT disclosure failures)
- **MD&A quality assessment:** Whether the MD&A provides genuine analytical insight or is boilerplate
- **Risk factor disclosure changes:** Year-over-year textual changes in Item 1A risk factors (additions, removals, modifications -- changes often signal emerging risks)

**Why it matters for D&O:** Revenue recognition is identified by D&O underwriters as "the most important issue" in accounting analysis. Non-GAAP metric abuse is a recurring SEC enforcement focus and a common basis for securities fraud claims. Critical accounting estimates provide the most direct window into management's willingness to push boundaries. Risk factor changes are an underutilized leading indicator -- NLP research shows that changes in risk factor language correlate with future adverse events. SEC comment letters disproportionately focus on these areas. The Beneish M-Score (included in the current structure) is a backward-looking fraud detection tool; the items listed here provide a forward-looking complement.

**Data source:** SEC EDGAR 10-K: Revenue recognition policy (Note 2 typically), Critical Accounting Estimates section of MD&A, Non-GAAP reconciliations (earnings releases / 8-K), Item 1A risk factors (text analysis), related party transaction disclosures (Note and proxy), XBRL-tagged data for quantitative comparisons

**Section:** Section 2 (Financial Health) -- enhanced substantially

**Importance: CRITICAL**

### GAP 19: Settlement / Loss Severity Modeling Framework

**What is missing:** The current structure assesses whether a claim is likely but does not provide a framework for estimating potential claim severity (loss amount):
- **Plaintiff-style damages estimation:** Cornerstone Research identifies this as "the single most important factor in explaining individual settlement amounts" -- calculated as the stock drop during the class period multiplied by shares outstanding
- **Disclosed Dollar Loss (DDL) tracking:** Average DDL in 2024 reached $438 million, nearly double the historical norm
- **Settlement benchmarking:** Median settlement as percentage of DDL varies dramatically by case type and size (7.3% overall in 2024, but 28.2% for cases with DDL under $25 million)
- **Institutional investor involvement:** Whether institutional investors are likely lead plaintiffs (correlates with larger settlements)
- **Ability to fund a settlement:** The company's financial capacity to fund a settlement (distressed companies settle for less in absolute terms but may exhaust D&O policy limits)
- **Defense cost modeling:** Estimated defense costs by claim type
- **Loss scenario analysis:** Best case / expected case / worst case loss modeling

**Why it matters for D&O:** An underwriter needs to assess not just the probability of a claim but the potential severity. The inherent risk baseline in Section 1 begins this analysis, but the worksheet should provide a framework for translating risk factors into estimated loss exposure. This is what ultimately determines whether the premium is adequate for the risk. Cornerstone Research provides extensive empirical data on settlement drivers that can be used to build severity models.

**Data source:** Cornerstone Research annual settlement reports, Stanford SCA database, Advisen loss database (commercial), company-specific DDL calculations based on market cap and stock volatility

**Section:** This should be a **synthesis output** that integrates findings from all sections, potentially as part of an enhanced Section 6 or as a standalone "Underwriting Conclusion" section.

**Importance: CRITICAL**

## STRUCTURAL RECOMMENDATIONS

Beyond missing content, these structural improvements would enhance the worksheet:

### 1. Forward-Looking Integration
The user's stated intent is to weave forward-looking intelligence INTO each section rather than siloing it in Section 6. To operationalize this, each section should have a dedicated "Forward-Looking Signals" subsection at the end, with Section 6 serving as a true synthesis / overall risk outlook rather than a repository for everything forward-looking.

### 2. Peer Benchmarking Framework
The current structure references peer comparison in sections 2 and 3 but doesn't establish how peers are selected. A formal peer selection methodology (by SIC/NAICS, market cap band, exchange, geographic footprint) should be established in Section 1 and applied consistently across all sections.

### 3. Risk Scoring / Weighting System
The worksheet should produce a quantitative risk score, not just a qualitative narrative. Each section should contribute a sub-score, with weights calibrated to the empirical evidence on what actually predicts claims (e.g., capital markets activity and financial distress are more predictive than governance scores).

### 4. Temporal Dimension
The worksheet is largely a point-in-time snapshot. It should explicitly track trends over 3-5 years for key metrics, as deteriorating trends are more predictive than absolute levels.

### 5. Interconnection Mapping
Many risks are interconnected (e.g., aggressive accounting + insider selling + auditor change + SEC comment letter = very high restatement risk). The worksheet should include a "risk convergence" analysis that identifies when multiple moderate signals co-occur to create a high-severity exposure.

### 6. Data Freshness Indicator
For each data point, the worksheet should note when it was last updated and flag stale data. SEC filings are periodic; news and market data are continuous. The worksheet should distinguish between "as of last filing" and "as of today" data.

### 7. Confidence Level Indicators
Not all data points are equally reliable. Public filings are audited; news sentiment is noisy. Each data point should carry a confidence indicator.

## REVISED SECTION STRUCTURE (Recommended)

Based on this analysis, the recommended expanded structure would be:

1. **Company Profile, Industry Context & Geographic Exposure** (expanded)
   - Includes corporate structure complexity, customer/supplier concentration, geographic risk
   - Inherent risk baseline remains here

2. **Financial Health & Accounting Quality** (expanded)
   - Includes tax aggressiveness, accounting policy deep dive, insolvency proximity, credit ratings
   - SEC comment letter history

3. **Market & Trading Signals** (largely unchanged)
   - Includes institutional ownership, analyst coverage, 10b5-1 plans

4. **Governance, Leadership & Individual D&O Risk Profiles** (significantly expanded)
   - Includes individual D&O risk profiles, anti-takeover provisions, shareholder activism, political spending, cybersecurity governance, ESG governance
   - Enhanced executive compensation analysis

5. **Litigation, Regulatory & Operational Exposure** (significantly expanded)
   - Includes securities litigation, regulatory enforcement, employment/workforce risk, environmental/product liability, IP/antitrust, whistleblower activity
   - International regulatory exposure

6. **Environmental, Social & Sustainability Risk** (new or expanded from existing ESG elements)
   - Climate disclosure compliance, greenwashing risk, mass tort exposure, community impact

7. **Capital Markets & Transaction Activity** (new)
   - Securities offerings and Section 11 exposure, M&A activity, SPAC/de-SPAC analysis

8. **Insurance Program Structure & History** (new)
   - Existing D&O tower, Side A/B/C, claims history, adequacy assessment

9. **Risk Synthesis & Underwriting Conclusion** (replaces/expands Section 6)
   - Forward-looking risk synthesis (not risk repository)
   - Settlement/loss severity modeling
   - Risk convergence analysis
   - Composite risk score
   - Underwriting recommendation
