## PART 3: Cross-Underwriter Pattern Analysis

### What ALL Four Underwriters Include (Universal Elements)
1. **Deal economics**: Layer, attachment, limit, premium, and at minimum a partial tower view
2. **Company description**: At least a basic overview of what the company does
3. **Stock data**: Price, market cap, at least one chart
4. **Financial tables**: Balance sheet, income statement, cash flow (standardized format across all four)
5. **Some litigation awareness**: Whether via SCAC search, claims summary, or discussion of known issues
6. **A recommendation or opinion**: Even if informal ("I am inclined to say yes" or "ok to support")
7. **Broker identification**: Who they are working with and at which firm

### What MOST (3 of 4) Include
- Full tower with all carriers, limits, and premiums (all except Angelo)

- Tariff/macro impact analysis (Tiago and Michelle)

- Filing citations with exact quotes (Angelo only)

- FAC/reinsurance discussion (Michelle only)

7. **Alternative signals** -- No underwriter references Glassdoor ratings, LinkedIn headcount changes, job posting patterns, customer review trends, or social media sentiment
8. **SEC comment letter review** -- Not mentioned in any example
9. **Whistleblower / internal investigation checks** -- Not mentioned in any example
10. **Industry/competitor litigation benchmarking** -- No underwriter compares the company's litigation profile against sector peers
11. **Short seller report analysis** -- Not mentioned despite short interest data being reported
12. **Structured forward-looking trigger analysis** -- While underwriters discuss current headwinds, none systematically catalog upcoming events that could trigger stock drops and litigation (e.g., upcoming earnings dates, FDA decisions, contract expirations, executive lockup expirations)

## PART 5: Critical Findings -- What the Proposed Structure is MISSING

The proposed six-section structure fails to account for several elements that are CENTRAL to real underwriter workflows:

### 1. DEAL ECONOMICS & TOWER CONTEXT (Not in proposed structure)
Every real underwriter devotes significant space to the deal itself: the specific layer being quoted, the premium, the attachment point, the full tower with all carriers, expiring vs. proposed terms, rate changes, ILFs, STM calculations, and how Liberty's position fits within the broader program. This is arguably the MOST IMPORTANT section of a real referral -- it is why the underwriter is writing the memo in the first place. The proposed six-section structure has no home for this.

**Recommendation**: Add "Section 0: Deal Summary & Tower Context" or integrate into a preamble. This must include:
- Proposed layer (limit, attachment, retention)
- Premium and rate (expiring vs proposed, % change)
- Full tower lineup with all carriers
- STM calculation
- Why the opportunity exists (market dislocation, carrier exit, new business)

### 2. LIBERTY RELATIONSHIP & ART DATA (Not in proposed structure)
Every underwriter checks the Liberty Mutual ART system for existing relationships across all lines. Tiago spoke to Lynn Richardson about the casualty relationship. Michelle noted $503k total relationship. Jackson showed the ART account search results. This internal data is critical for the business decision but has no place in the proposed structure.

### 3. BROKER INTELLIGENCE & MARKET DYNAMICS (Not in proposed structure)
Real underwriters embed significant broker-sourced intelligence: why the opportunity exists, what other carriers are doing, market positioning, broker expectations on pricing, and negotiation dynamics. Tiago's detail on carrier movements (Chubb dropping to primary, SwissRe exiting entirely, Axis limiting to $10M) is essential context. Jackson's multi-email negotiation over prior acts language vs. specific matter exclusions shows how deals actually evolve. None of this fits the proposed structure.

### 4. POLICY WORDING & COVERAGE STRUCTURE (Not in proposed structure)
Tiago includes specific reinstatement wording. Jackson negotiates prior acts exclusion vs. specific matter exclusion language. Michelle discusses DIC provisions and FAC support. Coverage structure decisions are a core underwriting function with no home in the proposed six sections.

### 5. CLAIMS / LOSS RUN DETAIL (Underrepresented in proposed structure)
Section 5 of the proposed structure covers "Litigation & Regulatory Exposure" but does not specifically call out claims on the Liberty policy, loss run review, or claims adjuster input. Michelle's inclusion of Brian McLoughlin's assessment and the specific claim number (NEWSPC000634869) with paid amounts is critical information that the proposed structure would bury.

### 6. REFERRAL REASON & AUTHORITY LEVEL (Not in proposed structure)
The template's Section 1 (Referral Reason) exists because underwriters must explain WHY this referral requires higher authority. Tiago's deal requires SVP approval due to the 2-year term and limit size. Jackson's deal needs Paul Larson due to STM. This governance context is absent from the proposed structure.

### 7. CONVERSATIONAL DELIBERATION (Cannot be captured in proposed structure)
Perhaps the most striking finding: real underwriting is not a static document -- it is a CONVERSATION. Jackson's Dollar Tree example spans weeks of back-and-forth where the recommendation evolves from "pass" (Paul Larson) to "consider with prior acts exclusion" to "specific matter exclusion" to "quote subject to endorsement review." The proposed structure assumes a one-shot analytical output, but real underwriting involves iterative judgment refinement. Any AI tool must support this conversational workflow, not just generate a report.

## PART 7: Recommendations for the AI-Assisted Referral Worksheet

### The proposed six-section structure should be EXPANDED to nine sections:

| # | Section | Source |
|---|---|---|
| 0 | **Deal Summary & Tower Context** | NEW -- from real practice |
| 1 | **Company Profile & Industry Context** | Original proposed |

### Priority gaps the AI tool should fill (things NO underwriter currently does):
1. **Peer-relative financial benchmarking** -- Highest-value, most feasible for AI
2. **Guidance track record with stock impact** -- Highly automatable from earnings data
3. **Analyst sentiment aggregation** -- Readily available from financial data providers
4. **Prospective trigger calendar** -- Can be assembled from SEC filings, earnings dates, regulatory calendars
5. **Industry/competitor litigation benchmarking** -- Can be built from SCAC/NERA data
6. **Accounting quality indicators** -- Can be computed from financial statement ratios
7. **Alternative signals** -- Glassdoor, LinkedIn, job postings -- can be scraped and trended

### What the AI tool should NOT try to replace:
1. Deal negotiation judgment (prior acts vs. specific matter exclusion decisions)
2. Broker relationship management
3. Internal Liberty relationship checks (ART system)
4. Claims adjuster input and interpretation
5. The conversational, iterative nature of the approval process
6. "Gut feel" on pricing adequacy relative to risk appetite
