## 4. Filing Trends by Type (10b-5, Section 11, Section 12)

**Rule 10b-5 (Exchange Act Section 10(b))**: The workhorse of securities fraud litigation. Requires proof of scienter (intent to deceive). Constitutes the majority of all core securities class action filings (typically 60-75%).

**Section 11 (Securities Act of 1933)**: Targets misstatements in registration statements for IPOs and other offerings. Does NOT require scienter. Post-Cyan (2018), Section 11 filings increased dramatically in state courts:
- 2015-2017: Only 19 state court Section 11 filings total
- 2018: 35 state court Section 11 filings
- 2019: 49 state court Section 11 filings

**Section 12 (Securities Act of 1933)**: Covers liability for offers/sales by means of a defective prospectus. Often paired with Section 11 claims. Less common as standalone.

**Merger-Objection Cases (Section 14(a))**: Proxy/disclosure challenges in M&A contexts. These drove the 2016-2019 filing surge (over 180 merger-related filings in 2017 alone), but declined **61%** from 2021 to 2022 and continued declining through 2024, with only **8% of 2024 cases** having merger-related allegations.

**Sources**: NERA 2024 Full-Year Review; Cornerstone Research; Stanford SCAC.

## 6. Impact of PSLRA (1995) on Filing Rates and Success Rates

The Private Securities Litigation Reform Act of 1995 was enacted to curb perceived abuses in securities class action litigation.

**Key PSLRA Provisions**:
- Heightened pleading standards (must plead with particularity facts giving rise to a "strong inference" of scienter)
- Lead plaintiff provisions (preference for institutional investors)
- Discovery stay pending motion to dismiss
- Safe harbor for forward-looking statements
- Proportionate liability (replacing joint and several for certain defendants)

**Impact on Filing Rates**: Contrary to expectations, the PSLRA **did not materially reduce the number of filings**. NERA data spanning years before and after the law's passage shows a roughly comparable number of suits filed from 1991 through 2007, with only a brief dip in 1996. Subsequent NERA and Cornerstone Research reports show no appreciable long-term drop.

**Impact on Dismissal Rates**: The PSLRA had a **measurable impact on dismissal rates**:
- Pre-PSLRA: Dismissal rates were approximately 30-40%
- Post-PSLRA (2000-2006): Dismissal rates ranged between **53% and 68%**
- More recent cohorts (2016-2017 filing years): Dismissal rates of **52-56%**

**Impact on Case Quality**: Statistically significant evidence suggests the Act improved overall case quality, particularly in circuits that strictly interpret the heightened pleading standard. The institutional lead plaintiff provision was associated with a **58.9% increase in settlement value** for cases led by institutional investors and pension funds.

**Impact on Venue**: Pre-PSLRA, plaintiffs could file in state court to avoid federal pleading standards. This led directly to the enactment of SLUSA in 1998.

**Sources**: Labaton Keller Sucharow (PSLRA history and impact); Bloomberg Law (30 years analysis); University of Illinois Law Review (Perino study); academic studies on PSLRA effectiveness.

## 7. Impact of SLUSA (1998) on State vs. Federal Filings

The Securities Litigation Uniform Standards Act of 1998 was Congress's response to the "migration" of securities class actions from federal to state courts following the PSLRA.

**Key Provisions**:
- Preempts state-law class actions (50+ members) alleging fraud "in connection with the purchase or sale" of covered securities
- Forces most securities fraud class actions into federal court under PSLRA standards
- Exempts: state-agency actions, pension plan actions, derivative actions, actions under state of incorporation law

**Impact**: SLUSA effectively closed the state-court back door for securities fraud class actions under state common law. However, it **did not preempt Section 11 claims** under the Securities Act of 1933, which retained concurrent state/federal jurisdiction -- an issue that became pivotal in Cyan v. Beaver County (2018).

**Sources**: Sullivan & Cromwell (SLUSA navigation guide); Louisiana Law Review; Congress.gov.

## 8. Impact of Morrison v. National Australia Bank (2010) on Foreign-Cubed Cases

**Morrison v. National Australia Bank, 561 U.S. 247 (2010)** established a bright-line "transactional test": Section 10(b) applies only in connection with:
1. The purchase or sale of a security listed on a United States stock exchange, OR
2. The purchase or sale of any other security in the United States

**Impact**: Morrison **extinguished two categories** of claims that had proliferated:
- **"Foreign-cubed" (F-cubed)**: Foreign plaintiffs suing foreign issuers for losses on foreign exchanges -- completely eliminated
- **"Foreign-squared"**: Domestic plaintiffs suing foreign issuers for losses on foreign exchanges -- eliminated

**D&O Underwriting Significance**: Post-Morrison, U.S.-listed foreign private issuers remain fully exposed to U.S. securities class actions, but foreign companies listed only on non-U.S. exchanges gained significant protection. This narrowed the population of potential defendants and reduced aggregate filing exposure for foreign-only listed companies. However, companies with ADRs or cross-listings remain exposed.

**Sources**: Hughes Hubbard & Reed; Harvard Law School Forum on Corporate Governance; Cornell Law Institute.

## 9. Impact of Cyan v. Beaver County (2018) on Section 11 State Court Filings

**Cyan, Inc. v. Beaver County Employees Retirement Fund, 583 U.S. ___ (2018)** -- unanimous Supreme Court decision:
- Confirmed that Securities Act of 1933 class actions (particularly Section 11 claims) **may be brought in state court**
- Confirmed that such cases are **not removable to federal court**
- SLUSA did not strip state courts of concurrent jurisdiction over '33 Act claims

**Quantitative Impact**:
- **2015-2017**: Only **19 total** state court Section 11 filings
- **2018**: **35** state court Section 11 filings (84% increase)
- **2019**: **49** state court Section 11 filings (157% increase over pre-Cyan baseline)
- Created a wave of **parallel litigation** -- simultaneous state and federal suits on the same offerings

**D&O Underwriting Significance**: Cyan dramatically expanded IPO litigation risk. Companies going public now face potential class actions in both federal court (under Rule 10b-5) and state court (under Section 11/12), with state courts offering more plaintiff-friendly procedural rules and no PSLRA pleading standards. This led many companies to adopt federal forum provisions (FFPs) in their charters/bylaws, designating federal court as the exclusive forum for Securities Act claims -- a practice the Delaware Supreme Court upheld in *Salzberg v. Sciabacucchi* (2020).

**Sources**: Cornerstone Research (After Cyan report); Harvard Law School Forum; Fenwick & West; D&O Diary.

## 10. COVID-19 Impact on Filing Rates and Types

**Filing Volume**:
- First COVID-related securities suit: March 2020
- 2020: **33 COVID-related filings** (~10% of all 2020 filings)
- 2021: **20 COVID-related filings** (decreased from 2020)
- 2022-2024: Continued at reduced but persistent levels
- **2024**: 19 COVID-related cases filed -- a **46% increase over 2023** (a surprising late resurgence)

**Categories of COVID Litigation**:
1. **Outbreak-in-facilities cases**: Companies that suffered COVID outbreaks at their operations and allegedly failed to disclose workplace safety risks
2. **COVID-profiteering cases**: Companies that claimed they would profit from the pandemic (tests, vaccines, PPE, telehealth) but whose products underperformed
3. **Pandemic disruption cases**: Companies whose operations or finances were disrupted by the pandemic, allegedly without adequate disclosure
4. **Pandemic reversal cases**: Companies that prospered initially due to pandemic restrictions but whose fortunes declined as restrictions eased (e.g., Peloton, Zoom)

**Settlement Activity**: The number of securities class action settlements reached a **15-year high in 2022**, with a dramatic increase in both the number and total dollar value, partly driven by resolution of pandemic-era cases.

**Sources**: Cornerstone Research; Woodruff Sawyer; D&O Diary; Skadden Insights.

## 11. SPAC-Related Litigation Wave (2020-2024)

**The SPAC Boom**: In 2021, an unprecedented **1,013 companies went public -- 613 of them via SPACs**. This created a massive pool of potential litigation targets.

**Filing Timeline**:
- **2021-2022**: SPACs contributed to **over one-third of all filings**
- **2023**: SPAC filings began declining but remained significant
- **2024**: SPAC-related filings fell to **only 9 suits** (decline of more than 50% from 2023)

**Settlement Patterns**:
- **19% of all 2024 settlements** were SPAC-related
- Median SPAC settlement: **$12.0 million** (vs. $15.3 million for non-SPAC cases)
- SPAC settlements tend to be **lower** than general securities class action settlements, partly due to limited D&O insurance availability for many SPACs
- **Record SPAC settlements (2025)**: Alta Mesa SPAC suit settled for **$126.3 million**; Grab Holdings SPAC suit settled for **$80 million** -- among the largest SPAC-related settlements ever

**D&O Underwriting Significance**: SPACs presented unique D&O risks including: thin capitalization of the SPAC entity, limited D&O insurance purchased pre-de-SPAC, potential for personal liability of SPAC sponsors, and questions about whether the de-SPAC transaction constituted an "IPO" triggering Section 11 liability.

**Sources**: Cornerstone Research 2024 Review; NERA Reports; D&O Diary.

## 11. Role of Insurance in Settlements

**D&O Insurance as Primary Funding Source**: In the vast majority of securities class action settlements, D&O insurance is the primary or sole source of settlement funding.

**Key Data Points**:
- **Enron**: $7.2B total -- banks paid $6.6B (JP Morgan $2.2B, Citigroup $2B, CIBC $2.4B); directors paid $168M collectively; D&O insurance paid an additional $155M
- **WorldCom**: D&O insurance paid **$36M**; directors paid **$18M** out-of-pocket (aggregate); total director-related settlement: $54M
- **Boeing 737 MAX derivative**: $237.5M settlement -- **entirely funded by D&O insurance**
- **Wells Fargo derivative (2019)**: $240M paid by **D&O insurers** to Wells Fargo
- **Wells Fargo securities class action (2018)**: $480M -- appeared to be **corporate-funded** (fully accrued)

**General Pattern**: For cases that settle within D&O policy limits, insurers pay 100% of the settlement plus defense costs. For mega-cases exceeding policy limits, the company often contributes corporate funds. In bankruptcy situations, Side A coverage becomes critical as the company cannot indemnify.

**Sources**: Stanford Law School (WorldCom/Enron director settlements); D&O Diary; Hunton Insurance Recovery Blog.

## 12. Individual Officer Contributions to Settlements

Personal out-of-pocket payments by directors and officers are **extremely rare** but have occurred in landmark cases:

**Notable Personal Contributions**:
- **WorldCom directors**: 10 directors paid **$18M** out-of-pocket (~20% of combined net worth, excluding primary residences and retirement accounts). D&O insurance paid an additional $36M.
- **Enron directors**: 10 directors paid **$13M** out-of-pocket. D&O insurance paid an additional $155M.
- **Just for Feet**: Executives drained all D&O policies, leaving independent directors to pay **$41.5M** from personal assets.
- **UnitedHealth Group (backdating)**: Former CEO McGuire agreed to a **$468M settlement** including reimbursement of equity-based compensation.

**Key Principle**: Lead plaintiffs in WorldCom and Enron **insisted** that directors' personal payments not be reimbursed by insurance or indemnification -- a deliberate departure from standard practice intended to create personal deterrence.

**D&O Underwriting Significance**: These cases drove demand for "Side A DIC" (difference-in-conditions) policies and personal "wealth security" policies for outside directors. They remain the primary justification for standalone Side A coverage.

**Sources**: Stanford Lawyer Magazine; Mondaq; Washington Post; Woodruff Sawyer.

# PART III: DISMISSAL RATES AND LITIGATION OUTCOMES

## 1. Motion to Dismiss Success Rates Over Time

**Overall Pattern**: With "surprising uniformity" across the most active judicial districts, plaintiffs and defendants each bat about **.500** at the motion to dismiss stage historically.

**Detailed Statistics**:
- Courts ruled on motions in **73%** of cases
- Of those decided: **80% dismissed** in whole or in part:
  - 54% dismissed without prejudice
  - 7% dismissed with prejudice
  - 19% partially granted
- In 2024, **57% of securities class actions** were dismissed before any discovery took place

**Post-MTD Settlement Rate**: Cases that survive a motion to dismiss settle **over 90%** of the time.

**Sources**: BakerHostetler; Davis Wright Tremaine; NERA.

---

## 2. How Tellabs v. Makor Issues (2007) Affected Dismissal Rates

**Tellabs, Inc. v. Makor Issues & Rights, Ltd., 551 U.S. 308 (2007)** clarified the PSLRA's scienter pleading standard:
- Replaced the lenient "reasonableness" standard with a requirement that the inference of scienter be "at least as compelling as any opposing inference" drawn from the facts alleged
- While this reversed a more lenient standard, the replacement standard was "nonetheless relatively generous to plaintiffs"

**Empirical Impact**: An Oxford Academic study found statistically significant evidence that Tellabs improved case quality in circuits that strictly interpret the heightened standard. However, the overall dismissal rate impact was **modest** -- the standard was clearer but not dramatically more stringent than what many circuits were already applying.

**Sources**: Oxford Academic (Journal of Law, Economics, and Organization); Supreme Court opinion.

---

## 3. Impact of Halliburton II (2014) on Class Certification

**Halliburton Co. v. Erica P. John Fund, Inc., 573 U.S. 258 (2014)**:
- Preserved the "fraud on the market" presumption of reliance (from Basic Inc. v. Levinson)
- BUT allowed defendants to **rebut the presumption at the class certification stage** by showing lack of price impact
- Created a new battlefield at class certification that had previously been largely pro forma

**Impact**: Halliburton II only affects cases that have **already survived a motion to dismiss** -- class certification is premature before then. A Princeton study analyzed the litigation effects and found that while the decision created additional defense tools, its practical impact on class certification rates was **limited** because defendants rarely succeed in proving zero price impact. The most significant effect has been increased use of event studies and expert economic testimony at the class certification stage.

**Sources**: Princeton GCEPS study; Supreme Court opinion; D&O Discourse.

---

## 4. Dismissal Rates by Circuit

Dismissal rates vary by circuit, though with "surprising uniformity" in the most active districts:

**General Pattern**:
- Second Circuit (New York): Historically moderate dismissal rates (~50-55%)
- Ninth Circuit (California): Similar to Second Circuit
- Third Circuit (Delaware/Pennsylvania): Variable; increasing Section 11 cases post-Cyan
- Northern District of California (Silicon Valley): Slightly more plaintiff-friendly

**2024-2025 Trends**: Defendants achieved a "considerably higher dismissal rate than the historical average" in the first half of 2025.

**Sources**: NERA; Davis Wright Tremaine; BakerHostetler.

---

## 5. Dismissal Rates by Allegation Type

| Allegation Type | Dismissal Rate | Notes |
|----------------|---------------|-------|
| **Merger-objection cases** | **93%** (2012-2021) | Vast majority dismissed; only 6% settle |
| **Missed earnings/guidance** | ~40-50% | Moderate; depends on additional allegations |
| **Accounting restatement** | ~30-40% | Lower dismissal rate due to stronger factual basis |
| **Insider trading** | ~35-45% | Moderate |
| **Regulatory/product failure** | ~45-55% | Variable |

**Key Insight**: Cases with accounting restatements or SEC parallel investigations have the **lowest dismissal rates** because the restatement or enforcement action itself provides strong circumstantial evidence of scienter and materiality.

**Sources**: NERA; Davis Wright Tremaine; Cornerstone Research.

---

## 6. Average Litigation Costs Even for Dismissed Cases

- Average legal defense costs for a securities class action: **~$10 million**
- Defense costs for the largest cases: Up to **$100 million**
- Defense costs in the past six years have **almost doubled** for large D&O claims
- Defense costs can absorb **one quarter to one third** of the insured sum

**Even Dismissed Cases Are Expensive**: A case that is successfully dismissed at the motion to dismiss stage may still incur **$2-5 million** in defense costs, depending on complexity, the number of motions, and whether there was an amended complaint. Cases dismissed after discovery may cost **$5-15 million+** in defense alone.

**Trend**: The NAIC D&O market's reported incurred loss and defense expense ratios increased from an average of **62% (2012-2016)** to an average of **75% (2017-2020)**. Calendar year 2021 was 64%, but 2022 saw a return to 69%.

**Sources**: Allianz AGCS; NAIC data; A.M. Best.

---

## 7. Partial Dismissal Patterns

**19% of motions to dismiss** are partially granted -- meaning some claims survive while others are dismissed. This commonly occurs when:
- Individual officer claims are dismissed but entity claims survive (or vice versa)
- Some class period allegations survive but others do not
- Certain statutory claims (e.g., Section 11) survive while others (e.g., 10b-5) are dismissed
- Claims against certain defendants (e.g., underwriters) are dismissed while claims against the company survive

**Significance for D&O**: Partial dismissals still result in settlements because surviving claims provide leverage. The settlement value is typically lower than if all claims survived, but significant.

**Sources**: BakerHostetler; NERA.

## 8. Relationship Between SEC Enforcement and Private Litigation

**Follow-On Litigation**: SEC investigations are **often followed** by private lawsuits, usually shareholder class actions or derivative suits. An SEC enforcement action against a company significantly increases the probability and severity of private securities litigation because:

1. **Admission/preclusion effects**: Settlements or findings in SEC proceedings can be admissible in private litigation and may have preclusive effect
2. **Privilege waiver**: Once a privilege is waived in SEC proceedings, it is waived in all proceedings -- documents produced to the SEC must also be produced in shareholder suits
3. **Scienter evidence**: SEC findings provide powerful evidence of fraudulent intent, reducing dismissal risk in private litigation
4. **Stock price impact**: SEC actions often trigger stock price declines that form the basis for private damages claims

**Quantitative Relationship**: Cases accompanied by SEC enforcement actions settle for **significantly more** than comparable cases without enforcement. The presence of a parallel SEC investigation or enforcement action is among the strongest predictors of settlement size.

**Sources**: ABA Business Law; Gibson Dunn; Winston & Strawn; PBI.

# PART V: LOSS DEVELOPMENT AND ACTUARIAL DATA

## 1. D&O Loss Development Triangles

D&O claims develop **slowly** compared to most property-casualty lines:

**Key Development Characteristics**:
- At **24 months** after policy inception: Almost **90% of losses are still unreserved** (IBNR)
- Claims from the competitive soft market years **(2015-2019)** continue to develop adversely, with an additional **$472 million** of adverse development in 2024 alone
- Full development typically requires **7-10+ years** for securities class actions due to litigation duration

**Development Pattern**:
- Year 1-2: Claim notification; initial reserves established
- Year 2-4: Motion to dismiss stage; significant reserve adjustments
- Year 4-6: Discovery/class certification; large reserve development
- Year 6-8: Settlement negotiations; final payments
- Year 8-10+: Tail development; appeals; late settlements

**Sources**: Milliman; Risk & Insurance; TransRe; Cornerstone Research.

- Mega-settlements (>$100M) account for 50-60% of total settlement value annually
- Derivative suit severity is increasing faster than class action severity

**Combined Effect**: The D&O market is experiencing **moderate frequency but rapidly increasing severity**, driven by:
- Larger defendant companies being sued
- Higher investor losses per case
- Increasing defense costs
- Growing derivative suit exposure
- AI-related claims targeting large technology companies

## 5. Attachment Point Erosion Patterns

**Excess Layer Dynamics**:
- D&O programs are typically structured as **towers** with a primary policy and multiple excess layers
- The primary layer is most likely to be eroded, but **mega-cases regularly burn through multiple excess layers**
- New market entrants (predominantly MGAs) are writing excess layers at **lower attachment levels** than seen in prior policy renewals

**Attachment Point Trends**:
- During the hard market (2020-2021), reinsurers secured **higher attachment points**
- In the soft market (2022-2025), carriers may secure **lower attachment points** from reinsurers
- Double-digit cost reductions are projected for 2026 treaty reinsurance renewals

**Erosion Risk**: In mega-cases (Enron, WorldCom, financial crisis), entire D&O towers of $100M-$500M+ were **completely exhausted**, leaving directors personally exposed.

**Sources**: D&O Diary; IRMI; AmWins; Price Forbes.

---

## 6. Side A vs. Side B vs. Side C Loss Allocation

| Coverage | What It Covers | Trigger | Typical Retention | % of Losses |
|----------|---------------|---------|-------------------|-------------|
| **Side A** | Non-indemnifiable losses to individual D&Os | Company unable/unwilling to indemnify (bankruptcy, derivative suit, SEC bars) | **None** (first dollar) | Growing share; derivative suits increasing |
| **Side B** | Reimburses company for indemnification of D&Os | Company indemnifies D&Os | SIR/deductible | Historically largest component |
| **Side C** | Entity coverage for securities claims | Company named in securities suit | SIR/deductible | Significant in class actions (public companies: securities claims only) |

**Allocation Disputes**: When claims involve both covered and uncovered matters or parties, allocation becomes contentious. Insurers often advance only **less than 20%, often less than 10%** of defense costs during disputes, even when acknowledging coverage.

**Trend**: Side A coverage has become **more expensive** and more critical as derivative suit severity increases. Side A DIC (difference-in-conditions) policies are increasingly standard for public company boards.

**Sources**: Woodruff Sawyer; Lockton; Hylant; The Coyle Group.

## 7. Defense Cost Trends and Ratios

**Historical Trend**: Defense costs have **almost doubled** for large D&O claims in the past six years.

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Average defense cost (typical case) | **~$10 million** |
| Defense cost (largest cases) | Up to **$100 million** |
| Defense costs as % of insured sum | **25-33%** of available limits |
| NAIC loss + defense ratio (2012-2016) | Average **62%** |
| NAIC loss + defense ratio (2017-2020) | Average **75%** |
| NAIC loss + defense ratio (2021) | **64%** |
| NAIC loss + defense ratio (2022) | **69%** |

**Key Issue**: Most D&O policies provide defense costs **within limits** (not in addition to limits), meaning every dollar spent on defense **reduces** the amount available for settlement. This creates tension between vigorous defense and policy limit preservation.

**Sources**: Allianz AGCS; A.M. Best; NAIC; Hinshaw & Culbertson.

---

## 8. Excess Layer Burning Patterns

**Tower Exhaustion in Mega-Cases**: In the largest D&O cases, the entire insurance tower is at risk:

- **Typical public company D&O tower**: $25M-$500M+ in total limits (primary + 5-15 excess layers)
- **Mega-settlements ($200M+)**: Will typically exhaust a standard D&O tower
- **Catastrophe cases ($1B+)**: Exhaust all available insurance; require corporate contribution and potentially personal director contributions

**Burning Pattern**:
1. Primary layer: Eroded first by defense costs and early settlement negotiations
2. First 2-3 excess layers: Often eroded during discovery and class certification
3. Upper excess layers: Engaged in mega-settlements; may have "follow form" coverage issues
4. Side A DIC: Last resort for individual directors when tower is exhausted

**Carrier Behavior**: Excess carriers take the position that they have **no obligation to "drop down"** to the underlying carrier's attachment point if the underlying insurer becomes insolvent or disputes coverage.

**Sources**: D&O Diary; Bailey Cavalieri; TransRe; AmWins.

# PART VI: NOTABLE CASE STUDIES

## 1. Enron/WorldCom Era Cases (2001-2008)

### Enron Corporation

**What Happened**: Enron used off-balance-sheet special purpose entities (SPEs) to hide debt and inflate profits. CFO Andrew Fastow created partnerships (LJM1, LJM2) that engaged in conflicted transactions with Enron. Arthur Andersen, Enron's auditor, was complicit in the fraud and destroyed documents. Enron filed for bankruptcy on December 2, 2001 -- then the largest bankruptcy in U.S. history.

**D&O Claim**: Shareholders filed a $40 billion class action alleging that officers, directors, and various banks and advisors made material misrepresentations about Enron's financial condition, concealed massive debt, and artificially inflated Enron's stock price.

**Resolution**:
- Total settlement: **$7.2 billion** (2008) -- the largest securities class action settlement in history
- **JP Morgan Chase**: $2.2B; **Citigroup**: $2.0B; **CIBC**: $2.4B (banks were primary payors)
- **Enron directors**: $168M collectively (with $155M from D&O insurance + $13M personal)
- Arthur Andersen: Convicted of obstruction; collapsed (reversed on appeal but too late)
- Andrew Fastow: 6 years prison; Jeff Skilling: 24 years (later reduced to 14); Ken Lay: convicted, died before sentencing

**Risk Factors Present BEFORE the Event**:
- Extreme complexity of financial structure; SPEs with conflicting management
- Auditor independence concerns (Andersen was also a major consulting provider)
- Aggressive revenue recognition; mark-to-market accounting for energy trades
- Insider trading by senior executives (Fastow self-dealing)
- Board failure to oversee conflicts of interest
- Executive compensation heavily tied to stock price
- Whistleblower warnings (Sherron Watkins memo) ignored

### WorldCom

**What Happened**: WorldCom overstated revenues by billions of dollars through improper capitalization of line costs and inflated revenue entries. CFO Scott Sullivan directed the fraud. Discovered by internal audit in June 2002. Filed for bankruptcy July 21, 2002 -- then the largest bankruptcy in U.S. history (surpassing Enron).

**D&O Claim**: Shareholders alleged massive accounting fraud that artificially inflated WorldCom's stock price.

**Resolution**:
- Total settlement: **$6.1 billion** (2005) -- second-largest securities class action settlement
- **10 outside directors** paid **$18 million out of pocket** (~20% of combined net worth)
- D&O insurance contributed **$36 million** toward director settlements
- CEO Bernard Ebbers: 25 years prison (released 2020); CFO Scott Sullivan: 5 years prison

**Risk Factors Present BEFORE the Event**:
- Rapid growth through serial acquisitions with aggressive accounting
- Dominant CEO with weak board oversight
- CFO under intense pressure to meet earnings targets
- Declining telecom industry fundamentals
- Internal controls weakness; small internal audit staff
- External auditor (Andersen) failed to detect capitalization of operating expenses

**Sources**: Stanford Law School; AEI; Robbins Geller; ISS SCAS.

## 2. Stock Option Backdating Wave (2006-2008)

**What Happened**: Hundreds of companies were discovered to have granted stock options to executives with exercise prices set to dates when the stock price was at a low, rather than the actual grant date. This practice understated compensation expense and violated GAAP.

**Scale**: By end of 2007, **141 companies** had been investigated (per WSJ); SEC brought no backdating cases before 2006; federal prosecutors filed criminal charges against **more than 100 executives**, convicting 12 and sending 5 to prison.

**D&O Claims**: 39 securities class actions were filed. By 2009, 30 had reached conclusion: **9 dismissed, 21 settled**. Average settlement: **$31.82 million** (excluding the largest).

**Notable Settlements**:
- **UnitedHealth Group**: Former CEO McGuire agreed to **$468 million** settlement including reimbursement of equity compensation
- **Mercury Interactive**: **$117.5 million** SEC settlement (then a record)
- **Research in Motion (RIM)**: Backdated millions of options over 8 years (1998-2006)

**Risk Factors Present BEFORE the Event**:
- Statistical impossibility of grant-date patterns (academic research by Erik Lie at University of Iowa first identified the pattern)
- Weak compensation committee oversight
- Lack of independent timing verification
- Compensation consultants with conflicts of interest
- Options-heavy executive compensation in technology sector

**Sources**: Gibson Dunn; WSJ investigation; CLS Blue Sky Blog; Kessler Topaz.

## 3. Financial Crisis Mortgage/CDO Cases (2008-2012)

**What Happened**: Banks packaged subprime mortgages into residential mortgage-backed securities (RMBS) and collateralized debt obligations (CDOs), selling them to investors while allegedly knowing the underlying mortgages were defective. When the housing market collapsed, these securities became virtually worthless.

**Scale of Government Settlements**:
| Bank | Total Settlement | Type |
|------|-----------------|------|
| **Bank of America** | **$16.65 billion** | DOJ/state settlement (largest civil settlement with a single entity in U.S. history) |
| **JPMorgan Chase** | **$13 billion** | DOJ settlement |
| **Citigroup** | **$7 billion** | DOJ settlement |
| **Goldman Sachs** | **$5 billion+** | DOJ/state settlement |
| **Deutsche Bank** | **$7.2 billion** | DOJ settlement |
| **Morgan Stanley** | **$3.2 billion** | DOJ settlement |

**Overall**: Bank settlements from 2008 financial crisis litigation totaled approximately **$100 billion+** across all regulatory and private proceedings.

**Securities Class Action Settlements**:
- **Bank of America/Merrill Lynch**: **$2.43 billion** (related to Merrill Lynch acquisition disclosures)
- **Lehman Brothers**: **~$735 million** (securities class action)
- **Goldman Sachs (ABACUS CDO)**: **$550 million** SEC settlement (then the largest SEC settlement with a Wall Street firm)

**SEC Enforcement**: The SEC brought enforcement actions resulting in over **$4.5 billion** in penalties related to the financial crisis.

**Risk Factors Present BEFORE the Event**:
- Rapidly deteriorating underwriting standards (stated income/"liar loans")
- Skyrocketing housing prices (bubble indicators)
- Massive growth in subprime originations
- Rating agency failures (AAA ratings on fundamentally risky securities)
- Compensation structures incentivizing volume over quality
- Excessive leverage ratios at major banks
- Derivatives complexity exceeding risk management capabilities

**Sources**: DOJ press releases; SEC enforcement spotlight; USC Gould Corporate Fraud Forum.

## 4. Chinese Reverse Merger Fraud Cases (2010-2013)

**What Happened**: Approximately 300+ Chinese companies accessed U.S. capital markets through reverse mergers with dormant U.S. shell companies, bypassing the traditional IPO registration process. Many were subsequently revealed to have fabricated financial statements, misrepresented assets, and diverted company funds.

**Scale**:
- **30+ shareholder suits** filed in H1 2011 alone
- Accounting cases rose to **70 in 2011** (from 46 in 2010), driven largely by Chinese reverse merger cases
- These cases increased accounting cases from **26% to 37%** of total filings

**Settlement Patterns**:
- Average settlement for three CRM cases: **$1.5 million** (extremely low)
- Settlement as % of total assets: **1.25%**; as % of market value: **7.54%**
- CRM cases "significantly more likely to involve restatements"

**D&O Underwriting Significance**:
- Many Chinese reverse merger companies had **minimal or no D&O insurance**
- Even when insurance existed, **coverage disputes** arose over misrepresentation in the insurance application
- **Uncollectible judgments**: Chinese-based defendants were often beyond U.S. court jurisdiction
- Led to severe underwriting scrutiny for China-based U.S.-listed companies
- Spawned development of short-seller research firms (Muddy Waters, Citron Research, etc.)

**Risk Factors Present BEFORE the Event**:
- Reverse merger entry (bypassing IPO due diligence)
- China-based operations with limited auditor access
- Small U.S.-based audit firms with limited China expertise
- Aggressive growth claims inconsistent with industry conditions
- Limited English-language disclosure
- Related-party transactions
- Variable Interest Entity (VIE) structures

**Sources**: University of Minnesota MJIL; Hofstra JIBL; Texas A&M Bush School study; Wikipedia.

## 5. Valeant Pharmaceuticals

**What Happened**: Valeant's business model centered on acquiring drugs and dramatically raising prices while cutting R&D. The company used a captive network of specialty pharmacies (given code names based on chess terms) to inflate revenues. When these practices were exposed (partly by short-seller Citron Research and journalist reports), the stock collapsed from ~$260 to under $10.

**D&O Claim**: Class action on behalf of investors who purchased securities from January 4, 2013 through March 15, 2016, alleging the company inflated revenues through captive pharmacy distribution and concealed its relationship with Philidor Rx Services.

**Resolution**:
- Settlement: **$1.21 billion** (announced December 2019; final approval January 2021)
- Ranks among the **top 10** securities class action settlements of all time
- D&O insurance was the subject of **separate litigation** -- the company had only $825M in cash when the settlement was announced

**Risk Factors Present BEFORE the Event**:
- Aggressive drug pricing strategy attracting political/regulatory scrutiny
- Serial acquisition model dependent on financial engineering, not organic growth
- Hidden captive distribution network (Philidor)
- Short-seller reports raising red flags
- CEO Michael Pearson's aggressive management style
- Board governance concerns
- Reliance on debt financing for acquisitions

**Sources**: Robbins Geller; Stanford SCAC; Broadridge; D&O Diary.

## 6. Wells Fargo Fake Accounts

**What Happened**: Between 2002 and 2016, Wells Fargo employees opened approximately **3.5 million unauthorized** bank and credit card accounts, enrolled customers in online banking without consent, and ordered debit cards without authorization -- all to meet aggressive cross-selling targets. The scandal was first publicly exposed in September 2016 when the CFPB, OCC, and City of Los Angeles jointly announced enforcement actions.

**D&O Claims and Resolutions**:
| Settlement | Amount | Year | Funded By |
|-----------|--------|------|-----------|
| **Securities class action** | **$480 million** | 2018 | Appears corporate-funded |
| **Derivative suit** | **$240 million** (cash) + $80M governance reforms | 2019 | **D&O insurers** paid $240M |
| **DOJ/SEC criminal/civil** | **$3 billion** | 2020 | Corporate |
| **SEC securities fraud** | **$500 million** | 2020 | Corporate |
| **Securities class action (consent order)** | **$1 billion** | 2023 | Corporate |

**Total D&O-related exposure**: Over **$5 billion** across all proceedings.

**Risk Factors Present BEFORE the Event**:
- "Eight is great" cross-selling culture (pushing 8 products per customer)
- Unrealistic sales targets with intense pressure on front-line employees
- Internal complaints and whistleblower reports **ignored for years**
- **2013**: Los Angeles Times expose on unauthorized accounts
- High employee turnover in retail branches
- Incentive compensation structure rewarding volume, not customer satisfaction
- Board audit committee failed to escalate early warnings

**Sources**: D&O Diary; Cohen Milstein; Paul Weiss; DOJ press release; Wikipedia.

## 7. Boeing 737 MAX

**What Happened**: Two Boeing 737 MAX aircraft crashed -- Lion Air Flight 610 (October 2018, 189 killed) and Ethiopian Airlines Flight 302 (March 2019, 157 killed) -- due to a flawed flight control system (MCAS) that was designed to compensate for engine placement changes but was inadequately tested and disclosed to pilots. The 737 MAX was grounded worldwide for 20 months.

**D&O Claims and Resolutions**:
| Proceeding | Amount | Year | Notes |
|-----------|--------|------|-------|
| **Derivative suit** (Delaware Chancery) | **$237.5 million** | 2021 | **100% funded by D&O insurance**; second-largest insurer-funded derivative settlement in history |
| **SEC settlement** | **$200 million** | 2022 | Boeing and former CEO Dennis Muilenburg misled investors about 737 MAX safety |
| **DOJ criminal** | Plea agreement | 2025 | Boeing reached deal to avoid prosecution |
| **Securities class action** | **Ongoing** | Pending | Labaton Keller Sucharow as lead counsel |

**Governance Reforms (from derivative settlement)**:
- Additional board director with aviation/engineering/safety expertise
- Codified separation of CEO and Board Chair roles
- Mandatory board safety committee reporting
- Five-year Ombudsperson Program for employee safety concerns
- Public disclosure of safety enhancement programs

**Risk Factors Present BEFORE the Event**:
- Post-merger culture shift (Boeing/McDonnell Douglas) from engineering-first to financial-engineering
- Cost pressure to avoid pilot simulator training for MAX transition
- Single-point-of-failure MCAS design (reliance on single angle-of-attack sensor)
- Weak internal safety reporting culture
- Board lacked aviation safety expertise
- CEO/COB combined role
- Regulatory capture (FAA delegated certification authority to Boeing itself)

**Sources**: Hunton Insurance Recovery Blog; Lieff Cabraser; SEC press release; D&O Diary.

## 8. Opioid Manufacturer D&O Cases

**What Happened**: Purdue Pharma and other opioid manufacturers marketed opioid painkillers aggressively while allegedly minimizing addiction risks, contributing to an opioid epidemic that has killed over 500,000 Americans.

**Key Settlements**:
- **Purdue Pharma/Sackler family**: **$7.4 billion** (January 2025) -- Sacklers pay up to $6.5B over 15 years; Purdue contributes $900M; Sacklers permanently banned from opioid business
- **Endo International**: Approximately **$22.3 million** to New York + additional amounts through bankruptcy
- **McKinsey** (consulting advice on opioid marketing): **$230 million** securities-related settlement
- **Total opioid settlements** across all pharmaceutical companies and distributors: Over **$50 billion**

**D&O Securities Claims**: Securities class actions were filed against opioid manufacturers alleging they made misrepresentations about the safety of their products, their compliance with regulations, and the sustainability of their business models.

**Risk Factors Present BEFORE the Event**:
- Aggressive marketing to physicians despite known addiction risks
- Whistleblower complaints to FDA and DEA
- Growing overdose death statistics
- Academic research questioning marketing claims
- State AG investigations beginning as early as 2007
- High executive compensation tied to sales growth
- Product concentration risk (single therapeutic area)

**Sources**: NY Attorney General; DOJ press release; OST Global Settlement Tracker.

## 9. Theranos/Elizabeth Holmes

**What Happened**: Theranos, founded by Elizabeth Holmes, claimed to have developed revolutionary blood-testing technology that could run hundreds of tests from a single drop of blood. The technology never worked as described. Holmes raised over **$700 million** from investors based on false claims about the technology, revenue, partnerships, and regulatory status.

**Regulatory/Criminal Resolution**:
- **SEC settlement (2018)**: Holmes paid **$500,000** penalty, was barred from serving as officer/director for 10 years, returned 18.9 million shares, and relinquished voting control
- **Criminal conviction (2022)**: Holmes convicted of 1 count conspiracy + 3 counts wire fraud involving **$140 million+** in wire transfers
- **Sentencing**: **135 months** (11 years, 3 months) in federal prison
- **Ramesh "Sunny" Balwani**: Convicted separately on all 12 counts; sentenced to nearly 13 years

**D&O Insurance**: As a private company, Theranos's D&O insurance situation is less transparent. The SEC settlement was modest ($500K), but the criminal convictions -- which cannot be covered by D&O insurance (fraud exclusion) -- resulted in severe personal consequences.

**Risk Factors Present BEFORE the Event**:
- Technology claims that could not be independently verified
- Extreme secrecy around technology and operations
- Board heavy on political figures, light on scientific/medical expertise
- Refusal to publish peer-reviewed data
- Employee turnover (particularly in laboratory operations)
- **2015**: WSJ investigative reports by John Carreyrou
- Regulatory deficiencies identified by CMS inspections
- Founder personality cult and extreme fundraising valuation ($9B at peak)

**Sources**: SEC press release; DOJ/USAO press releases; Wikipedia; Britannica.

## 10. FTX/Crypto Collapse Cases

**What Happened**: FTX, one of the world's largest cryptocurrency exchanges, collapsed in November 2022 after it was revealed that customer funds were being diverted to Alameda Research (FTX's affiliated trading firm, also run by Sam Bankman-Fried) to cover trading losses and personal expenses. FTX filed for bankruptcy in November 2022.

**Litigation**:
- **Class action MDL**: Dozens of actions consolidated in June 2023; settlements reached with former FTX executives, seven promoter influencers, and an ex-FTX attorney
- **Criminal**: Bankman-Fried convicted on **7 federal counts** (November 2023); sentenced to **25 years** (March 2024)
- **CFTC**: Obtained **$12.7 billion judgment** against FTX and Alameda
- **SEC**: Enforcement actions ongoing

**Satellite Litigation**:
- **Silvergate Capital**: Securities fraud class action settled for **$37.5 million** (connected to FTX)
- **Binance, BlockFi, Genesis Global Capital**: Separate class actions

**D&O Insurance**: FTX reportedly had **limited or no D&O insurance**, which is common for crypto companies that struggled to obtain coverage. This significantly limits recovery for shareholders.

**Risk Factors Present BEFORE the Event**:
- Commingling of customer and proprietary funds
- Lack of independent board governance (SBF controlled virtually all decisions)
- No independent audit by a Big 4 firm
- Related-party transactions between FTX and Alameda
- Political donation activity far beyond business norms
- Rapid international expansion outpacing compliance infrastructure
- Celebrity endorsements as marketing strategy (raising red flags about retail targeting)
- Bahamas incorporation and operations (regulatory arbitrage)

**Sources**: ClassAction.org; Dechert; Bloomberg Law; CFTC press release.

## 11. SVB/Signature Bank Failures

**What Happened**: Silicon Valley Bank (SVB) collapsed on March 10, 2023, after a run on deposits triggered by unrealized losses on its bond portfolio (long-duration Treasury and agency securities that lost value as interest rates rose). Signature Bank was seized two days later. SVB was the second-largest bank failure in U.S. history.

**D&O Litigation**:
- **Securities class action** filed March 13, 2023 (three days after collapse) against SVB parent, CEO Greg Becker, and CFO Daniel Beck
- **Signature Bank securities class action** filed March 14, 2023
- **FDIC authorization (December 2024)**: Board voted unanimously to authorize suits against **6 former officers and 11 former directors** of SVB and SVB Financial Group

**Resolution**: Cases are **ongoing** as of 2025. No settlements announced yet.

**D&O Insurance Insight**: SVB's bankruptcy created a classic Side A scenario -- the bankrupt company cannot indemnify its directors and officers, making Side A coverage (and Side A DIC policies) the only source of protection for the individual defendants.

**Risk Factors Present BEFORE the Event**:
- Massive unrealized losses in bond portfolio (publicly available information)
- **85%+ uninsured deposits** (concentration risk)
- Technology sector concentration in customer base
- Rising interest rate environment with long-duration asset mismatch
- Failed capital raise (stock offering withdrawn, triggering panic)
- Poor ALM (asset-liability management) relative to rate risk
- Limited hedging of interest rate risk

**Sources**: D&O Diary; BLB&G; Woodruff Sawyer; FDIC press release.

## 13. Major 2024-2025 Developments

### 2024 Key Developments:
- **Record total settlements**: $4.1-4.75 billion in total approved settlements (highest excluding $1B+ individual cases)
- **Top 2024 settlements**: Apple ($490M), Under Armour ($434M), Alphabet ($350M), Uber ($200M)
- **AI-related filings doubled**: 14 AI-related securities class actions (up from 7 in 2023)
- **SPAC litigation declining**: Only 9 SPAC-related filings (down 50%+ from 2023)
- **Jarkesy v. SEC**: Supreme Court limited SEC's ability to seek penalties through administrative proceedings
- **SEC Climate Rules**: Adopted March 2024, stayed one month later
- **SEC FY 2024 record remedies**: $8.2 billion (driven by Terraform Labs)

### 2025 Key Developments:
- **Filings declined to 207** but DDL/MDL at record highs -- fewer but larger cases
- **NERA aggregate settlement value**: $2.9 billion (25% decline from 2024's $3.9 billion inflation-adjusted)
- **AI filings continued growing**: 16 AI-related filings; 76% of S&P 500 now mention AI in 10-Ks
- **SEC enforcement collapsed under new administration**: Only 313 total actions (lowest in decade); $808M in remedies (lowest ever during administration change)
- **FDIC authorized SVB lawsuit**: Suit against 6 officers and 11 directors
- **Record SPAC settlements**: Alta Mesa ($126.3M) and Grab Holdings ($80M)
- **Pending mega-settlements**: Alibaba ($433.5M) and General Electric ($362.5M) awaiting final approval
- **D&O insurance market**: Loss ratio hit 49.0% (best in 11 years); premiums fell to 2018 levels; 58 primary carriers (up from 45 in 2019); soft market may be ending as severity increases

**Sources**: Cornerstone Research; NERA; D&O Diary; Woodruff Sawyer; WTW; TransRe.
