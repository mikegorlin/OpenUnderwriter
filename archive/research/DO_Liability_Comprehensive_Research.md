# PART I: CLAIM TRIGGERS -- What Events Precede D&O Lawsuits?

## 1. Stock Price Drops and Corrective Disclosures

### Empirical Findings
- **There is no single "bright line" stock drop threshold** that automatically triggers a securities class action, but empirical data reveals clear patterns.
- In 2024, the average market capitalization loss per Rule 10b-5 class action was approximately $2.78 billion per lawsuit and $1.37 billion per alleged stock drop (NERA 2023 data).
- In Q1 2025, market cap losses per Rule 10b-5 claim increased by **72%** relative to Q4 2024, while market cap losses per alleged stock drop increased by **63.4%**.
- Disclosure Dollar Loss (DDL) reached **$694 billion** in 2025, up from $429 billion in 2024 (a 62% increase). Maximum Dollar Loss (MDL) reached **$2,862 billion** in 2025, the third-highest on record.
- In real cases: plaintiffs alleged a **32% stock drop** on a market cap of $50B+ (California marketing automation case) and a **15% stock drop** on a similar market cap (Colorado case).

### The "Corrective Disclosure" Pattern
- A "corrective disclosure" is any statement or action that reveals previously concealed truth related to an alleged misstatement or omission.
- The pattern typically follows: (1) Company makes allegedly misleading statements; (2) Stock price is inflated during the "class period"; (3) A corrective disclosure (earnings miss, restatement, investigation announcement, whistleblower report, media expose) reveals the truth; (4) Stock drops significantly; (5) Plaintiff law firms file within days to weeks.
- **Key underwriting metric**: The DDL Index measures aggregate dollar value of investor losses based on stock price declines following corrective disclosures.

### Strategic Shift in Targeting
- In 2024, companies sued had an **average market cap of $89 billion** and had been in business for **27 years**, reflecting a shift toward targeting mature, large-cap companies where potential recoveries justify substantial litigation investment.
- The probability of an **S&P 500 company** facing a core federal securities class action filing was **6.1%** in 2024.
- The overall annualized litigation rate for all U.S. exchange-listed companies was **3.8%** as of mid-2025.

### Data Sources
- Stanford Securities Class Action Clearinghouse (securities.stanford.edu)
- Cornerstone Research annual filings reports
- NERA Economic Consulting semi-annual trend reports

## 2. Financial Restatements

### Empirical Findings
- **About half of the -9.2% average restatement announcement effect is attributable to expected litigation costs** (Bardos, Golec et al., Journal of Financial Research, 2013).
- Firms with restatements prior to D&O coverage inception pay **higher D&O insurance premiums**, reflecting market-based assessment of heightened litigation risk.
- Higher levels of D&O insurance coverage are themselves associated with **greater risk-taking and higher probabilities of financial restatement** due to moral hazard (aggressive financial reporting).
- **Restatement cases represented 38%** of all accounting-related securities class actions in 2023, up from 33% in 2022, and the number of accounting case filings involving restatements in 2023 reached its **second-highest level in the last ten years**.

### Restatement Type Matters
- **Revenue recognition** allegations are the most common: more than **one-third of accounting cases** filed in 2020 included improper revenue recognition allegations, nearly **double the proportion in 2019**.
- Allegations of **inappropriate revenue recognition** and **weaknesses in internal control** are the most common accounting issues, followed by **allegedly omitted disclosure of related-party transactions**.

### Counterintuitive Finding
- Although the number of accounting case settlements involving restatements increased, the **median settlement amount was 85% lower** than in cases not involving a restatement. This may be because restatement cases are sometimes clearer-cut (facilitating quicker, lower-amount settlements) or involve smaller companies.

### Early Disclosure Effect
- Early voluntary disclosure of a restatement **weakens the claim** that managers acted improperly by failing to disclose, thus lowering the probability of a lawsuit and potential damages.

### Data Sources
- Audit Analytics restatement database
- Cornerstone Research "Accounting Class Action Filings and Settlements" annual reports
- SEC EDGAR (restatements filed as 10-K/A, 10-Q/A, or 8-K)

## 3. SEC Investigations and Wells Notices

### Empirical Findings
- **80% of recipients** of Wells notices from 2011-2013 ended up facing SEC charges for allegedly violating securities law.
- Wells submissions are **discoverable** by private litigants -- a well-placed subpoena can obtain a copy, to be used in later civil litigation.
- Courts have held that Wells submissions are **not protected by settlement-privilege rules**.
- In FY 2024, the SEC filed **583 total enforcement actions** and obtained **$8.2 billion in financial remedies** -- the highest amount in SEC history.

### Connection to Private Litigation
- Cases with a **corresponding SEC action** have historically settled for **"substantially" higher amounts** than those without SEC involvement.
- SEC enforcement actions facilitate leveraging cooperation of a defendant during both investigation and litigation of a parallel case.
- The "piggyback" effect: entities targeted by SEC enforcement often face **parallel private class actions** based on the same underlying conduct.
- In 2023, the median settlement for cases with at least one accompanying derivative action was **$21 million, 40% higher** than in 2022.

### Data Sources
- SEC enforcement action database (sec.gov)
- SEC annual enforcement reports
- Stanford Law School FCPA Clearinghouse (for FCPA-specific)

## 4. Earnings Misses vs. Guidance

### Empirical Findings
- Missed earnings guidance allegations have reached a **five-year high**: **43% of filings** in 2025 included allegations related to missed earnings guidance.
- The trend has been steadily increasing:
  - 2022: **23%** of cases involved missed earnings guidance allegations
  - 2023: **31%** of cases
  - 2024: **41%** of cases
  - 2025: **43%** of cases (five-year high)
- No single threshold percentage of earnings miss that triggers litigation, but the decision to file depends on: (a) magnitude of the miss; (b) whether prior guidance was specific and affirmative; (c) whether insiders sold stock before the miss; (d) whether there were contemporaneous negative events (restatement, investigation, executive departure).

### Key Risk Indicators for Underwriting
- Companies that provide specific numerical guidance face higher litigation risk than those providing ranges or qualitative guidance.
- The gap between guidance and actual results, expressed as a percentage of the guidance midpoint, is a key metric.
- A markedly **higher percentage of cases are settling above the $50 million threshold**, indicating increasing stakes.

### Data Sources
- IBES (Institutional Brokers' Estimate System) for consensus estimates
- Company 8-K filings for guidance
- Bloomberg/Refinitiv earnings surprise data

## 5. Executive Departures

### Empirical Findings
- Prior studies document **increased CEO and director departures following allegations of securities violations**, and securities lawsuits are followed by increased turnover of executive officers and directors.
- **CFO turnover and bonus compensation are affected by restatements, but only when the restatement firm is the target of a class-action suit.** When expanded to other executives (CEOs, COOs), turnover only occurs in the presence of litigation.
- **SEC-sanctioned CEOs and CFOs experience a higher probability of turnover**, with this effect more prominent in the **post-SOX period**.
- CFOs tend to **leave office within 6-12 months** of a CEO's departure.
- **Forced (voluntary) CEO turnover has a higher significant marginal effect on forced (voluntary) CFO turnover.**

### Underwriting Signal
- Sudden, unexplained departures of CFOs, CAOs (Chief Accounting Officers), or General Counsels are among the strongest leading indicators of forthcoming bad news and litigation risk.
- Departure of multiple C-suite officers in close succession is an extreme red flag.
- Directors experience an increase in withheld votes nearly **20% above the sample mean** following interlocked executive departures -- larger than the increase from financial restatements, securities litigation, or poison pill adoptions.

### Data Sources
- SEC Form 8-K Item 5.02 (departure/appointment of officers)
- ExecuComp database
- BoardEx director tracking

## 6. Auditor Changes

### Empirical Findings
- **Mid-year auditor switches ("LateSwitches")** are significantly riskier regarding litigation risk, audit risk, and business risk.
- Stock market returns following an auditor change are **more negative for mid-year switches**.
- Mid-year switches have a **higher chance of announcing restatements and receiving going concern opinions** in the first year of audit with the successor auditor.
- **Big 4 auditor resignations reveal private information** about future audit risk and future business risk.
- **79% of Tier 1 firms** and Fortune 1000 public companies are concerned that changing public accounting firms increases the risk of an audit failure in the initial years.
- Big Four audits are associated with a **lower probability of auditor change**, and when such changes do occur, they carry elevated risk signals.

### Key Risk Indicators for Underwriting
- Mid-year auditor switch (especially during Q3-Q4 of fiscal year)
- Switch from Big 4 to non-Big 4
- Auditor resignation (vs. mutual non-renewal)
- Multiple auditor changes in a short period
- Disagreements noted in the 8-K disclosing the change

### Data Sources
- SEC Form 8-K Item 4.01 (changes in registrant's certifying accountant)
- PCAOB audit firm inspection reports
- Audit Analytics

## 7. Insider Selling Patterns

### Empirical Findings
- **Strong correlation between insider selling and securities fraud litigation**: If the estimate of litigation likelihood increases by 10 percentage points, the dollar value of net sales **decreases by over $24 million**. A 10 percentage point increase in SEC enforcement probability is associated with a decrease in insider net selling of **over $19 million**.
- Academic studies estimate a **70% increase in the probability of an insider sale** following the use of certain buzzwords in public company filings, with these sales often preceding significant stock price declines and correlating with future accounting restatements.
- Courts evaluate whether stock sales constitute insider trading **more often in the context of securities fraud litigation** than in stand-alone insider trading cases.
- Plaintiffs can satisfy the Rule 10b-5 element of **scienter** by pointing to insider sales around the time a material misrepresentation inflated the company's stock.
- Historical reference: In the Enron case, the government contended CEO Jeffrey Skilling grossed nearly **$63 million** from stock sales while holding MNPI about the company's fraud.

### Key Risk Indicators for Underwriting
- Unusual concentration of insider sales before negative announcements
- Large insider sales outside of 10b5-1 plan parameters
- Adoption/termination/modification of 10b5-1 plans close to material events
- Insider selling volume exceeding historical patterns
- Multiple executives selling simultaneously

### Data Sources
- SEC Forms 3, 4, and 5 (insider ownership and transactions)
- SEC Form 144 (notice of proposed sale)
- InsiderScore / Washington Service

## 8. Mergers & Acquisitions

### Empirical Findings
- **82% of public company merger transactions valued over $100 million** drew at least one lawsuit in 2018 (consistent with 2017).
- For deals valued over $500 million, **96% of target firms** reported deal-related litigation, with an average of **5.4 lawsuits per deal**.
- About **12% of M&A offers announced in 1999-2000** led to litigation, but rates have increased dramatically.
- Larger offers, hostile offers, offers with target termination fee provisions, offers with higher cash financing, and controlling shareholder squeeze-outs are associated with **significantly more litigation**.
- **"Bump-Up Claims"** (alleging inadequate deal consideration) are the dominant claim type and settle for multimillion-dollar payments.
- Deals involving target lawsuits have **lower completion rates but higher takeover premiums** if completed.
- Bidder and target lawsuits have **significantly lower settlement rates** than other types of lawsuits.

### Key Risk Indicators for Underwriting
- Deal size (larger = more litigation)
- Deal structure (cash vs. stock; hostile vs. friendly)
- Deal premium relative to unaffected share price
- Presence of conflicts of interest (financial advisor also lending)
- Go-shop provisions and competing bids
- Controlling shareholder involvement

### Data Sources
- SEC Schedule 14D-9, SC 13D, SC TO filings
- Merger market databases (Dealogic, Bloomberg M&A)
- Woodruff Sawyer M&A litigation tracking

## 9. IPO/SPO Timing -- The "IPO Litigation Window"

### Empirical Findings
- Of IPO companies that were sued, **33% were sued within the first year** of going public.
- **90% of sued IPO companies were sued within the first five years**.
- Approximately **20% of companies that went public in the past decade** faced a securities class action suit.
- The ratio of litigation to IPO filings increased from **10-16% (2010-2016)** to **18% (2017)** and projected to rise to **21% (2018-2019)**.
- In 2024, nine of 222 federal court securities suit filings (approximately **4%** of all filings) involved IPO companies.
- Sections 11 and 12 of the Securities Act authorize suits based on misstatements/omissions related to public offerings, with a **three-year statute of limitations**.

### Key Risk Indicators for Underwriting
- First 12-36 months post-IPO = highest risk window
- Lock-up expiration periods
- Pre-IPO revenue/profitability claims
- Complexity of business model described in S-1
- Quality and reputation of underwriters
- Post-IPO stock performance relative to offering price

### Data Sources
- SEC EDGAR (S-1 and S-1/A filings)
- Stanford Securities Class Action Clearinghouse IPO data
- Woodruff Sawyer IPO litigation tracker

## 10. SPAC Transactions and De-SPAC Litigation

### Empirical Findings
- **17% of de-SPACs were sued in 2024**, compared to approximately **13% of new traditional IPO companies** and about **3% of mature public companies**.
- SPAC-related securities class actions by year:
  - 2021: 33 cases
  - 2022: 24 cases
  - 2023: 27 cases
  - 2024: 12 cases (declining as SPAC volume declined)
- De-SPAC volumes declined dramatically: 199 in 2021, 102 in 2022, 35 as of mid-2023.
- 15 SPAC-related settlements in 2024 totaled **$305.5 million combined**.
- **Significant shift toward Delaware breach of fiduciary duty suits** (direct action) rather than traditional securities class actions, encouraged by Delaware courts' negative view of SPACs.

### Key Risk Indicators for Underwriting
- SPAC sponsor's track record and conflicts of interest
- Target company financial projections included in proxy
- Earnout provisions and their achievability
- PIPE (Private Investment in Public Equity) investor participation
- Post-merger stock performance
- Sponsor promote structure

### Data Sources
- SEC EDGAR (S-1, proxy statements for de-SPAC)

## 11. Bankruptcy or Financial Distress

### Empirical Findings
- Once a company is **insolvent**, many jurisdictions hold that directors owe **creditors a fiduciary duty** to maintain corporate assets.
- In the **"zone of insolvency"**, duties of care broaden -- directors owe fiduciary duties to creditors as well as shareholders, even if the company is not actually in bankruptcy.
- Potential claims include **mismanagement that deepens insolvency**, fraudulent trading and transfers, and other fiduciary, tort, and statutory violations.
- The U.S. Supreme Court struck down nonconsensual third-party releases in Chapter 11 reorganization plans (affecting mass tort situations like opioids), causing directors to reevaluate personal liability exposure.
- D&O policies may contain **bankruptcy or insolvency exclusions** that bar coverage during bankruptcy -- a critical coverage gap.

### Key Risk Indicators for Underwriting
- Altman Z-score below 1.81 (distress zone)
- Quick ratio below 1.0
- Negative working capital trends
- Going concern opinion from auditor
- Covenant violations on debt
- Material uncertainty about going concern in annual report

### Data Sources
- Bloomberg/Refinitiv financial data
- Moody's EDF-X Early Warning System (bankruptcy probability)
- SEC filings (going concern disclosures)
- Court filings (Chapter 7, 11, 15 petitions)

## 12. Regulatory Investigations (FDA, EPA, DOJ, State AGs)

### Empirical Findings
- In 2024, the technology, pharmaceutical and medical device sectors accounted for a combined **37% of securities class action filings**, many triggered by regulatory actions.
- FDA-related securities fraud cases commonly involve: misleading investors about FDA submission timelines, concealing FDA concerns about clinical trial safety/efficacy, and misrepresenting the likelihood of FDA approval.
- Notable examples: AVEO Pharmaceuticals (concealing FDA's recommendation for a second clinical trial), CytoDyn (false statements about leronlimab's FDA status).
- State attorney general actions (especially coordinated multi-state AG investigations) increasingly trigger D&O claims.

### Key Risk Indicators for Underwriting
- FDA Complete Response Letters (CRLs)
- Clinical hold orders
- FDA Warning Letters
- Consent decrees with regulatory agencies
- DOJ/State AG subpoenas
- Import alerts or product seizures

### Data Sources
- FDA MAUDE database (medical device adverse events)
- FDA drug approval databases
- DOJ press releases
- State AG enforcement databases

## 13. Cybersecurity Breaches and Data Privacy

### Empirical Findings
- **Over 1,300 data privacy lawsuits** were filed in 2023, more than **double** 2022 and **four times** 2021.
- Large cyber claims (>EUR 1 million) frequency was **up 14% in H1 2024**, with severity increasing **17%**, following a 41% increase in frequency in 2023.
- The **top 10 data breach class action settlements in 2023 totaled $516 million**, up significantly from $350 million in 2022.
- D&Os face allegations of failing to implement adequate cybersecurity measures or appropriately disclose cyber risks.
- SEC cybersecurity disclosure rules (adopted March 2024, then stayed) require material incident reporting within 4 business days on Form 8-K.

### Key Risk Indicators for Underwriting
- History of data breaches
- Volume and sensitivity of data stored/processed
- Cybersecurity budget as % of IT spend
- CISO reporting structure (reports to CEO/Board vs. buried in IT)
- Third-party vendor risk management program
- Compliance with applicable frameworks (NIST, SOC 2, ISO 27001)
- Industry sector (healthcare, financial services = highest exposure)

## 14. Product Liability Events Escalating to D&O Claims

### Empirical Findings
- Product liability crises frequently cascade into D&O claims through two mechanisms: (1) securities fraud allegations (company knew of product defects and concealed them from investors); (2) breach of fiduciary duty (board failed to oversee product safety -- Caremark claims).
- **Opioid litigation** example: Rite Aid and Walmart faced securities class actions after DOJ filings -- total opioid settlements across the industry exceeded **$21 billion** from distributors alone.
- Pharmaceutical and medical device sectors account for a disproportionate share of product-related D&O claims.
- Boeing faced Caremark claims for board's alleged failure to oversee safety culture.

### Key Risk Indicators for Underwriting
- Product recall history
- FDA warning letters / consent decrees
- Consumer Product Safety Commission actions
- NHTSA investigations (automotive)
- Volume and trend of product liability litigation
- Internal safety reporting mechanisms

## 15. Whistleblower Complaints

### Empirical Findings
- SEC received **45,130 tips, complaints, and referrals in FY 2024** -- the most ever in one year, including **over 24,000 whistleblower tips**.
- SEC awarded **over $255 million to 47 whistleblowers** in FY 2024 (third-highest annual amount in program history).
- The SEC has awarded more than **$2.2 billion to 444 individual whistleblowers** since program inception.

### Key Risk Indicators for Underwriting
- Existence and independence of internal reporting hotline
- History of whistleblower complaints
- Anti-retaliation policies and enforcement
- Use of restrictive employment agreements that could impede reporting

## 16. Proxy Contests and Activist Investor Campaigns

### Empirical Findings
- Activists initiated **over 300 "high impact" activism campaigns** annually in 2022-2023, consistent with the prior decade's levels.
- There were **110 proxy fights in 2016 alone** -- a 43% surge over 2012.
- CEOs were **three times as likely** to be replaced within 12 months after an activist received a board seat compared to baseline.
- Even when activists **did not** receive a board seat, CEOs were **twice as likely** to be replaced within 12 months.
- Litigation occurs in **less than 5%** of activist campaigns, but when it does occur, it typically involves challenges to board actions during proxy fights.
- The introduction of the **universal proxy card** (2022) has facilitated activist campaigns.

### Key Risk Indicators for Underwriting
- 13D/13G filings showing activist accumulation (>5% ownership)
- History of activist targeting in the sector
- Company underperformance relative to peers
- Excessive executive compensation relative to performance
- Entrenched governance provisions (staggered board, poison pills)

### Data Sources
- SEC Schedule 13D/13G filings
- SharkRepellent / FactSet activism databases
- ISS / Glass Lewis proxy advisor reports

## 17. Related Party Transactions

### Empirical Findings
- Related party transaction allegations are among the **most common accounting issues** in securities class actions, alongside revenue recognition and internal control weaknesses.
- Self-dealing involves a conflict of interest where a fiduciary is a party to a transaction with itself or its affiliates.
- The SEC recently brought enforcement actions against **Lyft and Maximus** for related party transaction disclosure failures.
- Common red flags include: nonstandard or undocumented terms, pricing that differs materially from market conditions, transactions lacking clear business purpose, round-trip transactions, and unusually large balances.
- RPTs can trigger both **securities fraud claims** (failure to disclose) and **breach of fiduciary duty claims** (self-dealing).

### Key Risk Indicators for Underwriting
- Volume and frequency of RPTs disclosed in proxy statement
- RPTs involving CEO, controlling shareholders, or board members' affiliates
- RPTs at prices deviating from market
- Lack of independent RPT review committee
- History of SEC comments on RPT disclosures

### Data Sources
- Company proxy statements (Related Party Transactions section)
- SEC EDGAR comment letters
- Audit committee reports

## 18. Options Backdating / Compensation Irregularities

### Empirical Findings (Historical)
- The options backdating scandal (2006-2013) resulted in **39 federal securities class actions**, with **31 settling for a combined value of more than $2.38 billion** and 8 dismissed.
- Triggered by University of Iowa Professor Erik Lie's study showing an unusually large number of stock option grants to executives at stock-price lows.
- D&O policies covered many of these lawsuits under "wrongful acts" provisions.

### Current Relevance: Compensation Irregularities
- SEC adopted **clawback rules** in October 2022 requiring publicly traded companies to recoup erroneously awarded incentive-based compensation following accounting restatements, looking back over **three years**.
- D&O insurers **cannot indemnify** clawed-back executive compensation under the new rules.
- Non-compliance with clawback rules may trigger **fiduciary duty claims** against boards.
- Excessive compensation relative to performance remains a trigger for shareholder derivative suits.

### Key Risk Indicators for Underwriting
- CEO pay ratio relative to peers
- Percentage of compensation in stock options vs. restricted stock
- Existence and adequacy of clawback provisions
- History of compensation-related shareholder proposals
- "Say-on-pay" voting results (below 70% support = red flag)

### Data Sources
- DEF 14A proxy statements (compensation tables)
- ISS/Glass Lewis compensation analysis
- SEC enforcement actions database

## 19. Environmental Disasters and ESG Failures

### Empirical Findings

## 20. Antitrust Investigations

### Empirical Findings
- Follow-on securities litigation in the wake of antitrust enforcement actions is an **established phenomenon**.
- Examples include: poultry production companies hit with follow-on securities suits after price-fixing antitrust litigation; generic drug companies facing securities suits after DOJ criminal price-fixing charges; auto parts companies facing securities suits following DOJ and EU collusion investigations.
- Pattern: DOJ/EU antitrust investigation/indictment -> stock price drop -> securities class action alleging company concealed anticompetitive conduct from investors.
- Many of these follow-on cases have **not been particularly successful**, though they remain common enough to represent a material exposure.

### Key Risk Indicators for Underwriting
- Industry concentration (highly concentrated industries = higher antitrust risk)
- History of antitrust investigations in the sector
- Trade association participation
- Parallel pricing patterns
- CID (Civil Investigative Demand) or grand jury subpoena receipt

### Data Sources
- DOJ Antitrust Division press releases
- FTC enforcement actions
- EU Commission competition decisions

## 21. Foreign Corrupt Practices Act (FCPA) Violations

### Empirical Findings
- As of 2023, U.S. authorities were investigating at least **35 different entity groups** for possible FCPA violations.
- Example: Honeywell International agreed to pay **$202.7 million** to resolve parallel anti-corruption investigations by DOJ, SEC, and Brazilian prosecutors.
- FCPA enforcement routinely involves **parallel SEC and DOJ actions** against the same corporate defendant.
- **Money laundering** is the most prevalent "FCPA-related" charge alongside core FCPA violations.
- FCPA violations frequently trigger follow-on shareholder derivative suits and securities class actions alleging the company concealed bribery practices.

### Key Risk Indicators for Underwriting
- Revenue from countries ranked poorly on Transparency International Corruption Perceptions Index
- Operations in high-risk regions (Africa, Middle East, parts of Asia, Latin America)
- Use of third-party agents/consultants in government-facing operations
- Acquisition of companies with foreign government-facing operations
- Inadequacy of anti-corruption compliance programs

### Data Sources
- Stanford Law School FCPA Clearinghouse (fcpa.stanford.edu)
- DOJ/SEC FCPA enforcement action databases
- Transparency International CPI rankings

## 22. Failure to Maintain Internal Controls (SOX 404)

### Empirical Findings
- **Material weakness (MW) reports are strongly associated with the likelihood of subsequent financial statement fraud litigation**, with entity-level MW having a stronger influence than account-level MW (academic study).
- **Stock price declines on securities fraud litigation announcement dates are significantly more severe** for firms receiving SOX 404 internal control material weakness opinions.
- Entity-level material weaknesses are **more predictive of litigation** than account-level weaknesses.
- The number of **outside board directorships significantly increases** the influence of entity-level MW on litigation likelihood -- "board busyness" significantly increases litigation risk.
- If ineffective internal controls go undiscovered, they cannot be remediated, and unremediated issues can lead to **restatements that trigger litigation**, and evidence of unremediated issues can indicate **managerial negligence**.

### Key Risk Indicators for Underwriting
- Disclosure of material weakness in 10-K/10-Q
- Adverse ICFR (Internal Control over Financial Reporting) opinion from auditor
- Pattern of significant deficiencies (below material weakness)
- Rapid growth without corresponding investment in internal controls
- Recent acquisitions without integration of controls
- "Overloaded" board members serving on multiple audit committees

### Data Sources
- SEC EDGAR (management assessments of ICFR in 10-K)
- Audit Analytics material weakness database
- PCAOB inspection reports

## 23. Going Concern Opinions

### Empirical Findings
- Going concern opinions serve as a **public signal of severe financial distress**, often triggering a cascade of negative consequences including stock price declines, credit facility covenant violations, and D&O litigation.
- First-time going concern opinions are particularly significant as they represent a **step-change in risk assessment** by the auditor.
- Mid-year auditor switches often precede going concern opinions, creating a **compounding risk signal**.

### Key Risk Indicators for Underwriting
- History of going concern qualifications
- "Substantial doubt about ability to continue as a going concern" language
- Management plans to mitigate going concern uncertainty
- Auditor track record of issuing going concern opinions (some auditors are more conservative)

## 25. Late SEC Filings (NT Forms)

### Empirical Findings
- Late filers filing Form 12b-25 (NT form) experience an **abnormal return of -1.34%** in the three days surrounding the filing.
- The SEC has charged companies for **failing to disclose in Form NT filings** that their delayed filing was caused by an anticipated restatement or correction of prior financial reporting.
- **Careful drafting of Form NT disclosures is critical** because companies may face class action litigation filed by stockholders based upon Form NT disclosures.
- Late filings often signal deeper problems: restatement preparation, auditor disagreements, internal control failures, or pending investigations.

### Key Risk Indicators for Underwriting
- Filing of Form NT (12b-25)
- Repeated late filings
- Late filing combined with auditor change
- Reason given for the late filing (more concerning if vague or related to accounting issues)
- Whether the company remains a "current filer" for SEC purposes

### Data Sources
- SEC EDGAR (Form 12b-25 filings)
- Exchange compliance notifications (NYSE, Nasdaq)
- Audit Analytics filing timeliness data

# PART II: SEVERITY DRIVERS -- What Makes Settlements Bigger?

## 1. Market Capitalization at Time of Loss

### Empirical Findings
- **Market capitalization losses are the single most important factor** in explaining individual settlement amounts (Cornerstone Research).
- Investor plaintiffs' alleged market capitalization losses have grown **disproportionately**: while aggregate market cap of U.S. equities grew by **22.37%** from 2020-2024, annual aggregate market cap losses alleged against defendants grew by **51.68%** (from ~$438.5B to ~$665.2B).
- The median settlement in 2024 was **$14 million** (down 10% from 2023); the average was **$42.4 million** (down 13%).
- Overall recovery rate: The median settlement in 2024 was **7.3% of plaintiff-style damages**.
- For cases with plaintiff-style damages **under $25 million**, recovery was **28.2%** (highest since 2017).

---

## 2. Magnitude of Stock Price Decline

### Empirical Findings
- Disclosure Dollar Loss (DDL) is the primary proxy for claims severity: **$694 billion** in 2025.
- Maximum Dollar Loss (MDL): **$2,862 billion** in 2025 -- the third-highest level on record.
- Larger stock drops correlate with larger settlements, but the relationship is not linear.
- In H1 2025, DDL reached **$403 billion** (56% increase from H2 2024), while MDL surged to **$1.851 trillion** (154% increase from prior six months).

---

## 3. Accounting Irregularities vs. Forward-Looking Statement Claims

### Empirical Findings
- Cases involving accounting allegations and restatements have historically settled for **higher amounts** than those involving only forward-looking statement claims.
- Revenue recognition allegations are the most common and among the most damaging.
- However, the paradoxical finding that restatement cases settle for **lower median amounts** (85% lower) suggests that clear-cut accounting fraud cases may settle more quickly and efficiently.
- Forward-looking statement claims (Safe Harbor defense under PSLRA) face higher dismissal rates but can yield large settlements when they survive motions to dismiss.

---

## 4. Institutional Investor as Lead Plaintiff

### Empirical Findings
- The **PSLRA lead plaintiff provision** was designed to empower institutional investors to serve as lead plaintiffs and improve case quality.
- Public pension fund lead plaintiffs correlate with **higher class recoveries** as a fraction of potential damages.
- Median settlement with public pension fund plaintiff was **$21 million** in 2018, vs. **$6 million** without.
- **Important caveat**: Public pension funds may "cherry-pick" the strongest cases with the largest potential damages, making causation uncertain.

---

## 5. Public Pension Fund as Lead Plaintiff

### Additional Data
- Public pension funds served as lead plaintiffs in:
  - 46% of settled cases in 2012
  - 41% in 2016
  - 32% in 2017
  - Declining further by 2019 (lowest in 10 years)
- Private institutional lead plaintiffs are **not associated with larger class recoveries** despite expectations when the PSLRA was enacted.
- The declining involvement of public pension funds as lead plaintiffs has been identified as a factor in declining settlement amounts.

---

## 6. Length of Class Period

### Empirical Findings
- In 2019, the median class period length was **1.7 years** -- the longest over the last 10 years.
- Plaintiffs often **substantially expand the class period** between initial filing and settlement -- initial filings during 2015-2018 had class periods just under one year.
- "Simplified tiered damages" is **generally correlated with the length of the class period**.
- A claim with a **longer class period creates greater exposure** and requires significantly greater resources to defend (due to hourly billing structure).
- Defense costs are directly correlated with class period length, accelerating limits erosion.

---

## 7. Officer vs. Company Defendants

### Empirical Findings
- Individual officer defendants face personal liability risk that drives different settlement dynamics.
- **Side A coverage** (personal liability when company cannot indemnify) is the most critical coverage for individual D&Os.
- Cases naming individual officers as defendants tend to be more serious and involve stronger allegations of scienter.
- Officers facing personal liability may have stronger incentives to settle, potentially increasing settlement amounts.

---

## 8. Criminal Parallel Proceedings

### Empirical Findings
- Cases with parallel criminal proceedings (DOJ investigation/indictment) settle for **substantially higher amounts**.
- Respondents face "lifetime bars, multimillion-dollar fines, and follow-on civil litigation."
- Criminal proceedings provide plaintiffs with additional evidence and leverage in civil litigation.
- The Fifth Amendment creates a dilemma: defendants may refuse to testify in civil cases, which juries/judges may view negatively.

---

## 9. SEC Co-Enforcement

### Empirical Findings
- Cases with a **corresponding SEC action** have historically settled for **substantially higher amounts**.
- In 2023, the median settlement for cases with at least one accompanying derivative action was **$21 million**, **40% higher** than in 2022.
- SEC cooperation agreements create additional pressure on defendants.
- SEC-obtained discovery and findings can be used by private plaintiffs.

---

## 10. Industry Sector Effects

### Empirical Findings
- Technology and healthcare sectors combined account for **more than half of all filings**.
- Pharmaceutical and medical device sectors account for **37% of securities class action filings** (with tech).
- Financial institutions command approximately **30% of D&O insurance market** share; technology at **25%**; healthcare at **20%**.
- Life sciences and technology companies, particularly **mid-cap ($2B-$10B market cap)**, see the highest rates of D&O claims.

---

## 11. Presence of Confidential Witnesses

### Empirical Findings
- Confidential witnesses (typically former employees) provide specific factual allegations that strengthen securities fraud complaints.
- The presence of detailed confidential witness allegations makes it **harder for defendants to win motions to dismiss** under PSLRA heightened pleading standards.
- Cases with strong confidential witness testimony tend to survive to discovery and settlement.
- Different circuits apply different standards: the 1st and 9th Circuits permit **more general allegations** for pleading scienter based on internal documents, while the 2nd, 3rd, 5th, 7th, and 10th Circuits require **particularized allegations**.

---

## 12. Prior Litigation History

### Empirical Findings
- Securities class action litigations target approximately **5% of all S&P 500 companies** in an average year.
- Companies with prior litigation history face higher D&O insurance premiums and heightened scrutiny.
- Prior settlements, even without admission of wrongdoing, are considered in underwriting.
- Repeat litigation can signal systemic governance or compliance failures.

---

## 13. Insurance Tower Structure and Limits

### Empirical Findings
- D&O insurance is structured as a **tower of A/B/C sides**: Side A (personal liability), Side B (corporate indemnification), Side C (entity coverage, typically securities claims only for public companies).
- **Defense costs erode policy limits** in most D&O policies -- every dollar of defense expense means one less dollar for settlements.
- In the Collins & Aikman claim, aggregate defense expense consumed **$15 million in just nine months**.
- Self-insured retention (SIR) amounts affect claims frequency and severity.
- Escalating defense expense is an **increasingly important part of the limits adequacy equation**.
- Program structure is as important as total limits -- carrier quality, coverage terms, and erosion provisions matter significantly.

---

## 14. Jurisdiction Effects

### Empirical Findings
- The Second and Ninth Circuits accounted for **61% of filings** in recent years.
- Circuits differ significantly on key legal standards:
  - 1st and 9th Circuits: permit **more general allegations** for pleading scienter
  - 2nd, 3rd, 5th, 7th, 10th Circuits: require **particularized allegations**
  - 9th Circuit alone holds that **expert opinions suffice** for alleging falsity
  - 2nd and 5th Circuits: expert opinions can only **"bolster"** factual allegations
- Delaware courts have shown a **negative view of SPACs**, facilitating breach of fiduciary duty claims.
- Reincorporation trends (Delaware to Nevada/Texas) may reduce fiduciary duty exposure long-term.

---

## 15. Judge Assignment Effects

### Empirical Findings
- Judge assignment can materially affect case outcomes, particularly at the motion to dismiss stage.
- From 1997-2018, **43% of core federal class actions were dismissed**, 49% settled, 7% remained live, and <1% reached trial verdict.
- **Motions to dismiss were filed in 96%** of federal securities class actions from 2013-2022.
- Judge-specific dismissal rates vary significantly, though comprehensive published data is limited.
- 2013 federal securities class actions had a **57% dismissal rate**; 2016 filings had a **49% rate**.

---

## 16. Discovery Stage Reached

### Empirical Findings
- Roughly **42% of all settlements** occur before the pleading stage ends.
- For cases settling during discovery, the median time from denial of motion to dismiss to settlement was **14 months**.
- Cases that survive the motion to dismiss and proceed to discovery have significantly higher settlement values.
- Discovery creates exponential defense costs, increasing settlement pressure.

---

## 17. Time to Resolution

### Empirical Findings
- Average time to settlement: approximately **three years (1,196 days)**.
- Median time to settlement: approximately **under three years (1,068 days)**.
- Cases with plaintiff-style damages <$500M settled on average within **two years**.
- Cases with plaintiff-style damages >$500M settled on average in **3.5 years**.
- Longer duration = higher defense costs = more limits erosion.

---

## 18. Number of Related Lawsuits

### Empirical Findings
- Cases with accompanying derivative actions settle for **40% higher** median amounts.
- Multiple lawsuits (securities class action + derivative + SEC enforcement) create compounding pressure.
- Multidistrict litigation (MDL) consolidation can affect settlement dynamics.
- The "multiplier effect": parallel proceedings across multiple forums increase total defense costs and settlement pressure dramatically.

---

## 19. Media Attention / Public Outrage Factor

### Empirical Findings
- While not formally quantified in academic studies as a standalone settlement factor, media attention and public outrage demonstrably affect:
  - **Speed of filing** (high-profile events attract plaintiff firms within hours)
  - **Lead plaintiff competition** (more institutional investors compete to lead high-profile cases)
  - **Settlement dynamics** (companies prefer to settle to end negative publicity)
  - **Jury pool effects** in the rare cases that go to trial
- Social media amplification has accelerated the "public outrage" cycle significantly.
- Cases involving consumer-facing companies, environmental disasters, or public safety failures generate the most media attention.

---

# PART III: GOVERNANCE RISK FACTORS

## 1. Board Independence Ratios

### Empirical Findings
- A meta-analysis of **135 studies spanning 20+ countries** found that the board independence-corporate misconduct relationship is **generally negative** (more independence = less misconduct).
- The relationship varies by implementation: independence of the **whole board**, **audit committee**, or **separation of CEO/chair roles** all have different effect sizes.
- The negative relationship is **more strongly negative** in countries with less corruption.

---

## 2. Audit Committee Expertise

### Key Factors
- Financial expertise on the audit committee (as required by SOX) correlates with better financial reporting quality.
- "Overloaded" audit committee members (serving on multiple boards) **significantly increase litigation likelihood** when combined with material weaknesses.
- Audit committee independence and meeting frequency are key governance indicators.

---

## 3. CEO/Chair Duality

### Empirical Findings
- CEO duality has a **significant negative effect** on ESG performance (551 publicly listed firms in Southeast Asia study).
- Meta-analytic study from **297 studies across 32 countries**: board independence is positively related to CEO duality (more independent boards tolerate duality because they can exercise oversight).
- CEO duality leads to **greater risk-taking** because of over-reliance and reduced accountability.
- If the CEO is the chair, the board **cannot effectively hold the CEO accountable**.

---

## 4. Staggered vs. Annual Board Elections

### Empirical Findings
- Staggered boards are recognized as the **most potent takeover defense**.
- Critics argue they **entrench management** and limit accountability.
- No academic consensus on net effects on firm value -- some studies show positive effects, others negative.
- Shareholder activism has pushed firms to **drop staggered boards in favor of annual elections**.
- For D&O underwriting: staggered boards may signal management entrenchment and reduced accountability.

---

## 5. Poison Pills and Anti-Takeover Provisions

### Empirical Findings
- Poison pills in combination with staggered boards represent the strongest takeover defense.
- No consensus on real effects on firm performance.
- For D&O underwriting: excessive anti-takeover provisions may signal governance weakness and management entrenchment, potentially increasing fiduciary duty claims.

---

## 6. Executive Compensation Structure

### Empirical Findings
- SEC clawback rules (2022) require recoupment of erroneously awarded incentive compensation following accounting restatements (3-year lookback).
- D&O insurers **cannot indemnify** clawed-back compensation.
- Excessive compensation relative to performance is a frequent trigger for shareholder proposals and derivative suits.
- Say-on-pay votes below **70% support** are a significant governance red flag.

---

## 7. Clawback Provisions

### Empirical Findings
- SEC final rule requires all NYSE/Nasdaq-listed companies to have clawback policies.
- Companies without adequate clawback policies face potential delisting.
- Disagreements about clawback implementation may themselves generate D&O claims.
- The D&O insurance implications are significant -- clawed-back amounts are not insurable losses.

---

## 8. Stock Ownership Requirements

- Alignment between executive compensation and shareholder interests through mandatory stock ownership reduces agency risk.
- Low insider ownership may correlate with higher litigation risk (reduced alignment).
- Very high insider ownership may create entrenchment concerns.

---

## 9. D&O Insurance Program Structure

- The structure of D&O coverage itself can affect litigation behavior.
- **Higher D&O insurance coverage** is associated with more aggressive financial reporting and greater risk-taking (moral hazard).
- D&O insurance settlement amounts are strongly influenced by available policy limits.
- Research shows that D&O insurance limits are a key determinant of settlement size -- suggesting that **more insurance can attract more litigation**.

---

## 10. Bylaw Provisions (Forum Selection, Fee Shifting)

### Empirical Findings
- Delaware enacted legislation in 2015 **prohibiting fee-shifting bylaws** for stock corporations but **validating forum selection bylaws** requiring internal corporate claims to be brought in Delaware courts.
- Delaware Supreme Court upheld **federal forum selection provisions** requiring Securities Act of 1933 claims to be brought in federal court.
- The 7th Circuit ruled that Delaware forum selection clauses **cannot prevent** Securities Exchange Act of 1934 claims from being brought in federal court.
- For underwriting: forum selection provisions can materially reduce multi-forum litigation costs.

---

## 11. Board Diversity

### Empirical Findings
- Research finds that **female independent directors on boards reduce securities litigation risk** (Journal of Corporate Finance, 2021).
- Mechanisms: female directors increase **conditional accounting conservatism** and improve **CSR policies**, thereby reducing litigation risk.
- Firms with gender-diverse boards pay **less in gender discrimination litigation damages**.
- An exogenous reduction in shareholder litigation risk results in **11.44% less female board representation** -- suggesting companies proactively add women to boards to mitigate litigation risk.

---

## 12-15. Other Governance Factors

### Board Meeting Frequency
- More frequent board meetings (beyond the minimum) can signal either proactive governance or crisis management.
- Post-crisis increases in meeting frequency are common.

### Committee Structure
- Separate risk committee, compliance committee, and cybersecurity committee correlate with better risk oversight.
- Audit committee financial expertise is mandated by SOX.

### Shareholder Rights Provisions
- Dual-class share structures reduce shareholder accountability but may reduce short-term litigation risk.
- Majority voting provisions for director elections increase board accountability.
- Proxy access provisions enable shareholders to nominate directors more easily.

---

# PART IV: FINANCIAL HEALTH INDICATORS

## 1. Revenue Growth Volatility

### Empirical Findings
- Revenue volatility is a proxy for operational risk and potential earnings manipulation.
- Companies with highly volatile revenue streams face greater scrutiny when they miss expectations.
- Rapid growth companies face higher litigation risk because of the pressure to maintain growth rates.

---

## 2. Earnings Quality Metrics (Accruals / Cash Flow Divergence)

### Empirical Findings
- Academic research shows **significantly lower quality earnings (earnings overstatement) in the four quarters immediately prior to the sued quarter** in securities fraud class actions (Earnings quality and Rule 10b-5 securities class action lawsuits, ScienceDirect).
- **Accounting-based securities class action lawsuits target only firms with lower earnings quality.**
- When accrual and cash flow components of net income are estimated separately, **fraud firms' accruals are more persistent pre-fraud but less so post-fraud**.
- The SEC's own **Accounting Quality Model (AQM)** uses accruals-based metrics to identify companies with potential earnings manipulation for investigation.
- Firms with **higher D&O insurance coverage** exhibit **lower quality accruals** (moral hazard effect).

### Key Metrics for Underwriting
- Sloan Accrual Ratio (total accruals / average total assets)
- Cash flow from operations vs. net income divergence
- Days sales outstanding (DSO) trend
- Inventory turnover trends
- Deferred revenue patterns
- Change in working capital anomalies

---

## 3. Debt Leverage Ratios

### Empirical Findings
- Higher leverage increases financial distress risk, which correlates with zone-of-insolvency D&O exposure.
- Goodwill impairment write-downs reduce equity, increasing debt-to-equity and debt-to-assets ratios.
- Highly leveraged companies face greater pressure to meet earnings targets (to maintain covenant compliance), increasing earnings manipulation risk.

### Key Metrics
- Debt-to-equity ratio
- Interest coverage ratio
- Net debt / EBITDA
- Fixed charge coverage ratio

---

## 4. Goodwill Impairment Risk

### Empirical Findings
- Companies with significant goodwill should have strong equity, because goodwill impairment write-downs eat away equity without affecting cash flow.
- Impairment write-downs may trigger restatement allegations (was the goodwill value supportable when recorded?).
- Triggering events include: adverse economic changes, increased competition, changes in key personnel, declining cash flows, declining market values.

### Key Metrics
- Goodwill as a percentage of total assets
- Goodwill as a percentage of stockholders' equity
- History of goodwill impairments
- Acquisition activity and premium paid

---

## 5. Off-Balance Sheet Exposure

### Key Factors
- Traditional leverage ratios may miss significant off-balance-sheet obligations like operating leases, guarantees, contingent liabilities.
- Modern accounting standards (ASC 842) have addressed some lease-related issues.
- Variable interest entities (VIEs) and special purpose entities (SPEs) remain potential hidden exposures.
- Key metric: Ratio of off-balance sheet commitments to total assets.

---

## 6. Customer Concentration

### Empirical Findings
- A **one-standard-deviation increase** in customer-base concentration results in a **22.2% decrease** in corporate risk-taking (volatility of profitability).
- A supplier depending on major customers for at least 10% of annual revenues has a cost of equity that is **21.2 basis points higher**.
- Firms with concentrated customer bases are incentivized to **withhold bad news**, tend to adopt **income-increasing accounting methods**, manage earnings upward, issue **optimistically biased earnings forecasts**, and conceal **bad news regarding litigation loss contingencies**.
- Loss of a major customer can trigger catastrophic stock declines and resulting litigation.

### Key Metrics
- Revenue concentration (% from top 1, 3, 5 customers)
- Disclosed customer identity
- Contract duration and renewal terms

---

## 7. Supply Chain Concentration

### Empirical Findings
- Network centrality has a **significantly negative effect on stock price crash risk** (higher centrality = less crash risk).
- Higher network centrality reduces information asymmetry and improves investor sentiment.
- Concentrated supply chains create operational vulnerability that can cascade to financial reporting and litigation risk.

---

## 8. Foreign Operations Exposure

### Empirical Findings
- Companies with **foreign operations in tax havens** face higher D&O litigation risk (as priced by D&O insurers).
- D&O insurers focus on firms with **outbound tax haven activity** when pricing tax aggressiveness.
- Foreign exchange risk, political risk, and sanctions compliance risk all contribute to D&O exposure.
- Securities litigation risk for foreign companies listed in the U.S. is a studied phenomenon.

---

## 9. Tax Aggressiveness

### Empirical Findings
- D&O insurers view tax aggressiveness (measured by industry/size-adjusted cash effective tax rates) as **increasing tax-related litigation risk**.
- D&O insurers focus specifically on firms with **outbound tax haven activity**.
- More aggressive tax strategies may result in: higher future cash tax outflows, increased reputational costs, higher costs of capital, and decreased financial statement transparency.
- Widespread leaks about offshore entities and "sweetheart" tax deals may expose firms to **increased non-IRS tax-related litigation**.

---

## 10. Working Capital Trends

### Key Factors
- Deteriorating working capital trends signal potential financial distress.
- Sudden changes in DSO (days sales outstanding), DIO (days inventory outstanding), or DPO (days payable outstanding) may signal earnings manipulation.
- Channel stuffing and other revenue manipulation techniques manifest as working capital anomalies.

# PART VI: EMERGING AND EVOLVING RISK FACTORS (2024-2026)

## 1. Artificial Intelligence Claims
- **AI-related securities class actions doubled** from 2023 (7 cases) to 2024 (14 cases), with 12+ in H1 2025.
- "AI washing" is the primary claim theory -- companies accused of overstating or misrepresenting AI capabilities.
- The majority of AI-related cases have targeted **tech companies (33)**, followed by **services (10)** and **financial (4)**.
- CrowdStrike (32% stock drop after IT outage), Evolv Technologies, Innodata are notable examples.

## 2. Delaware Reincorporation Debate
- Companies are openly debating whether Delaware remains the best state of incorporation.
- Nevada and Texas offer potentially more business-friendly alternatives with less fiduciary duty exposure.
- Long-term implications: fewer fiduciary duty suits and reduced demand for dedicated Side A coverage.

## 3. DEI Backlash Litigation
- Growing anti-DEI litigation targeting companies with diversity programs.
- Both pro-DEI and anti-DEI litigation creates D&O exposure.
- ESG disclosure scrutiny continues to intensify.

## 4. Tariff and Trade Policy Risk (2025-2026)
- Trade policy uncertainty creates disclosure obligations.
- Supply chain disruption from tariffs may trigger securities claims.
- Companies must accurately disclose tariff impacts on financial performance.

## 5. Caremark Duty of Oversight Expansion
- While historically rare, successful Caremark claims have **increased in recent years**.
- Success rate estimated at approximately **30%** (up from near-zero historically).
- Recent cases: Wells Fargo, Boeing, Regions Financial Corporation.
- California courts have now **adopted the Caremark standard**, expanding it beyond Delaware.
