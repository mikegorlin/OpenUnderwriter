# D&O Inherent Risk Baseline: Building the Actuarial Starting Point

## Purpose

This document establishes the actuarial foundation for D&O liability underwriting: the **inherent risk baseline**. This is the expected claim frequency and severity based purely on objective company characteristics -- BEFORE examining any company-specific behavior, governance quality, or red flags.

The core question: **"A company with these characteristics has X% annual litigation probability and an expected loss severity in the $Y-$Z range."**

---

## PART 1: FREQUENCY DRIVERS

### What Predicts Whether a Company Gets Sued?

The overall base rate for U.S. exchange-listed public companies being named in a securities class action is approximately **3.9% per year** (2024), up from 3.2% in 2023, against a 2010-2023 historical average of approximately 3.6%.

Sources: Cornerstone Research, "Securities Class Action Filings -- 2024 Year in Review" (January 2025); NERA Economic Consulting, "Recent Trends in Securities Class Action Litigation: 2024 Full-Year Review" (January 2025).

---

### 1. Market Capitalization Tier

Market cap is the single most important frequency driver. Larger companies are sued at significantly higher rates than smaller ones, primarily because they have more shareholders, more analyst coverage, more trading volume, and larger potential damages pools -- all of which attract plaintiff attorneys.

**Annual Securities Class Action Filing Rates by Market Cap Tier (Estimated)**

| Market Cap Tier | Definition | Estimated Annual Filing Rate | Source Basis |
|---|---|---|---|
| Mega Cap | >$200B | ~8-10% | Extrapolated from S&P 500 rate of 6.1% in 2024; mega caps disproportionately targeted |
| Large Cap | $10B-$200B | ~5-7% | Cornerstone: large-cap filings "remained steady" with "highest increase over the 10-year average, with a notable gain of five percentage points" vs. other tiers |
| Mid Cap | $2B-$10B | ~3-5% | Near overall average; core filing rate of 3.9% (2024) |
| Small Cap | $300M-$2B | ~2-4% | Below average; smaller damages pool reduces plaintiff attorney incentive |
| Micro Cap | <$300M | ~1-2% | Lowest rate; insufficient damages to justify litigation economics |

**Key Statistics:**
- S&P 500 companies (overwhelmingly large/mega cap): **6.1%** annual filing rate in 2024, down from 7.1% in 2023. Historical average: **5.3%** per year (Cornerstone Research, 2024 Year in Review).
- Overall U.S. exchange-listed companies: **3.9%** in 2024 (Cornerstone Research).
- NERA reports 2024 litigation rate of **4.19%**, up from 4.01% in 2023, 3.38% in 2022 (NERA, 2024 Full-Year Review).
- Large-cap companies (>$10B market cap) saw the "highest increase over the 10-year average, with a notable gain of five percentage points" in filing share (Woodruff Sawyer, 2024 Year-End Recap).

**Why market cap matters for frequency:** The litigation economics of securities class actions create a natural size filter. Plaintiff attorneys working on contingency need the *potential damages pool* (measured by Disclosure Dollar Loss or Maximum Dollar Loss) to justify the cost of litigation. A micro-cap company with a $50M DDL will rarely attract plaintiff interest; a mega-cap with a $5B DDL almost certainly will.

### 3. Stock Exchange

**NYSE vs. NASDAQ vs. OTC**

There is no robust publicly available data isolating filing rates by exchange after controlling for market cap and industry. However, the following structural differences are relevant:

- **NASDAQ** lists significantly more technology and biotech companies, which have higher inherent filing rates. Approximately 3,600+ companies are listed on NASDAQ vs. ~2,400 on NYSE.
- **NYSE** lists larger companies on average (70% of S&P 500 companies are NYSE-listed as of 2025), so per-company filing rates may appear higher due to the market-cap effect.
- **OTC** companies rarely attract securities class actions due to minimal institutional ownership, low trading volume, and small damages pools.

**Practical Implication:** Exchange listing is primarily a proxy for market cap and industry composition rather than an independent risk factor. After controlling for those variables, the exchange itself adds little predictive power.

Source: Statista, NYSE and Nasdaq listed company comparison (2023-2025); general market structure data.

### 4. Years Public / IPO Recency

Recently public companies face **dramatically elevated** litigation risk.

**Key Statistics:**
- **14% of IPOs** (2010-2019) became targets of securities class action litigation within the three-year statute of limitations (Stanford Securities Litigation Analytics, Guest Post: IPO Litigation Risk, June 2021).
- **21% projected rate** for 2019 IPOs based on historical filing patterns (Stanford SLA).
- Upward trend from **10% in 2012 to 21% in 2019** (Stanford SLA).
- **74% of IPO lawsuits** occur within five quarters post-IPO (Stanford SLA).
- **16% of all suits filed in H1 2024** were against companies that went public in the prior three years (Woodruff Sawyer, 2024 Year-End Recap).
- IPO companies experiencing a 20% stock decline: **18% face lawsuits** vs. **13% for mature companies** with similar stock drops (Stanford SLA).
- Median IPO settlement: **$8 million** with 16% recovery vs. **$5.5 million** with 12.6% recovery for mature companies (Stanford SLA).
- IPO cases are dismissed at a **39% rate** vs. **56% for Section 10(b) cases** against mature companies (Stanford SLA), meaning IPO cases survive motions to dismiss more frequently.

**Practical Implication:** Companies within 3 years of IPO should receive a **3-5x multiplier** on the base filing rate. An IPO company in the tech sector could face a **15-25% annual probability** of litigation in the first three years.

### 5. Incorporation State

**Delaware vs. Other States**

Delaware's role is primarily relevant for **derivative suits** (breach of fiduciary duty) rather than federal securities class actions (10b-5).

- Approximately **66% of Fortune 500 companies** and over 50% of all publicly traded companies are incorporated in Delaware.
- Delaware Court of Chancery saw **4,741 fiduciary duty complaints** filed from 2004-2019 (approximately 296 per year), reduced to 2,958 unique complaints after removing duplicates (Vanderbilt Law Review, Cox & Thomas empirical study).
- Delaware's "demand futility" standard under *Zuckerberg* (2021) simplified rules for bringing derivative actions, potentially increasing derivative filing frequency.
- **Texas enacted competing legislation** in May 2025 creating business courts and minimum ownership requirements to bring derivative suits, which could reduce derivative frequency for Texas-incorporated companies.
- Forum selection bylaws adopted by many Delaware-incorporated companies (requiring derivative suits be filed in Delaware) consolidate but do not necessarily reduce litigation.

**Practical Implication:** Incorporation state primarily affects derivative suit exposure. For securities class actions filed under federal law, incorporation state is minimally predictive. For derivative exposure, Delaware incorporation may *slightly increase* risk due to plaintiff-friendly Chancery Court, though recent federal forum selection provisions have mitigated some effects.

Sources: Harvard Law School Forum on Corporate Governance (December 2021); Vanderbilt Law Review, Vol. 78 No. 2; Holland & Knight (March 2024).

### 6. Index Membership

**S&P 500 vs. Russell 2000 vs. Non-Index**

| Index | Annual Filing Rate | Source |
|---|---|---|
| S&P 500 | **6.1%** (2024); **5.3%** historical average | Cornerstone Research, 2024 Year in Review |
| Russell 2000 | **~2-3%** (estimated) | Extrapolated from overall rate minus large-cap concentration |
| Non-Index | **~1-2%** (estimated) | Lowest visibility, smallest damages pools |

**Why index membership matters:**
- Index membership is highly correlated with market cap (S&P 500 = large/mega cap; Russell 2000 = small cap).
- Index membership also drives **institutional ownership** (index funds must hold these stocks), which increases the pool of sophisticated plaintiffs.
- Index inclusion increases **analyst coverage** and media scrutiny.
- 6% of the 1,420 settlements over the last 10 years involved S&P 500 members (Cornerstone Research).

Source: Cornerstone Research, 2024 Year in Review; Stanford Securities Class Action Clearinghouse, Litigation Activity Indices.

### 7. ADR / Foreign Private Issuer Status

Foreign private issuers (FPIs) face **lower** litigation rates than domestic companies.

**Key Statistics:**
- **36 securities class actions** filed against non-U.S. issuers in 2024 (up from 33 in 2023), vs. peak of 88 in 2020 (Dechert, "2023 Developments in U.S. Securities Fraud Class Actions Against Non-U.S. Issuers").
- Foreign companies represent **25.9% of U.S.-listed companies** but only **16.8% of filings** in standard cases in 2024 -- a ten-year low (NERA, 2024 Full-Year Review).
- Implied FPI filing rate: approximately **2.7%** (vs. 4.19% overall), or roughly **0.65x the domestic rate**.

**Why FPIs are sued less frequently:**
- Lower U.S. institutional ownership
- Jurisdictional complexity (Morrison v. National Australia Bank limits extraterritorial application of 10b-5)
- Higher dismissal rates for foreign issuer cases
- Often smaller U.S. trading volumes

Source: NERA, 2024 Full-Year Review; Harvard Law School Forum on Corporate Governance (March 2020).

---

### 8. SPAC vs. Traditional IPO

SPACs have faced **extraordinarily high** litigation rates.

**Key Statistics:**
- **2021:** 199 closed de-SPAC transactions, 33 securities class actions = **~16.6% filing rate** (ABA, "SPAC Litigation by the Numbers," February 2023).
- **2022:** 102 de-SPACs, 24 securities class actions = **~23.5% filing rate** (ABA).
- SPAC-related filings in 2024: 11 (down from 27 in 2023), reflecting the collapse in new SPAC issuance (Cornerstone Research).
- **19% of 2024 settlements** involved SPACs (Cornerstone Research, Settlements 2024 Review).
- SPAC median settlement: **$12 million** vs. **$15.3 million** for non-SPAC cases in 2024 (Cornerstone Research).
- For comparison, traditional IPOs face approximately **14-21%** cumulative three-year filing rates (Stanford SLA).

**Practical Implication:** De-SPAC transactions carry the highest inherent litigation frequency of any category -- **approximately 5-6x the base rate** for established public companies. The filing rate of ~17-24% per de-SPAC transaction compares to ~3.9% for an average listed company.

Sources: American Bar Association, "SPAC Litigation by the Numbers" (February 2023); Woodruff Sawyer, "SPAC Litigation Numbers 2022"; Cornerstone Research, 2024 Year in Review.

### 9. Company Age / Maturity

Mature companies face lower filing rates, but the relationship is partly captured by IPO recency (Factor 4).

**Key Statistics:**
- Companies sued in 2024 had an **average age of 27 years** and **average market cap of $89 billion**, indicating a shift toward targeting mature, large companies (Woodruff Sawyer, 2024 Year-End Recap).
- However, companies within 3 years of going public represent **~16% of all filings** despite being a small fraction of listed companies (Woodruff Sawyer).
- The IPO filing rate of 14-21% over three years vs. the base rate of ~3.9% per year demonstrates that maturity reduces risk.

**Approximate Filing Rate by Company Age:**

| Company Age | Estimated Annual Filing Rate | Basis |
|---|---|---|
| 0-3 years public | **~7-10%** (annualized from 14-21% cumulative) | Stanford SLA |
| 3-10 years public | **~4-5%** | Transitional period |
| 10+ years public | **~3-4%** | At or below base rate |

### 10. Dual Class Stock Structure

**Limited direct data available.** Dual-class structures create theoretical litigation risk through:

- Misalignment between voting rights and economic ownership, enabling self-dealing (Harvard Law School Forum, August 2024).
- Controller opportunism: In *Ezcorp*, management controlled 100% of voting power with 5.5% of economic interest, leading to derivative litigation over "rubber-stamped" related-party transactions (SEC Investor Advisory Committee).
- Dual-class companies are **more likely to face derivative suits** (breach of fiduciary duty) but not necessarily more Section 10b-5 class actions.
- Dual-class structures may **reduce securities class action frequency** because controlling shareholders can resist short-term market pressures, potentially reducing stock volatility and earnings management.

**Practical Implication:** Dual-class structure is a **derivative suit risk enhancer** (+20-30% estimated adjustment to derivative frequency) but has an **ambiguous effect on securities class action frequency**. No definitive published rate differential exists.

Sources: Harvard Law School Forum on Corporate Governance (August 2024); SEC Investor Advisory Committee; Committee on Capital Markets Regulation, "The Rise of Dual Class Shares" (April 2020).

---

### 11. Revenue Size

Revenue is a secondary predictor of filing frequency, highly correlated with market cap.

- Revenue proxies for **operational complexity** -- more product lines, more regulatory touchpoints, more disclosure obligations.
- Companies with **revenue recognition issues** are disproportionately targeted: accounting-related filings had median pre-disclosure market cap of **$445.6 million** in 2024 (Cornerstone Research, Accounting Class Action Report 2024).
- Revenue size is embedded in Cornerstone Research's models through total assets (strongly correlated with revenue), which is a statistically significant predictor of settlement size.
- **No published filing rate breakdown by revenue tier exists** independent of market cap.

**Practical Implication:** Revenue is best used as a **secondary check** -- high revenue relative to market cap (low P/S ratio) may indicate a more mature business with lower volatility, while low revenue relative to market cap (high P/S ratio, e.g., biotech) signals higher filing risk.

---

### 12. Number of Employees

Employee count is an **indirect proxy** for operational complexity and regulatory exposure but is not a standard predictor in securities litigation models.

- More employees = more potential for **employment-related claims** that can become D&O matters (wage/hour class actions, discrimination suits, EEOC actions).
- More employees = greater **operational footprint** increasing regulatory surface area.
- **No published filing rate differential by employee count exists** for securities class actions specifically.

**Practical Implication:** Employee count is most relevant for **Side A/C D&O claims** (employment practices liability) and as a rough proxy for operational complexity. It adds minimal predictive power for securities class action frequency beyond what market cap and revenue already capture.

---

### 13. Geographic Concentration (US-Only vs. Multinational)

- Multinational operations increase regulatory complexity: **FCPA exposure**, multi-jurisdictional compliance risks, and currency-related disclosure issues.
- However, foreign-only companies (FPIs) face **lower** U.S. litigation rates (see Factor 7).
- U.S. domestic companies with **multinational operations** may face elevated risk due to additional disclosure obligations around foreign operations, transfer pricing, and geopolitical risks.
- **No definitive published rate differential** exists for domestic multinationals vs. domestic-only companies.

**Practical Implication:** Geographic diversification is a modest risk enhancer (+10-15% estimated adjustment) primarily through FCPA and regulatory exposure, but this effect is difficult to isolate from industry and size effects.

## PART 2: SEVERITY DRIVERS

### What Predicts How Big the Settlement Will Be?

The overall settlement landscape (2024): median **$14 million**, average **$42.4 million**, total aggregate **$3.7-$4.1 billion** across 88-93 settlements.

Sources: Cornerstone Research, "Securities Class Action Settlements -- 2024 Review and Analysis" (March 2025); NERA, 2024 Full-Year Review.

---

### 1. Market Capitalization at Time of Event (The #1 Predictor)

Market cap is the dominant severity driver because it determines:
- **The size of the potential damages pool** (more shareholders, more shares affected)
- **The resources available to fund settlements** (larger companies have deeper pockets and more insurance)
- **Plaintiff attorney interest** (higher potential contingency fee)

**Relationship:**
- Cornerstone Research regression analysis consistently finds that **issuer defendant total assets** (strongly correlated with market cap) is a statistically significant predictor of settlement amounts.
- In 2024, issuer defendants were **65% smaller** than in 2023 (by median total assets), reaching the lowest level since 2018, and the median settlement correspondingly declined from $15.4M to $14M (Cornerstone Research, 2024 Settlements Review).
- Accounting cases in 2024 had median pre-disclosure market cap of **$445.6 million** (the lowest in 10 years), and the median accounting case settlement shrank accordingly (Cornerstone Research).

**Settlement Size by Market Cap Tier (Estimated Ranges)**

| Market Cap at Time of Alleged Fraud | Median Settlement Range | Average Settlement Range |
|---|---|---|
| Mega Cap (>$200B) | $75-300M | $150-500M+ |
| Large Cap ($10B-$200B) | $25-75M | $50-200M |
| Mid Cap ($2B-$10B) | $10-30M | $20-75M |
| Small Cap ($300M-$2B) | $5-15M | $10-30M |
| Micro Cap (<$300M) | $2-8M | $3-15M |

*Note: These ranges are estimated from historical settlement distributions. Actual settlements vary enormously based on case-specific factors. The top 10 settlements typically account for ~60% of annual aggregate settlement value (NERA, 2024).*

---

### 2. Disclosure Dollar Loss (DDL)

DDL is Cornerstone Research's proprietary metric measuring the **market-cap change between the trading day before and after the end of the class period** -- essentially, the stock price decline attributable to the corrective disclosure.

**Key Statistics:**
- DDL Index rose **23% to $438 billion** in 2024, far above the historical annual average of **$237 billion** (Cornerstone Research, 2024 Year in Review).
- H1 2025 DDL reached **$403 billion** (56% increase over H2 2024), the highest semiannual total since 2022 H1 (Cornerstone Research).
- DDL is the **"most important factor"** in Cornerstone's regression model for predicting settlement amounts (Cornerstone Research, Settlement Analysis methodology).
- Higher DDL correlates with larger settlements, but the relationship is sub-linear: **larger cases settle for a smaller percentage of DDL**.

**DDL to Settlement Mapping (Approximate):**

| DDL Range | Typical Settlement as % of DDL | Implied Settlement Range |
|---|---|---|
| <$100M | ~15-30% | $2-30M |
| $100M-$500M | ~5-15% | $5-75M |
| $500M-$2B | ~3-8% | $15-160M |
| $2B-$10B | ~1-4% | $20-400M |
| >$10B | ~0.5-2% | $50M-$1B+ |

Source: Derived from Cornerstone Research, "Securities Class Action Settlements -- 2024 Review and Analysis" (settlement as percentage of plaintiff-style damages: overall median 7.3%; cases under $25M in damages: 28.2%).

---

### 3. Maximum Dollar Loss (MDL)

MDL measures the **peak-to-trough decline** in market capitalization during the class period -- the theoretical maximum investor loss.

**Key Statistics:**
- MDL Index: **$1.6 trillion** in 2024 (52% decline from $3.3 trillion in 2023) (Cornerstone Research).
- MDL is a statistically significant variable in Cornerstone's settlement regression model.
- MDL captures the full magnitude of potential investor losses over the entire class period, while DDL captures only the corrective disclosure decline.
- Filings with DDL >$5 billion or MDL >$10 billion are classified as "mega filings" (Cornerstone Research).

**MDL vs. DDL:** MDL is typically 3-10x larger than DDL because it captures the entire class period stock decline, not just the corrective disclosure. MDL provides an upper bound on potential damages; DDL provides a more realistic measure of fraud-related losses.

---

### 4. Industry Effects on Severity

Some industries settle at higher amounts due to larger average company size, more complex disclosure regimes, and more aggressive plaintiff bar targeting.

**2024 Settlement Data by Industry:**
- Technology sector: **$2 billion in settlements** in 2024; 6 of the top 10 settlements came from tech (Woodruff Sawyer, 2024 Year-End Recap).
- Total 2024 settlements: **$4.1 billion** (highest annual figure, excluding individual settlements exceeding $1B) (Woodruff Sawyer).
- The top 10 settlements accounted for approximately **60% of the annual aggregate** (NERA, 2024).

**Industry Severity Multipliers (Relative to Median):**

| Industry | Severity Multiplier | Reasoning |
|---|---|---|
| Technology | 1.5-2.5x | Largest market caps, highest DDLs, most active plaintiff bar |
| Biotech/Pharma | 1.0-2.0x | Binary events (FDA decisions), specialized damages |
| Financial Services | 1.2-1.8x | Complex disclosure, regulatory overlay, accounting complexity |
| Consumer/Retail | 0.8-1.2x | Near average |
| Industrial | 0.6-1.0x | Below average severity |
| Energy | 0.7-1.0x | Below average severity |

### 5. Institutional Ownership Concentration

Higher institutional ownership correlates with more sophisticated lead plaintiffs and larger settlements.

**Key Statistics:**
- Institutional investors served as lead plaintiffs in only **39% of 2024 settlements** -- the lowest rate in two decades (Cornerstone Research, 2024 Settlements Review).
- Institutional investor involvement has "historically been associated with cases with larger settlements" (Cornerstone Research).
- Cases with accompanying derivative actions showed **47% higher** median plaintiff-style damages (Cornerstone Research, 2024).

**Mechanism:** Institutional investors (mutual funds, pension funds, hedge funds) have:
- Larger losses per investor, giving them standing as lead plaintiff under PSLRA
- Resources to hire sophisticated counsel
- Incentive to maximize recovery
- Greater ability to provide company-specific knowledge to counsel

---

### 6. Public Pension Fund as Lead Plaintiff

The single most impactful severity factor after damages size.

**Key Statistics:**
- Median settlement with public pension fund as lead plaintiff: **$21 million** (Cornerstone Research, 2018 Settlements Review).
- Median settlement without public pension fund: **$6 million** (Cornerstone Research, 2018 Settlements Review).
- This represents a **3.5x multiplier** when a public pension fund serves as lead plaintiff.
- Public pension plans have been the **most frequent type of institutional lead plaintiff** since passage of the PSLRA in 1995 (Cornerstone Research).
- However, public pension plan participation has **declined**: from 46% of cases in 2012 to 27% in 2019 (Cornerstone Research, 2019 Settlements Review), and institutional lead plaintiff participation reached only 39% in 2024.

**Why pension funds drive higher settlements:**
- Larger losses (they hold bigger positions)
- Fiduciary duty to their own beneficiaries to maximize recovery
- Political visibility creates accountability pressure
- More willing to fund aggressive litigation strategies
- Tend to select more experienced, aggressive plaintiff firms

Source: Cornerstone Research, "Securities Class Action Settlements -- 2018 Review and Analysis"; Cornerstone Research, 2019 and 2024 Settlements Reviews.

---

### 7. Number of Analysts Covering

More analyst coverage correlates with:
- Higher visibility and media scrutiny
- More available analyst reports that plaintiff attorneys can use to establish "loss causation"
- Greater potential for analyst-driven class period events
- More pre-class-period optimistic projections to contrast with corrective disclosures

**No definitive published regression coefficient** exists for analyst coverage as an independent settlement predictor, but it is strongly correlated with market cap (which is a proven predictor). Analyst coverage primarily serves as a risk factor for **filing probability** rather than settlement size.

**Practical Implication:** Companies with >15 analysts likely have 2-3x the filing probability of companies with <5 analysts, but this effect is largely captured by market cap.

---

### 8. Trading Volume

Trading volume directly affects the size of the plaintiff class and therefore potential damages.

**Key Statistics:**
- Cornerstone Research's "simplified tiered damages" proxy explicitly incorporates **reported trading volume** and **shares outstanding** (Cornerstone Research methodology description).
- Volume is adjusted using exchange-specific volume reduction assumptions (different for NYSE vs. NASDAQ) to avoid double-counting turnover.
- Higher volume = more shares traded during the class period = more potentially damaged shares = larger damages = larger settlement.

**Practical Implication:** Trading volume is embedded in damages calculations rather than serving as an independent predictor. Companies with average daily trading volume exceeding $50M have significantly larger potential damages pools than those trading $1M daily.

### 2. How Actuaries Currently Model D&O Inherent Risk

**The Frequency-Severity Framework:**

D&O pricing follows the standard actuarial frequency x severity = expected loss model, but with significant modifications for the unique characteristics of D&O claims.

**Step 1: Frequency Model**

```
Expected Annual Filing Probability = f(Market Cap, Industry, Years Public,
                                       Index Membership, IPO/SPAC status,
                                       FPI status)
```

The base rate (~3.9%) is adjusted by multiplicative factors:

| Factor | Adjustment |
|---|---|
| Market Cap: Mega (>$200B) | 2.0-2.5x |
| Market Cap: Large ($10B-$200B) | 1.3-1.8x |
| Market Cap: Mid ($2B-$10B) | 1.0x (base) |
| Market Cap: Small ($300M-$2B) | 0.6-0.8x |
| Market Cap: Micro (<$300M) | 0.3-0.5x |
| Industry: Tech/Biotech | 1.5-2.0x |
| Industry: Financial Services | 0.8-1.0x |
| Industry: Other | 0.5-0.8x |
| IPO within 3 years | 2.0-3.0x |
| SPAC de-merger | 3.0-5.0x |
| Foreign Private Issuer | 0.65x |

Sources: Derived from Cornerstone Research filing data, NERA litigation rates, Stanford SLA IPO study, ABA SPAC litigation study.

**Step 2: Severity Model**

```
Expected Settlement = g(DDL, MDL, Total Assets, Market Cap,
                        Lead Plaintiff Type, Accounting Allegations,
                        Derivative Overlay, Section 11 Claims)
```

Cornerstone Research's regression model uses these key variables:
- **Plaintiff-style damages** (the most important single predictor)
- **Maximum Dollar Loss** (peak-to-trough)
- **Total assets** of issuer defendant (proxy for settlement capacity)
- **Public pension fund** as lead plaintiff (+3.5x median settlement)
- **Accompanying derivative action** (+47% to median damages)
- **Accounting-related allegations** (typically higher settlements)

Source: Cornerstone Research, Settlement Analysis methodology; CAS (Casualty Actuarial Society) Forum, "D&O Reinsurance Pricing -- A Financial Market Approach" (2005).

**Step 3: Conditional Adjustment**

Not all filings result in settlements:
- **57% of cases are dismissed** before discovery (NERA, 2024).
- Motion to dismiss success rate: **61% granted in full**, 20% partially granted, 19% denied (NERA).
- Of cases that survive MTD, the vast majority settle (very few go to trial).
- Settlement-to-filing ratio: approximately **88 settlements out of ~217 resolutions** = ~41% settlement rate (2024) (Cornerstone Research).

Therefore: **Expected Loss = Filing Probability x Probability of Settlement (given filing) x Expected Settlement Amount (given settlement)**

```
Expected Loss = P(filing) x P(settlement|filing) x E[settlement|settlement]
             ≈ 3.9%     x  ~40%                  x  ~$42M (average) or ~$14M (median)
             ≈ 3.9%     x  40%                    x  $14M (median)
             ≈ $218,000 expected median loss per company per year
```

*Or using the average:*
```
             ≈ 3.9%     x  40%                    x  $42M (average)
             ≈ $655,000 expected average loss per company per year
```

*Note: These are illustrative calculations for the "average" company. Actual expected losses vary by orders of magnitude based on the frequency and severity factors above.*

**Step 4: Market Cap Exposure Adjustment**

The CAS Forum paper on D&O reinsurance pricing argues that **market capitalization is the appropriate exposure base**, similar to how payroll is the exposure base for workers' compensation:

```
Expected Loss Rate = Expected Loss / Market Capitalization
```

This normalizes across companies of different sizes and allows for rate-on-line comparisons.

Source: CAS Forum, "D&O Reinsurance Pricing -- A Financial Market Approach" (2005).

### 4. How to Present to an Underwriter

**Recommended Presentation Format:**

**A. Inherent Risk Score (Percentile Rank)**

Assign each company a composite inherent risk score based on its objective characteristics, then express this as a percentile rank against all public companies:

```
Inherent Risk Score = w1(Market Cap Factor) + w2(Industry Factor) +
                      w3(IPO/SPAC Factor) + w4(FPI Factor) +
                      w5(Index Factor)
```

Present as: *"This company's inherent risk profile places it at the Xth percentile of all public companies -- i.e., X% of companies have lower inherent D&O litigation risk."*

**B. Expected Frequency Band**

| Risk Tier | Annual Filing Probability | Description |
|---|---|---|
| Very Low | <1.5% | Micro-cap, low-risk industry, mature |
| Low | 1.5-3.5% | Below-average risk profile |
| Moderate | 3.5-6% | Average public company |
| Elevated | 6-10% | Large cap, high-risk industry |
| High | 10-15% | Mega cap tech/biotech, or recent IPO |
| Very High | >15% | SPAC de-merger, mega-cap tech IPO |

**C. Expected Severity Range**

If sued, present the range of likely outcomes:
- **25th percentile settlement** (favorable outcome): $X
- **Median settlement**: $Y
- **75th percentile settlement** (adverse outcome): $Z
- **Tail scenario** (mega-settlement, top 5%): $W

**D. Expected Annual Loss**

```
Expected Annual Loss = Filing Probability x Settlement Probability x Expected Settlement
```

Present alongside peer benchmarking: *"Companies with similar characteristics (industry, market cap, years public) have historically produced expected annual D&O losses of $X per year."*

**What We Can Be Confident About (High Reliability):**

- Overall annual filing rate: **~3.5-4.5%** for all public companies (25+ years of data, consistent reporting from Cornerstone, NERA, Stanford SCAC)
- S&P 500 filing rate: **~5-7%** per year (well-documented)
- Technology and healthcare dominate filings: **>50% of total** (consistent across all sources)
- Market cap is the primary predictor of both frequency and severity
- Median settlement: **$10-15M** range, average: **$30-50M** range (stable over 10+ years in inflation-adjusted terms)
- DDL is the strongest settlement predictor

**Moderate Confidence:**
- Filing rates by market cap tier (must be inferred from limited published breakdowns)
- Industry-specific filing rates (sector definitions vary across sources)
- IPO/SPAC elevated rates (clear directional effect, specific rates depend on vintage)

**Low Confidence / Limitations:**
- Filing rates by stock exchange (confounded by market cap and industry)
- Dual-class stock effect (limited empirical data)
- Revenue and employee count as independent predictors (too correlated with market cap)

### 6. Limitations of This Approach

1. **Inherent risk is necessary but insufficient.** The baseline captures "nature" but not "nurture." Company-specific factors -- governance quality, financial health, stock volatility, earnings management, insider trading patterns -- can dramatically alter the risk from the baseline. These are addressed in the behavioral risk overlay (separate analysis).

2. **Correlation between factors.** Market cap, index membership, analyst coverage, institutional ownership, and trading volume are all highly correlated. Double-counting these factors in a multiplicative model will overstate risk. A properly specified model should use multivariate regression, not independent multipliers.

3. **Survivorship and selection bias.** Published settlement data only includes cases that settled -- not the 57% that are dismissed. The expected loss calculation must account for the probability of dismissal.

4. **Temporal instability.** Filing rates fluctuate with market conditions, regulatory environment, and plaintiff bar economics. The 2024 rate of 3.9% may not persist; the 2017-2018 period saw rates above 8% (including M&A filings). Models should use multi-year averages rather than single-year snapshots.

5. **Mega-settlement concentration.** Settlement distributions are extremely right-skewed. The top 10 settlements regularly account for 50-60% of annual aggregate value. Mean-based models will overstate risk for most companies while understating tail risk.

6. **Defense costs are excluded.** Published settlement data does not include defense costs (typically $5-15M+ for a securities class action that goes through motion to dismiss), which can consume primary D&O layers even in dismissed cases.

7. **Private data advantage.** The most accurate inherent risk models use proprietary broker/carrier claims data unavailable publicly. Tools like WTW's D&O Quantified, Aon's proprietary models, and carrier loss databases contain granularity that cannot be replicated from public sources alone.

### 2. Loss Distribution Across Tower Layers

**Settlement Size Distribution (2024):**

| Settlement Range | % of All Settlements | Cumulative | Primary Tower Impact |
|---|---|---|---|
| <$5M | ~25% | 25% | Primary only (partial) |
| $5M-$10M | ~17% | 42% | Primary only or primary + first excess |

**Defense Costs Impact:**
- In addition to settlements, defense costs of $5-15M+ consume primary layers.
- A company with a $10M primary and $2M SIR may have only ~$7M of primary policy left for settlement after defense costs (D&O Diary).
- The median settlement ($14M) now regularly exceeds what remains in primary layers after defense costs, meaning **most settlements now burn into excess layers**.

### 3. Market Cap Tier and Layer Exposure

| Market Cap Tier | Most Exposed Layers | Reasoning |
|---|---|---|
| Micro (<$300M) | **Primary only** | Median settlement ~$3-8M; most claims fully contained in primary. Small tower means total limits may be insufficient for adverse outcomes. |
| Small ($300M-$2B) | **Primary + 1st excess** | Median settlement ~$8-15M; defense costs exhaust primary, settlement burns first excess. |
| Mid ($2B-$10B) | **Primary through 3rd excess** | Median settlement $15-30M; typical claims consume 2-3 layers. Mega-settlements can penetrate higher. |
| Large ($10B-$200B) | **Primary through 5th-8th excess** | Median settlement $35-75M; large-cap claims regularly penetrate mid-tower. Top-of-tower layers still rarely touched except in mega-settlements. |
| Mega (>$200B) | **Full tower at risk** | Median settlement $80M+; mega-settlements ($200M-$1B+) can exhaust entire tower. Even high excess layers face material burn risk. |

**"Burn Layer" Trend:**
Insurers report that severity creep is pushing claims into layers that were historically safe. Layers attaching above $40M are now considered "burn layers" for large-cap companies, meaning excess carriers that used to enjoy near-zero loss ratios are now exposed (industry market commentary, 2024-2025).

Source: D&O Diary; Insurance Business Magazine, "D&O in 2026" (2025); Aon market commentary.

### 4. Estimating Attachment Point Vulnerability from Public Data

**Framework for Assessment:**

For any given company, you can estimate which tower layers are most at risk using publicly available data:

**Step 1:** Estimate filing probability (from inherent risk matrix above).

**Step 2:** Estimate conditional settlement size distribution:
- Use company's market cap to estimate median and 75th/95th percentile settlements
- Adjust for industry and other severity factors

**Step 3:** Map settlement distribution to tower structure:
- Use industry benchmarks for typical tower size (from market cap tier)
- Identify which layers are in the "zone of likely attachment"

**Example Calculation:**

> *Company: $5B market cap, technology sector, 4 years public*
>
> **Filing probability:** ~8% (mid-cap base rate ~4% x tech multiplier ~2x)
>
> **If sued, settlement distribution (estimated):**
> - 25th percentile: $8M (primary only)
> - Median: $25M (burns into 2nd excess)
> - 75th percentile: $60M (burns into 4th-5th excess)
> - 95th percentile: $200M+ (potential tower exhaustion)
>
> **Assumed tower:** $75M total ($10M primary, 7 excess layers of $5-15M each)
>
> **Layer vulnerability:**
> - Primary ($0-$10M): ~60% probability of attachment (if filed and survives MTD)
> - 1st excess ($10-20M): ~45% probability of attachment
> - 2nd excess ($20-30M): ~35% probability of attachment
> - 3rd excess ($30-40M): ~25% probability of attachment
> - 4th excess ($40-50M): ~15% probability of attachment
> - 5th excess ($50-60M): ~10% probability of attachment
> - 6th-7th excess ($60-75M): ~5% probability of attachment

**Note:** These are illustrative only. Actual attachment probabilities require proprietary severity distributions calibrated to market cap tiers.
