# SECTION 1: WHY BIOTECH IS DIFFERENT

## 1.1 The Biotech Paradox

Biotech accounts for **17% of securities class actions** but only ~2% of public companies. This raw statistic drives most underwriters to heavily penalize or avoid the sector. That reaction is wrong.

**The reality:**
- Biotech dismissal rates run 56-80% vs. 43-46% market average
- Mean settlements are comparable to non-biotech ($9M vs. $8.9M for small caps)
- Probability-weighted annual exposure is only 16% higher than comparable non-biotech companies

**What this means:** The litigation that gets filed against biotechs largely doesn't stick. Courts have developed strong doctrines protecting companies from hindsight-based claims about inherently uncertain scientific outcomes.

## 1.2 Three Dynamics That Define Biotech Risk

### Dynamic 1: Binary Outcome Dependency

Unlike most industries where value deterioration is gradual, biotech value can evaporate in a single press release. A Phase 3 failure or FDA rejection can trigger 40-80% stock declines overnight.

**Underwriting implication:** You're not assessing general operational risk├втВмтАЭyou're assessing the likelihood and timing of specific trigger events.

### Dynamic 2: Judicial Protection of Scientific Uncertainty

Federal courts consistently hold that clinical trial outcomes are "inherently unknowable" and that optimism about uncertain outcomes doesn't constitute fraud. The PSLRA safe harbor for forward-looking statements provides strong protection for management statements about drug development.

**Key precedents:**
- Courts routinely dismiss claims based on failed trials where management expressed optimism
- "Puffery" doctrine protects general positive statements about pipeline prospects
- Scienter (intent to deceive) is extremely hard to prove for clinical outcomes

**Underwriting implication:** Pre-approval stage companies have built-in legal protection that mature commercial companies lack.

### Dynamic 3: Inverse Size-Risk Relationship

Contrary to every other industry, small biotechs carry lower probability-weighted exposure than large pharma when properly structured. This is because:

- Small biotechs are sued more often but dismissed more often
- Large caps face higher filing rates (6.1% for S&P 500 vs. 3.9% market)
- Large cap settlements are dramatically larger
- Proper retention structure shields insurers from dismissed case defense costs

**Underwriting implication:** Stop auto-penalizing small biotechs. With proper retention, they're often our best risks.

## 1.3 What Makes Biotech Litigation Different

| Factor | Standard D&O | Biotech D&O |
|--------|--------------|-------------|
| Claim trigger | Gradual disclosure of problems | Single binary event |
| Stock decline pattern | Progressive deterioration | Sudden, one-day collapse |
| Primary allegation | Misrepresentation of known facts | Over-optimism about unknowable outcomes |
| Dismissal rate | 43-46% | 56-80% |
| Discovery intensity | Extensive document review | Focus on what management knew and when |
| Settlement timing | 3-4 years | Often longer (defendants wait for dismissal) |

# SECTION 2: BIOTECH BUSINESS MODEL PRIMER

## 2.1 Development Stage Economics

Understanding where a biotech sits in its lifecycle is essential. Each stage has distinct risk characteristics.

### Pre-Clinical / Discovery
- **Business model:** Burning cash to advance research; no revenue
- **Investors:** VCs, early-stage specialists who understand binary risk
- **Litigation profile:** Rarely sued; too small for plaintiff interest
- **Key risk:** Running out of money before reaching value inflection

### Phase 1 / Phase 2
- **Business model:** Testing safety (P1) and initial efficacy (P2)
- **Investors:** Crossover funds, biotech specialists
- **Litigation profile:** Modest; high judicial protection for uncertain outcomes
- **Key risk:** Trial design flaws that only become apparent later

### Phase 3
- **Business model:** Large, expensive definitive trials; often multiple indications
- **Investors:** Broader institutional participation
- **Litigation profile:** ELEVATED around data readouts; but still high dismissal
- **Key risk:** Pivotal trial failure after significant capital deployment

### FDA Review / Pre-Approval
- **Business model:** Awaiting regulatory decision; often "binary event" focused
- **Investors:** Event-driven funds may participate
- **Litigation profile:** HIGHEST immediately post-CRL
- **Key risk:** Complete Response Letter triggers immediate lawsuits

### Early Commercial (0-2 Years Post-Approval)
- **Business model:** Transitioning from R&D to sales execution
- **Investors:** Growth and value crossover
- **Litigation profile:** Elevated; focus shifts to commercial execution
- **Key risk:** Launch performance vs. guidance; manufacturing issues

### Mature Commercial
- **Business model:** Revenue generation, lifecycle management
- **Investors:** Traditional pharma investors
- **Litigation profile:** Similar to other industries; lower dismissal rates
- **Key risk:** Financial reporting, pricing, competitive dynamics

## 2.2 Revenue Models

| Model | Description | D&O Implications |
|-------|-------------|------------------|
| **Pre-revenue** | No product sales; burning cash | Lower lawsuit interest; disclosure focused on pipeline |
| **Collaboration/License** | Milestones and royalties from partners | Revenue recognition complexity; partner dependency |
| **Single-product commercial** | One approved drug driving revenue | Extreme concentration; launch execution scrutiny |
| **Diversified commercial** | Multiple products | More traditional pharma profile; manufacturing/pricing risks |
| **Platform/Services** | Technology licensing, CRO/CDMO | Different risk profile; more predictable |

## 2.3 Cash and Runway

Biotech cash positions require different interpretation than other industries.

**Standard view:** Low cash = distress signal
**Biotech reality:** Cash position is measured against planned spending, not current operations

**Runway Calculation:**
```
Runway (months) = Cash & Equivalents ├Г┬╖ Quarterly Cash Burn
```

**Runway Interpretation:**

| Runway | Assessment | Implication |
|--------|------------|-------------|
| >30 months | Strong | Can weather setbacks; time to raise if needed |
| 24-30 months | Adequate | Comfortable but will need to raise |
| 18-24 months | Watchlist | Financing needed within policy period |
| 12-18 months | Concern | Distress indicators may emerge |
| <12 months | Elevated risk | May face down-round or dilutive terms |

**Important:** A biotech with 12 months runway but a major readout in 6 months may be fine├втВмтАЭpositive data typically enables financing. Context matters.

# SECTION 3: DISCOVERY FRAMEWORK

## 3.1 The Biotech Discovery Sequence

When a biotech submission arrives, follow this sequence to build understanding efficiently.

### Step 1: Situational Awareness (15 minutes)

**Goal:** Understand what this company does and where it sits in lifecycle

**Actions:**
- Read company description in 10-K Item 1
- Identify lead programs and development stages
- Note therapeutic areas and modalities
- Check market cap and recent stock performance

**Key questions answered:**
- What do they do?
- What stage are they?
- How big are they?
- Has something happened recently?

### Step 2: Pipeline Deep Dive (30 minutes)

**Goal:** Map the entire pipeline and identify value drivers

**Sources:**
- 10-K pipeline table
- Company investor presentation
- ClinicalTrials.gov

**Build this table:**

| Program | Indication | Phase | Anticipated Milestones | % of Value |
|---------|------------|-------|------------------------|------------|
| [Lead] | [Disease] | [X] | [Date/Event] | [Est. %] |
| ... | ... | ... | ... | ... |

**Critical:** Identify what % of company value depends on near-term binary events.

### Step 3: Binary Event Calendar (15 minutes)

**Goal:** Map all potential lawsuit triggers in next 18 months

**Sources:**
- Earnings call transcripts (management guidance on timing)
- FDA PDUFA calendar
- ClinicalTrials.gov expected completion dates
- Analyst reports

**Build this calendar:**

| Event | Expected Date | Stock Impact if Negative | Lawsuit Risk |
|-------|---------------|-------------------------|--------------|
| Phase 3 readout | Q2 2026 | -40 to -60% | HIGH |
| PDUFA decision | Aug 2026 | -50 to -70% if CRL | VERY HIGH |
| ... | ... | ... | ... |

### Step 4: Litigation & Regulatory History (30 minutes)

**Goal:** Identify any prior issues that inform current risk

**Sources:**
- Stanford Securities Class Action Clearinghouse
- PACER (federal court search)
- 10-K Item 3 (Legal Proceedings)
- 10-K Item 1A (Risk Factors├втВмтАЭlook for specific disclosures)
- SEC EDGAR (8-K search for litigation disclosures)
- FDA warning letters database

**What you're looking for:**
- Prior securities class actions (even if dismissed)
- SEC inquiries or enforcement
- FDA warning letters, clinical holds, CRLs
- DOJ investigations
- State AG actions
- Whistleblower activity

### Step 5: Market Signals (20 minutes)

**Goal:** Identify what sophisticated market participants think

**Check:**
- **Short interest:** >10% is notable; >15% is concerning
- **Short seller reports:** Hindenburg, Muddy Waters, Citron, etc.├втВмтАЭthese are NUCLEAR triggers
- **Insider transactions:** Unusual selling patterns (Form 4 via SEC EDGAR)
- **Analyst ratings:** Recent downgrades, target cuts
- **Stock performance vs. XBI:** Is this company or sector driven?

### Step 6: Governance Quick Check (10 minutes)

**Goal:** Identify any structural concerns

**Check:**
- Board independence (proxy statement)
- CEO/Chair separation
- Audit committee composition
- Management tenure and track record
- Related party transactions

## 3.2 What to Look for in SEC Filings

### 10-K Deep Dive Points

| Section | What to Find | Red Flags |
|---------|--------------|-----------|
| Item 1 - Business | Pipeline status, competitive position | Vague language about timelines |
| Item 1A - Risk Factors | Specific vs. boilerplate risks | New risks added; unusually specific language |
| Item 3 - Legal | Pending litigation | Any securities suits or investigations |
| Item 7 - MD&A | Cash usage, guidance | Deteriorating trajectory |
| Item 8 - Financials | Revenue recognition, going concern | Unusual accounting; auditor notes |
| Item 9A - Controls | Internal control assessment | Material weaknesses |

### 8-K Alert Items

These 8-K filings warrant immediate attention:

- **Item 2.02** - Results of Operations (earnings; guidance)
- **Item 4.01** - Auditor changes (investigate reason)
- **Item 4.02** - Non-reliance on prior financials (NUCLEAR)
- **Item 5.02** - Executive departures (especially CFO)
- **Item 7.01/8.01** - Clinical trial results, FDA actions

### Proxy Statement Points

- Executive compensation structure (clawbacks, performance metrics)
- Board tenure and independence
- Related party transactions
- Shareholder proposals (governance concerns)

# SECTION 4: BIOTECH-SPECIFIC RISK FACTORS

## 4.1 Clinical Trial Risk

### What Can Go Wrong

| Issue | Description | Lawsuit Likelihood |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Efficacy failure** | Drug doesn't meet primary endpoint | HIGH if stock drops significantly |
| **Safety signal** | Unexpected adverse events | HIGH; raises prior knowledge questions |
| **Trial design flaw** | Endpoints, statistics, patient selection | MODERATE; harder to allege fraud |
| **Enrollment delays** | Can't recruit patients as planned | LOW; usually disclosed progressively |
| **Data integrity** | Site fraud, GCP violations | VERY HIGH; suggests prior knowledge |

### Dismissal Dynamics

Courts routinely dismiss claims based on:
- "Inherently unknowable" clinical outcomes
- Management optimism that proved wrong but wasn't fraudulent
- Forward-looking statement safe harbor
- Lack of scienter (no evidence management knew and concealed)

Courts do NOT dismiss when:
- Internal documents show management knew of problems
- Specific safety signals were concealed
- Data was manipulated or selectively disclosed
- Insiders sold before bad news

### What to Look For

**Positive indicators:**
- Experienced management with prior successful trials
- Robust trial design reviewed by KOLs
- Multiple programs reducing concentration
- Clear disclosure of risks and uncertainties

**Warning signs:**
- First-time management team
- Novel endpoints without precedent
- Single-asset dependency
- Vague or evasive discussion of trial progress
- Heavy insider selling before readout

## 4.2 FDA Regulatory Risk

### Approval Pathways Matter

| Pathway | Timeline | Implications |
|---------|----------|--------------|
| Standard review | 10-12 months | Normal risk profile |
| Priority review | 6 months | Higher stakes; less FDA feedback time |
| Accelerated approval | Variable | Post-market requirements; confirmatory trial risk |
| Breakthrough therapy | Accelerated | Intensive FDA engagement (positive signal) |

### Complete Response Letters (CRLs)

A CRL is a formal FDA rejection. This is the highest-risk biotech event.

**What to know:**
- CRLs trigger lawsuits 75-90% of the time if stock drops materially
- CRL details are often initially confidential, creating disclosure disputes
- CMC (manufacturing) issues are most common CRL reason
- Clinical data issues are most serious for litigation

**Discovery focus:**
- Has company received prior FDA feedback suggesting concerns?
- Any manufacturing or quality issues in filings?
- What has FDA communication looked like? (10-K usually discusses this)

### Clinical Holds

FDA can place trials on clinical hold for safety concerns.

**Implications:**
- Immediate stock impact
- Potential lawsuit if hold wasn't adequately disclosed as a risk
- Duration uncertainty creates extended exposure

## 4.3 Financial and Accounting Risk

### Why Financials Matter Differently

Pre-revenue biotechs have simple financials├втВмтАЭthey're just burning cash. The risk isn't earnings manipulation; it's:

- **Cash runway accuracy** - Is burn rate accelerating?
- **R&D capitalization** - Are they inappropriately capitalizing expenses?
- **Collaboration revenue** - Is milestone recognition appropriate?
- **Going concern** - Has auditor raised concerns?

### Commercial Biotech Accounting Issues

Once a biotech has product revenue, accounting complexity increases dramatically:

| Issue | Description | Risk Level |
|-------|-------------|------------|
| Gross-to-net adjustments | Rebates, discounts, returns | MODERATE; estimation complexity |
| Revenue recognition timing | When is sale complete? | MODERATE |
| Channel inventory | Is revenue stuffing occurring? | HIGH if pattern emerges |
| Milestone recognition | Partner collaboration income | MODERATE |
| Acquired IPR&D | Fair value of acquired assets | MODERATE |

### Auditor Red Flags

Watch for:
- Auditor change (always investigate why)
- Going concern paragraph
- Critical audit matters related to revenue
- Material weakness disclosures
- Restatements (NUCLEAR for scoring)

## 4.4 Governance and Management Risk

### Why Biotech Governance Differs

Biotech boards often include:
- Venture capital investors (not independent)
- Scientific founders (may lack public company experience)
- Fewer traditional corporate governance experts

This isn't necessarily bad├втВмтАЭdomain expertise matters├втВмтАЭbut creates different dynamics.

### What to Assess

**Board composition:**
- Sufficient independence for audit/comp committees?
- Public company experience among independents?
- Diverse skill sets (scientific, commercial, financial)?

**Management track record:**
- Prior public company leadership?
- Any prior litigation involvement?
- Scientific credibility (publications, reputation)?

**Compensation structure:**
- Appropriate clawback provisions?
- Milestone-based incentives aligned with long-term value?
- Any unusual arrangements?

### Turnover Concerns

Executive departures can signal problems:

| Role | Concern Level | Investigation |
|------|---------------|---------------|
| CEO | HIGH | Full circumstances review |
| CFO | VERY HIGH | Often precedes accounting issues |
| CMO | MODERATE | May signal clinical concerns |
| General Counsel | HIGH | May signal legal/regulatory concerns |
| Chief Accounting Officer | VERY HIGH | Often precedes restatements |

# SECTION 5: MARKET SIGNAL INTERPRETATION

## 5.1 Short Interest Analysis

### What Short Interest Tells You

Short sellers are sophisticated investors who profit when stocks decline. Elevated short interest means informed market participants are betting against the company.

**However:** Biotech short interest runs higher than other sectors because shorts target binary event uncertainty. Don't apply the same thresholds.

### Biotech-Adjusted Thresholds

| Short Interest | Interpretation | Action |
|----------------|----------------|--------|
| <5% | Normal | No concern |
| 5-10% | Modestly elevated | Note but no escalation |
| 10-15% | Elevated | Investigate why |
| 15-20% | Significantly elevated | Material risk factor |
| >20% | Extreme | Strong negative signal |

### Context Matters

**Higher tolerance warranted:**
- Binary event approaching (shorts may be event-betting, not fraud-betting)
- High-growth momentum names (shorts as contrarian play)
- Sector-wide short activity elevated

**Lower tolerance warranted:**
- Specific short seller report published
- Shorts initiated after recent disclosures
- Unusual borrowing cost increases
- Combined with other warning signs

## 5.2 Short Seller Reports

### Nuclear Trigger Status

A published short seller report from a credible activist short (Hindenburg, Muddy Waters, Citron, Kerrisdale, etc.) is a **NUCLEAR TRIGGER**.

**Why:**
- These reports are extensively researched
- Accuracy rate on fraud allegations is ~60%+
- Reports themselves trigger SEC/DOJ interest
- Even if allegations are overblown, litigation is nearly certain

**Recent examples:**
- Hindenburg vs. Nikola: 80% stock decline, CEO convicted
- Muddy Waters vs. Luckin: Confirmed $310M sales fraud
- Hindenburg vs. Adani: $150B value loss

### How to Handle

If you discover a short seller report:
- **Published <6 months ago:** Decline or refer to senior underwriter
- **Published 6-12 months ago:** Extreme terms if proceeding
- **Published >12 months, resolved:** May be bindable with context

## 5.3 Insider Trading Patterns

### What to Monitor

Form 4 filings show insider transactions. Look for:

| Pattern | Interpretation |
|---------|----------------|
| Regular 10b5-1 sales | Normal; pre-planned, no signal |
| Large unplanned sales | Concerning; investigate timing |
| Cluster selling across executives | Very concerning |
| Selling before binary event | High scienter risk |
| Buying | Positive signal |

### Contextual Considerations

**Less concerning:**
- Post-IPO lockup expiration sales
- Scheduled 10b5-1 plan executions
- Sales for tax obligations
- Small percentages of holdings

**More concerning:**
- First-time sales by long-tenured executives
- Sales representing high % of holdings
- Sales clustered before announcements
- Executives selling while issuing bullish guidance

## 5.4 Stock Performance Analysis

### Absolute vs. Sector-Adjusted

Always compare to XBI (Biotech ETF) to determine if decline is company-specific or sector-driven.

**Calculation:**
```
Sector-Adjusted Decline = Company Decline - XBI Decline
```

**Example:**
- Company: -35% TTM
- XBI: -20% TTM
- Sector-adjusted: -15% (company-specific)

### Decline Attribution

| Decline Type | Litigation Risk | Scoring Impact |
|--------------|-----------------|----------------|
| Market-driven (matches XBI) | LOW | Minimal |
| Sector-driven (exceeds XBI modestly) | LOW-MODERATE | Modest |
| Company-specific (10-20% excess) | MODERATE | Standard loading |
| Company-specific (20-40% excess) | ELEVATED | Significant loading |
| Company-specific (>40% excess) | HIGH | Major factor |

### Event Window Analysis

For biotech, single-event declines matter more than trailing declines.

**Analyze:**
- Day 0 (announcement) decline
- Day 0-5 cumulative decline
- Day 0-10 cumulative decline

**Thresholds:**
- Single-day >20% company-specific: Elevated risk
- 5-day >30% company-specific: High risk
- 10-day >40% company-specific: Very high risk

# SECTION 6: KEY UNDERWRITING QUESTIONS

## 6.1 Pipeline and Development

1. What percentage of company value depends on events in the next 12 months?
2. Is this a single-asset company or diversified pipeline?
3. What stage is the lead program and when is the next readout?
4. Has management run successful late-stage trials before?
5. What is the trial design and how robust is it?

## 6.2 Regulatory

1. What FDA pathway is being pursued?
2. Has the company received any FDA warning letters, CRLs, or clinical holds?
3. What is the PDUFA date for any pending applications?
4. Has FDA raised concerns in prior interactions?
5. Are there any manufacturing or CMC issues disclosed?

## 6.3 Financial

1. What is the cash runway in months?
2. Is there a financing need during the policy period?
3. For commercial biotechs: What are the gross-to-net adjustment trends?
4. Any auditor changes or going concern language?
5. Any material weaknesses in internal controls?

## 6.4 Market Signals

1. What is current short interest and how has it trended?
2. Are there any short seller reports in the past 18 months?
3. What does insider trading activity look like?
4. How has the stock performed vs. XBI?
5. What is analyst sentiment (ratings, target changes)?

## 6.5 History and Governance

1. Any prior securities class actions (even if dismissed)?
2. Any SEC or DOJ inquiries?
3. Any regulatory enforcement actions?
4. How experienced is the management team?
5. Any recent C-suite turnover?

# SECTION 7: RETENTION STRATEGY

## 7.1 Why Retention Matters More in Biotech

Biotech's high dismissal rate means most cases resolve before significant insurer payment. **The key question is whether the retention absorbs defense costs through dismissal.**

### Defense Cost Benchmarks

| Stage | Defense Cost Range |
|-------|-------------------|
| Through early MTD | $1.5M - $2.5M |
| Through MTD with amended complaint | $2.5M - $4.0M |
| Through class certification | $4M - $8M |
| Through fact discovery | $6M - $12M |
| Through trial | $12M - $25M+ |

### Retention Absorption Math

If biotech dismissal rate is 62% and most dismissals occur at MTD for ~$2.5M defense cost:

| Retention | % Dismissed Cases Absorbed | Insurer Defense Exposure |
|-----------|---------------------------|-------------------------|
| $1.5M | ~50% | Moderate |
| $2.0M | ~70% | Low |
| $2.5M | ~85% | Minimal |
| $3.0M+ | ~95% | Negligible |

**Implication:** With $2.5M retention, the insurer pays essentially nothing on the 62% of cases that are dismissed.

## 7.2 Minimum Retention Floors

These should be non-negotiable:

| Market Cap | Minimum Retention |
|------------|-------------------|
| <$500M | $1.5M |
| $500M - $2B | $2.5M |
| $2B - $5B | $5.0M |
| $5B - $10B | $7.5M |
| >$10B | $10M |

## 7.3 Sweet Spot Retentions

These optimize the risk-reward:

| Market Cap | Sweet Spot | Why It Works |
|------------|------------|--------------|
| <$500M | $2.5-3.5M | Absorbs essentially all dismissed case costs |
| $500M - $2B | $4-5M | Meaningful participation; still competitive |
| $2B - $5B | $7.5-10M | Appropriate for elevated severity |
| $5B - $10B | $10-15M | Mega-settlement cushion |
| >$10B | $15-25M | Must be significant for mega-cap exposure |

## 7.4 Retention Negotiation

When broker pushes for lower retention:

**Their argument:** "Market is at $X"

**Your response:** "At $X retention, this is Tier [Y] risk at Rate+[Z]. At $[X+1M] retention, we can offer Tier [Y-1] at Rate+[Z-15%]. The additional retention saves [$A] in premium and provides meaningful first-dollar protection against frivolous suits."

**Key point:** Frame higher retention as beneficial to client├втВмтАЭnot just insurer protective.

# SECTION 8: TERMS AND CONDITIONS TOOLKIT

## 8.1 Standard Biotech Terms

These apply to most biotech accounts:

| Term | Standard | Notes |
|------|----------|-------|
| Retention | Per Section 7 floors | Non-negotiable minimums |
| Limits | Market-appropriate | Consider sublimits for challenged risks |
| Coverage | A/B/C standard | May modify Side C for distressed accounts |
| Territory | Worldwide | Standard |
| Claim definition | Standard D&O | No modification |

## 8.2 Enhanced Terms Menu

For elevated-risk accounts (Tier D+), consider:

### Retention Adjustments
- **Higher base retention:** Floor + 50-100%
- **Event-specific retention:** Additional SIR for claims arising from identified binary events

### Limit Modifications
- **Sublimit on Side C:** For companies with financial distress or prior regulatory issues
- **Aggregate limit:** For repeat-risk accounts

### Coinsurance
- **10-20% co-pay:** On first $5-10M above retention
- **When to use:** Pipeline concentration, balance sheet concerns, prior issues

### Exclusions
- **Binary event exclusion:** For imminent PDUFA/Phase 3 with no alternative
- **Prior acts exclusion:** Post-event binding
- **Specific circumstances exclusion:** Known issues that may generate claims
- **Conduct exclusion:** For prior regulatory violators

### Timing Modifications
- **Short-term policy:** 6-month term expiring before major binary event
- **Extended reporting:** For M&A or wind-down situations

## 8.3 When NOT to Write

Some accounts are simply not bindable:

| Condition | Recommendation |
|-----------|----------------|
| Active securities class action | DECLINE |
| Wells Notice received | DECLINE |
| DOJ criminal investigation | DECLINE |
| Short seller report <6 months | DECLINE |
| Going concern opinion | DECLINE |
| Restatement <12 months | DECLINE or prospective only |
| Clinical hold on lead program | DECLINE or exclude program |
| Stock decline >70% company-specific | DECLINE or extreme terms |

## 9.2 Common Pitfalls

### Pitfall 1: Treating All Biotechs the Same
**Reality:** A pre-clinical company is completely different from a mature commercial pharma. Apply stage-specific analysis.

### Pitfall 2: Penalizing Small Market Cap
**Reality:** Small biotechs with proper retention are often our best risks due to dismissal rate economics.

### Pitfall 3: Ignoring Binary Events
**Reality:** A company that looks fine today may have a 50/50 outcome in 60 days. Always build the event calendar.

### Pitfall 4: Missing Short Seller Activity
**Reality:** Short seller reports are among the strongest predictors of actual fraud. Always check.

### Pitfall 5: Applying Non-Biotech Thresholds
**Reality:** Biotech short interest, volatility, and stock declines all run higher than other sectors. Adjust your baselines.

## 9.3 Efficiency Tips

### For Routine Submissions (Tier A/B likely):
- Triage should take 30-45 minutes
- Focus on nuclear triggers and binary calendar
- Standard terms with minimal negotiation

### For Challenged Submissions (Tier C/D likely):
- Full discovery takes 2-3 hours
- Build detailed scoring justification
- Develop terms alternatives

### For Borderline Submissions (Tier D/E):
- Consider senior underwriter consultation
- Document thoroughly
- May require negotiation cycles
