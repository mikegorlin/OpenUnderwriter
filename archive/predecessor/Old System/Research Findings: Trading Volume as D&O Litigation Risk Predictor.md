# Research Findings: Trading Volume as D&O Litigation Risk Predictor

## Key Academic Paper: Kim & Skinner (2012)

**Title**: "Measuring securities litigation risk"  
**Journal**: Journal of Accounting and Economics, Volume 53, Issues 1-2  
**Authors**: Irene Kim, Douglas J. Skinner  
**Citations**: 999+ (highly influential paper)  
**DOI**: https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jacceco.2011.09.005

### Main Findings

**Primary Research Question**: What firm characteristics predict securities class action litigation risk?

**Key Variables Tested**:
1. Industry membership (FPS industries: biotech, computers, electronics, retail)
2. **Firm size**
3. **Growth** (market-to-book, sales growth)
4. **Stock performance** (returns)
5. **Stock volatility** (return volatility)
6. Corporate governance quality
7. Insider trading
8. Securities issuance

### Results on Trading Volume

**IMPORTANT**: The Kim & Skinner (2012) paper does NOT specifically identify "trading volume / shares outstanding" as a significant predictor.

**What they DID find as significant predictors**:
1. **Stock volatility** (return volatility) - STRONG PREDICTOR ✅
2. **Firm size** - STRONG PREDICTOR ✅
3. **Growth** (market-to-book) - STRONG PREDICTOR ✅
4. **Stock performance** (negative returns) - STRONG PREDICTOR ✅
5. Industry membership - WEAK PREDICTOR alone ⚠️

**What they found NOT significant**:
- Corporate governance proxies - NOT COST-BENEFICIAL ❌
- Insider trading - NOT COST-BENEFICIAL ❌
- Securities issuance - LIMITED ADDITIONAL VALUE ⚠️

### Quote from Paper:

> "We show that while the relationship between the FPS industry measure and litigation is robust in a statistical sense, using industry membership alone does a relatively poor job of predicting litigation. However, when we supplement this variable with measures of firm characteristics that include **size, growth, and stock performance and volatility**, predictive ability improves considerably."

> "Further, including additional variables, such as proxies for corporate governance quality, issuance of securities, insider trading, and so forth, adds relatively little to predictive ability."

### Interpretation

**Trading volume is NOT mentioned as a predictor in this seminal paper.**

The paper focuses on:
- **Stock volatility** (variance of returns) - NOT trading volume
- **Stock returns** (price performance) - NOT trading volume
- **Firm size** (market cap) - NOT trading volume

### Possible Confusion

**Trading volume vs. Stock volatility**:
- **Stock volatility** = variance of stock returns (price movements)
- **Trading volume** = number of shares traded per day

These are DIFFERENT metrics. High trading volume does NOT necessarily mean high volatility.

**Liquidity vs. Litigation Risk**:
- High liquidity (high trading volume) may REDUCE litigation risk by:
  - Enabling efficient price discovery
  - Reducing illiquid price declines
  - Supporting orderly markets
- But this is NOT the same as saying "high volume PREDICTS litigation"

## Theoretical Mechanisms (Speculative)

### Why HIGH trading volume MIGHT increase D&O severity:

1. **Larger plaintiff class**: More shares traded = more potential plaintiffs
2. **Higher damages**: Damages calculated based on shares traded during class period
3. **Market attention**: High volume = more investors watching = more likely to notice problems

### Why HIGH trading volume MIGHT decrease D&O severity:

1. **Efficient markets**: High liquidity = efficient price discovery = less mispricing
2. **Orderly declines**: High volume prevents illiquid crashes that trigger litigation
3. **Professional investors**: High institutional ownership (correlated with volume) = less frivolous suits

### Why LOW trading volume MIGHT increase D&O severity:

1. **Illiquid declines**: Low volume = larger price impact from selling = bigger stock drops
2. **Information asymmetry**: Low volume = less efficient markets = more mispricing
3. **Retail investors**: Low institutional ownership = more retail investors = more frivolous suits

## CONCLUSION

**Evidence Status**: **INCONCLUSIVE / NOT VALIDATED**

1. **No direct academic evidence** that "trading volume / shares outstanding" predicts D&O claim severity
2. **Kim & Skinner (2012)** - the seminal paper on litigation risk - does NOT include trading volume as a predictor
3. **Possible confusion** with stock volatility (which IS a validated predictor)
4. **Theoretical mechanisms** point in BOTH directions (high volume could increase OR decrease risk)

**Recommendation**: 
- Do NOT claim that trading volume is a "validated predictor" of D&O severity
- CAN note that Primoris has "above-average liquidity for its size"
- CAN note that "high liquidity may reduce risk of illiquid price declines"
- CANNOT claim that "high volume predicts lower D&O severity" without evidence

**For Primoris Analysis**:
- Focus on VALIDATED predictors: stock volatility, size, growth, stock performance
- Note liquidity as a POSITIVE factor (reduces illiquid decline risk)
- Do NOT overweight trading volume in risk assessment
