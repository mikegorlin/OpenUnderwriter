# Howard Marks AI Bubble Analysis - Key Findings

**Source**: Oaktree Capital Management Memo "Is It a Bubble?" (December 9, 2025)

---

## Key Themes

### 1. Two Types of Bubbles

**"Inflection Bubbles"** (Good):
- Based on revolutionary technological progress
- Create foundation for future prosperity
- Accelerate technology adoption through capital deployment
- Examples: Railroads, electricity, internet, automobiles
- "Installation Phase" investments lay groundwork for "Deployment Period"
- May destroy wealth short-term but create long-term progress

**"Mean-Reversion Bubbles"** (Bad):
- Based on financial miracles with no real progress
- Examples: South Sea Company, subprime mortgages, portfolio insurance
- Destroy wealth without creating value

### 2. Current AI Landscape - Areas of Uncertainty

**Who will be the winners?**
- Leading companies today may not prevail
- Technology is disruptive - challengers can emerge
- Warren Buffett auto analogy: 2,000 car companies → only 3 survived
- Massive capital race for AI leadership with uncertain outcome

**Will AI produce profits, and for whom?**
- Unknown: Will it be monopoly/duopoly or highly competitive?
- AI may increase efficiency without increasing profitability
- Savings might go to customers rather than companies
- "Circular deals" concern - money round-tripping between AI players

**Useful life of AI assets**
- Unknown lifespan of AI chips and infrastructure
- Obsolescence risk from next-generation technology
- Debt repayment concerns if assets become obsolete quickly

**Demand unpredictability**
- Growth of AI demand is "totally unpredictable"
- Adoption today may have nothing to do with adoption tomorrow
- AI may improve 10x or 100x in short timeframes

### 3. Warning Signs of Bubble Behavior

**Speculative financing**:
- Vendor financing proliferates
- Coverage ratios thin
- Hyperscalers leverage balance sheets to maintain capex velocity
- Revenue momentum lags behind spending
- Resembles 2000 telecom bust financing gymnastics

**Excessive debt use**:
- Oracle, Meta, Alphabet issuing 30-year bonds for AI investments
- Yields exceed Treasuries by only 100bps
- Startups borrowing to build data centers for other startups
- SPV structures hiding debt off balance sheet
- "Minsky moment" risk - credit expansion exhausts good projects

**Lottery-ticket thinking**:
- $1 billion+ seed rounds at $10+ billion valuations
- Companies with no product refusing to explain what they're building
- Example: Thinking Machines raised $2B at $10B valuation (no product)
- Safe Superintelligence valued at $32B with no released product

**Circular deals**:
- OpenAI receives billions from tech companies, sends billions back for computing power
- Nvidia investing $100B in OpenAI while OpenAI buys Nvidia chips
- Goldman estimates 15% of Nvidia sales from circular deals

### 4. Key Risks Identified

1. **Speculative asset** - unknown AI capacity needs in 2-5 years
2. **Lender incentives misaligned** - make loans with no long-term exposure
3. **Supply/demand mismatch** - AI capacity may catch up or surpass demand
4. **Technological obsolescence** - next-gen chips may reduce value of existing infrastructure
5. **Competitive pressure** - competitors cutting prices and running losses for market share
6. **Data center glut risk** - overbuilding may render some centers uneconomic, leading to bankruptcies

### 5. Debt vs. Equity in AI

**Oaktree's Bob O'Leary perspective**:
- "Most technological advances develop into winner-takes-all or winner-takes-most competitions"
- **Equity is the right way** to play this dynamic, not debt
- Diversified equity portfolio captures eventual winner
- Debt portfolio only makes coupon on winner, insufficient to compensate for losers' impairments
- **Exception**: If you can identify the winner pool, debt vs equity is irrelevant

**Historical parallel**: Search (Lycos lost to Google) and social media (MySpace lost to Facebook)

### 6. Comparisons to Past Bubbles

**Similar to Internet Bubble**:
- Change-the-world technology ✓
- Exuberant, speculative behavior ✓
- FOMO driving participation ✓
- Suspect circular deals ✓
- Use of SPVs ✓
- $1 billion seed rounds ✓

**Different from Internet Bubble**:
- Existing product with strong demand ✓
- 1 billion users already (vs. small internet user base in 1999) ✓
- Well-established players with revenues, profits, cash flow ✓
- No IPO craze with prices doubling daily ✗
- Reasonable P/E ratios for established participants ✓

**P/E Comparison** (Goldman Sachs data):
- Internet bubble (Dec 1999): Median P/E = 41x
- Current AI leaders: Median P/E = 31x
- Nvidia: 30x P/E (vs. Microsoft 69x in 1999)

### 7. Historical Parallels: Radio & Aviation Bubbles

**Radio (RCA)**:
- Peaked 1929, lost 97% by crash
- Most high-traded stock on market
- "Nvidia of its day"
- Powerful technology but market overestimated speed of profitability

**Aviation**:
- Lindbergh 1927 flight = coordinating event (like ChatGPT launch)
- "Narrative of inevitability largely drowned out caution"
- "Technological uncertainty framed as opportunity, not risk"
- Aviation stocks dropped 96% by May 1932
- Both radio and aviation were wrapped in uncertainty, hyped with powerful narratives, accessible to retail investors
- Both helped inflate bubbles that burst in 1929

### 8. Howard Marks' Conclusion

**"Irrational exuberance" is present**:
- Investors are applying exuberance with regard to AI
- Question is whether it's irrational
- **No one can say definitively whether this is a bubble**
- Won't know until years from now whether enthusiasm was excessive

**Recommended approach**:
- "No one should go all-in without acknowledging risk of ruin if things go badly"
- "No one should stay all-out and risk missing out on great technological step forward"
- **Moderate position, applied with selectivity and prudence, seems like best approach**

**On debt specifically**:
- Okay to supply debt financing where outcome is uncertain
- NOT okay where outcome is purely conjecture
- Must make the distinction correctly

**Final wisdom**:
- "Intelligent investment in data centers, and thus in AI – like everything else – requires sober, insightful judgment and skillful implementation"
- Bubbles are best identified in retrospect
- This time IS different (AI may replace human cognition, not just augment it)
- AI progressing at "incredibly rapid clip" - scant time for mankind to adjust

## D&O Underwriting Implications

### High-Risk Indicators:
1. Companies with massive debt for AI infrastructure
2. Pre-revenue AI startups with $1B+ valuations
3. Companies engaged in circular deals
4. Heavy reliance on vendor financing
5. SPV structures hiding AI-related debt
6. Companies "building airplane in flight" (no clear product/business model)
7. Data center operators with thin coverage ratios

### Moderate-Risk Indicators:
1. Established tech companies (Microsoft, Amazon, Google, Meta) making "prudent" AI investments from cash flow
2. Companies with existing AI products generating revenue
3. Diversified companies where AI is enhancement, not core business

### Key Questions for AI Company Underwriting:
1. Is this equity-financed or debt-financed AI investment?
2. Does the company have existing AI product with revenue traction?
3. What is the debt maturity profile vs. expected AI asset useful life?
4. Are there circular deal relationships inflating revenue?
5. Is the company a "pure play" AI bet or diversified?
6. What is the competitive moat vs. risk of disruption?
