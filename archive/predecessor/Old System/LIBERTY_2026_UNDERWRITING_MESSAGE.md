## Part 1: The Market Reality for 2026

### **The Severity Surge: Numbers That Matter**

The most critical trend for 2026 is the dramatic increase in settlement severity despite stable claim frequency. Cornerstone Research reports that 2024 average settlements were $42.4 million, representing a 13% decline from 2023's $48.7 million. However, the first half of 2025 saw average settlements surge to **$56 million**, a 32% increase from 2024 and 15% above 2023 levels. This V-shaped recovery in severity means that **one severe claim can generate more loss than multiple smaller claims**, fundamentally changing our risk calculus.

The median settlement also tells an important story. While it declined 10% in 2024 to $14 million from 2023's 13-year high of $15.6 million, it remained 24% above the 2015-2023 median. This suggests that while the "typical" settlement may have moderated slightly, the overall settlement environment remains elevated compared to historical norms. The divergence between declining frequency and surging severity demands that we **price for worst-case outcomes, not average scenarios**.

### **Top D&O Risk Drivers: What's Keeping Directors Awake**

Allianz's 2026 D&O Insights identifies six primary risk drivers that will shape our underwriting decisions. **Geopolitical uncertainty** tops the list, with 45 armed conflicts in the Middle East and North Africa alone, combined with fluctuating tariffs and accelerating deglobalization. Directors face liability for failing to anticipate and adapt to geopolitical changes that impact operations, financial performance, and regulatory compliance across jurisdictions. Forty-two percent of exporting companies expect turnover to decline by 2-10% over the next 12 months, creating both operational and disclosure risks.

**Cyber risk** maintains its position as the number one risk for the fourth consecutive year in Allianz's Risk Barometer. Ransomware attacks account for 60% of the value of large cyber insurance claims, while data exfiltration incidents have doubled to represent 40% of large claims. The EU's NIS2 directive significantly increases personal accountability for D&Os, who can now be held directly responsible for overseeing cyber security with fines reaching €10 million or 2% of global turnover.

**Artificial intelligence** is generating a new wave of securities litigation, with 53 lawsuits filed since March 2020 and 12 in the first half of 2025 alone. The SEC's April 2025 enforcement action against AI-startup Nate, Inc for misleading investors demonstrates regulatory willingness to pursue "AI-washing" claims. Companies face D&O exposure from two angles: overstating AI benefits to investors and understating AI risks to profitability. This is distinct from product liability risk, which we saw with Character.AI's two teen suicides and resulting wrongful death litigation.

**PFAS (forever chemicals)** represents a massive emerging liability, with Verisk estimating potential US litigation costs of up to $165 billion. Thirty US states have initiated litigation against PFAS manufacturers, and 3M has already settled for $10.3 billion. The D&O exposure arises from securities litigation where shareholders allege directors failed to identify and adequately disclose PFAS liability risks. As Allianz notes, "The duty of a director now is greater than ever before. They need to be able to look forward, as well as back, when it comes to assessing risks to their company."

**Bankruptcy risk** is accelerating, with global business insolvencies expected to rise 6% in 2025 and 5% in 2026, marking five consecutive years of increases and reaching 24% above pre-pandemic averages. The US has seen 32 "mega bankruptcies" (companies with over $1 billion in assets) in the past 12 months, well above the historical average of 23. Insolvency risks are particularly concentrated in automotive, construction, retail, and consumer goods sectors. D&Os' liabilities are heightened during and following bankruptcy proceedings, with creditors and bankruptcy trustees looking to recover funds by targeting directors and officers.

**Trade tariffs** create additional D&O exposure beyond the revenue impacts. Directors face potential regulatory investigations and enforcement actions for tariff evasion or misleading disclosures, with the US government actively using the False Claims Act to enforce tariff compliance. A recent example saw the US government file an FCA complaint against a South Carolina furniture company alleging the firm falsified documents and underreported tariffs.

### **Real-Time Market Validation: CoreWeave, Oracle, Broadcom**

The market has provided us with three powerful case studies in the past six months that validate our risk assessment framework. **CoreWeave** went public in June 2025 and has since lost $33 billion in value with its stock declining 61% from its peak. The company exhibited multiple red flags: 67% customer concentration (Microsoft), attempted circular deal (tried to acquire its landlord Core Scientific for $9 billion, which shareholders rejected), and inability to afford AI investments from operations (4% operating margin versus 8-10%+ interest on debt). Jim Chanos, the famous Enron short seller, is now actively shorting CoreWeave, and the cost of default insurance has soared to 7.9% (junk territory). One analyst called CoreWeave's balance sheet "the ugliest in technology, by far."

**Oracle** provides an even more dramatic example of debt-financed AI infrastructure risk. The company carries $100 billion in debt with 500% debt-to-equity ratio and $248 billion in lease commitments. In Q2 FY2026, Oracle spent $12 billion in AI capex versus $2.1 billion in operating cash flow, representing 571% capex/OCF. This means Oracle is spending $5.71 for every $1 of operating cash flow—a fundamentally unsustainable position. The company also has 40% customer concentration with OpenAI for its AI data center business. On December 11, 2024, Oracle's stock dropped 11-16.5% in a single day after disappointing earnings, and it has declined 42-45% from its peak. Bonds have been downgraded to junk territory.

**Broadcom** demonstrates a more moderate risk profile but still faced market consequences. On December 12, 2024, the company's stock dropped 11% after management warned that "margins will be lower" due to AI infrastructure costs. Unlike Oracle and CoreWeave, Broadcom has strong optionality (profitable non-AI semiconductor business), can afford AI investments from operations (30% capex/OCF versus Oracle's 571%), and has proven execution capability. The 11% decline represents market concern rather than crisis, but it demonstrates that even well-positioned companies face scrutiny when AI economics become uncertain.

These three examples span the risk spectrum: CoreWeave (automatic decline—multiple red flags), Oracle (automatic decline—cannot afford from OCF), and Broadcom (elevated pricing—manageable concerns). The market validated these assessments within weeks of the issues emerging, demonstrating that disciplined underwriting based on fundamental risk factors works.

## Part 2: Our Strategic Positioning for 2026

### **Core Philosophy: Optionality Over Optimism**

Our fundamental underwriting philosophy for 2026 is captured in one sentence: **"We underwrite companies that succeed whether AI transforms the economy or proves to be overhyped."** We are not in the business of predicting whether AI will be the next electricity (transformative) or the next dot-com bubble (destructive). We are in the business of identifying companies with **multiple paths to success** that can survive either scenario.

This means we prioritize companies with profitable non-AI business cores (Microsoft, Salesforce, Adobe), adequate financial resources to fund AI investments from operating cash flow, proven execution track records, and robust governance structures. We decline companies making existential bets on AI without fallback options (C3.ai, Character.AI, CoreWeave), companies that cannot afford AI investments from operations and are betting on future AI revenue to service debt (Oracle), and companies with circular deal structures or extreme customer concentration that create Enron-like risk patterns.

The historical parallel is instructive. During the dot-com bubble, the internet was indeed transformative—but 90% of internet companies failed. Amazon crashed 94% from peak to trough but ultimately vindicated the technology. Our job is not to predict which scenario unfolds, but to underwrite companies that survive the crash if it comes and participate in the upside if it doesn't. This is classic Howard Marks optionality thinking applied to D&O underwriting.

### **Severity-Driven Pricing: One Claim Can Ruin the Year**

With average settlements at $56 million and rising, our pricing strategy must reflect severity exposure rather than frequency trends. A company with a 2% annual claim probability but $100 million severity exposure is more dangerous than a company with 5% probability but $10 million severity. We need to model pricing based on worst-case outcomes, not average scenarios.

This means applying significant surcharges for characteristics associated with high-severity claims: large market capitalization (more shareholder value at risk), high-profile AI or technology claims (media attention amplifies settlements), PFAS exposure (potentially catastrophic liability), cyber incidents with data exfiltration (class action magnets), and geopolitical or tariff-related regulatory investigations (government enforcement plus shareholder litigation). Even if overall claim frequency is declining, these severity drivers warrant premium loading.

### **Forward-Looking Assessment: Horizon Scanning Required**

Traditional D&O underwriting has been backward-looking, focusing on historical track record, prior claims experience, and proven governance practices. The 2026 environment demands a forward-looking component. As Allianz emphasizes, "The duty of a director now is greater than ever before. They need to be able to look forward, as well as back, when it comes to assessing risks to their company."

This means we must assess not just what the company has done, but how the company identifies and discloses emerging risks. Does the company maintain an emerging risks task force or horizon scanning process? How does the company evaluate risks that haven't yet materialized (PFAS, AI regulatory changes, geopolitical disruptions)? What is the quality and timeliness of risk disclosures in SEC filings? Has the company been subject to securities litigation for failure to disclose emerging risks? Does the board receive regular briefings on emerging risks from external experts?

Companies with robust emerging risk identification processes, task forces, and high-quality disclosure practices warrant pricing credits of 10-15%, as these practices materially reduce securities litigation risk from failure to disclose emerging liabilities. Conversely, companies with poor disclosure practices or reactive (rather than proactive) risk management warrant surcharges.

### **Disciplined Declination: Saying No is Strategic**

In a competitive market, the temptation is to accept marginal risks to grow premium. Our 2026 strategy explicitly rejects this approach. We will maintain a **~75% decline rate on AI-exposed companies**, accepting only those that meet our optionality, financial health, and governance standards. This discipline serves multiple strategic purposes.

First, it protects our loss ratio. The CoreWeave, Oracle, and Broadcom examples demonstrate that companies with fundamental weaknesses face severe market consequences quickly. Declining these risks before they blow up is far better than paying claims after the fact. Second, it differentiates our positioning. We are "the AI underwriters who know how to say no," which builds credibility with brokers and clients who value disciplined risk selection. Third, it allows us to grow premium through elevated pricing on quality risks rather than volume on marginal risks. We can reach our $30 million to $100 million growth target by charging appropriate premiums on companies that meet our standards, not by lowering our standards to write more business.

The automatic decline triggers are non-negotiable: debt-financed AI infrastructure where capex/OCF exceeds 200% (cannot afford from operations), customer concentration exceeding 40% for AI-related revenue (existential dependency), circular deals exceeding 20% of revenue (Enron parallel), product liability causing death or serious harm (Character.AI parallel), PFAS manufacturing without adequate disclosure and insurance, active tariff-related FCA investigation or documented evasion, and mass layoffs exceeding 30% attributed to AI automation (EPL and securities litigation risk). Any one of these triggers results in automatic declination.

## Part 3: Specific Underwriting Guidance for 2026

### **Enhanced Assessment Framework: Five Lenses**

We are implementing a five-lens assessment framework for all D&O submissions, with particular emphasis on AI-exposed companies. The lenses must be evaluated in order, with red flags assessed first to avoid wasting time on risks we will ultimately decline.

**Lens 1: Red Flags (Disqualifying)** - Check these first. If any red flag is present, decline immediately and document the reason. The seven automatic decline triggers are: (1) Debt-financed AI infrastructure (capex/OCF >200%), (2) Customer concentration >40% for AI revenue, (3) Circular deals >20% of revenue, (4) Product liability causing death/serious harm, (5) PFAS manufacturing without adequate disclosure, (6) Active tariff investigation or documented evasion, (7) Mass layoffs >30% attributed to AI. Document the specific red flag(s) in the declination memo and provide clear explanation to broker.

**Lens 2: Optionality (40% weight)** - Assess whether the company survives if AI fails. Three options: STRONG (profitable non-AI core, <20% revenue from AI, proven business model without AI), MODERATE (some AI dependency, 20-70% revenue from AI, viable but challenged without AI), WEAK (pure-play AI or >70% revenue from AI, existential bet on AI success). Take the worst answer from these questions: What percentage of revenue comes from AI? Can the company return to profitability if AI revenue disappears? Does the company have a proven business model independent of AI?

**Lens 3: Financial Health (30% weight)** - Assess whether the company can afford AI investments from operating cash flow. Three options: YES (capex/OCF <100%, can fund AI from operations, manageable debt levels), TIGHT (capex/OCF 100-200%, strained but manageable, some debt concerns), NO (capex/OCF >200%, cannot afford from operations, betting on AI revenue to service debt). Calculate: (AI capex) / (operating cash flow). If >200%, automatic NO. If 100-200%, TIGHT. If <100%, YES. Also consider: Debt-to-equity ratio (>300% is concerning), Interest coverage ratio (<2x is concerning), Sector exposure (automotive, construction, retail, consumer goods = elevated bankruptcy risk).

**Lens 4: Execution Capability (20% weight)** - Assess whether the company can deliver on AI strategy. Three options: PROVEN (successful track record, no major failures, no physical/operational constraints), UNCERTAIN (mixed track record or physical constraints, some execution concerns), UNLIKELY (history of failures, severe constraints, poor governance). Consider: Track record (past AI or technology initiatives), Physical constraints (data center construction, chip manufacturing), Governance quality (board independence, expertise, responsiveness).

**Lens 5: Emerging Risk Management (10% weight)** - NEW FOR 2026. Assess forward-looking risk identification and disclosure. Three options: STRONG (task force, external experts, proactive disclosure, high-quality SEC filings), MODERATE (some processes, reactive rather than proactive, adequate disclosure), WEAK (no formal processes, poor disclosure history, securities litigation for failure to disclose). This lens specifically addresses Allianz's emphasis on directors needing to "look forward, as well as back."

### **Sector-Specific Overlays: Tailored Assessment**

Different sectors require different underwriting approaches based on their specific risk profiles. We are implementing four sector-specific overlays that apply additional scrutiny beyond the five-lens framework.

**Technology and AI Companies** require assessment of AI-washing risk (securities litigation for overstating AI benefits) in addition to product liability risk. Key questions: What percentage of revenue or market valuation is attributed to AI capabilities? How specific and verifiable are the company's AI-related claims to investors? Has the company disclosed potential AI risks including regulatory uncertainty, competition, and technology limitations? Does the company have processes to validate AI performance claims before public disclosure? Has the company been subject to SEC inquiries or securities litigation related to AI disclosures? Companies with vague AI claims, lack of risk disclosure, or history of SEC inquiries warrant surcharges of 20-30%.

**Manufacturing and Industrial Companies** face PFAS exposure that could generate massive liability. Key questions: Does the company manufacture or use PFAS chemicals in products or operations? What is the company's environmental contamination history and remediation track record? How has the company disclosed PFAS exposure and potential liability to investors? Has the company been named in PFAS-related litigation or regulatory investigations? What insurance coverage does the company maintain for environmental and product liability? Companies with known PFAS exposure and inadequate disclosure warrant surcharges of 30-50% or specific PFAS exclusions.

**Exporters and Trade-Dependent Companies** confront tariff compliance risk and revenue volatility. Key questions: What percentage of revenue comes from exports, and to which countries? How sensitive is the company's business model to tariff changes? What compliance processes does the company maintain for tariff classification and payment? Has the company been subject to customs investigations or FCA complaints? How does the company scenario plan for trade policy changes? Companies with >30% export dependence, high tariff exposure, or weak compliance processes warrant surcharges of 15-30%.

**Automotive, Construction, Retail, and Consumer Goods Sectors** carry elevated insolvency risk requiring enhanced financial scrutiny. Additional diligence: Detailed cash flow analysis and working capital trends, Debt maturity schedules and refinancing risk, Customer payment trends and days sales outstanding, Supplier concentration and supply chain stability, Management's contingency plans for revenue declines or margin compression. Companies in these sectors with debt-to-equity >300% or interest coverage <2x warrant surcharges of 20-40% or declination if financial distress signals are severe.

### **Cyber Governance: Board-Level Accountability**

Cyber governance deserves special emphasis given NIS2 personal accountability requirements and the fact that ransomware represents 60% of large cyber claims value. For all submissions, assess: Does the board include members with cyber security expertise (CISO background, technology executives)? Has the company implemented a documented incident response plan and business continuity plan? For EU companies, what is the status of NIS2 compliance (directors can be personally fined up to €10 million)? What is the company's annual investment in cyber security as a percentage of IT budget (benchmark: 10-15%)? Has the company experienced cyber incidents in the past three years, and how were they managed? Does the company maintain cyber insurance, and at what limits?

Companies demonstrating strong cyber governance (board expertise, NIS2 compliance, documented incident response, adequate cyber insurance) warrant credits of 10-20%, as Allianz data shows proactive management significantly reduces litigation and regulatory action risk. Conversely, companies lacking board cyber expertise or documented incident response plans warrant surcharges of 15-25%, particularly if they operate in high-risk sectors (healthcare, financial services, critical infrastructure).

### **Geopolitical Risk: The New Core Assessment**

Geopolitical risk assessment must become a standard component of our underwriting process, not an afterthought. For all companies with >20% international revenue, require completion of a geopolitical risk questionnaire assessing: Geographic revenue concentration (what percentage from regions with active armed conflicts or high political instability?), Tariff exposure and sensitivity (how would 25% tariffs on key imports/exports impact margins?), Supply chain dependencies (single-source suppliers in geopolitically unstable regions?), Scenario planning processes (does the company model geopolitical disruption scenarios?), Board expertise (any directors with geopolitical intelligence or international relations background?), Disclosure quality (how does the company communicate geopolitical risks to investors?).

Companies with >50% revenue concentration in active conflict zones (Middle East, Ukraine, Taiwan Strait), high tariff exposure without mitigation strategies, or poor geopolitical risk disclosure warrant surcharges of 20-35%. Companies with robust scenario planning, board geopolitical expertise, and proactive risk mitigation warrant credits of 5-10%.

### **EPL Integration: Separate Assessment, Integrated Pricing**

Employment Practices Liability risk from AI-driven layoffs requires separate assessment but integrated pricing. We are implementing a four-tier EPL classification that applies surcharges or exclusions based on the scale and nature of workforce reductions.

**GREEN EPL** (<5% AI-driven layoffs): Minimal AI automation workforce reduction, no "AI replaced me" narrative, normal workforce transitions. No EPL surcharge. Examples: Microsoft, Salesforce, Apple (using AI to augment workers, not replace them).

**YELLOW EPL** (5-20% AI-driven layoffs): Moderate AI automation workforce reduction, some "AI replaced me" risk, manageable EPL exposure. Apply 10-25% EPL surcharge. Examples: Companies implementing AI-driven efficiency programs with moderate headcount reductions.

**RED EPL** (20-30% AI-driven layoffs): Significant AI automation workforce reduction, clear "AI replaced me" class action risk, age discrimination concerns (older workers replaced by AI). Apply 50-100% EPL surcharge OR separate EPL exclusion. Examples: Companies announcing major AI automation initiatives with substantial workforce reductions.

**DECLINE** (>30% AI-driven layoffs): Mass AI-driven workforce reduction, automatic Red Flag Category 7. Decline the submission. This level of workforce reduction creates unacceptable EPL and securities litigation risk (failure to disclose workforce reduction plans, impact on operations).

## Part 4: Pricing Guidance and Portfolio Management

### **Base Pricing Adjustments for 2026**

Given the 32% increase in average settlement severity in H1 2025, we are implementing base rate increases across the portfolio. For renewals with no material changes in risk profile, apply minimum 5-10% rate increases to reflect market severity trends. This is not negotiable—severity is rising, and our pricing must reflect this reality.

For new business, establish pricing based on the five-lens assessment with the following guidelines:

**GREEN accounts** (all lenses GREEN, no red flags, strong emerging risk management): Base rate + 10-25% for quality and profitability. These are our target accounts—companies with strong optionality, financial health, proven execution, and forward-looking risk management. We want to write these risks at appropriate pricing that reflects their quality.

**YELLOW accounts** (mix of GREEN and YELLOW assessments, no red flags): Base rate + 30-70% depending on specific concerns. These accounts require careful evaluation of which lenses are YELLOW and why. A company with YELLOW Financial Health (tight but manageable) and GREEN on all other lenses is very different from a company with YELLOW Optionality (some AI dependency) and YELLOW Execution (mixed track record). Use judgment to determine appropriate pricing within the range.

**RED accounts** (any RED assessment on Lenses 2-5, or any red flag on Lens 1): Decline. Do not attempt to price RED risks. If a company has WEAK optionality (pure-play AI), cannot afford AI from operations (NO on Financial Health), has UNLIKELY execution capability, or triggers any red flag, we decline the submission. There is no price at which these risks are acceptable.

### **Surcharge and Credit Matrix**

In addition to base pricing, apply specific surcharges or credits based on the following factors:

**Surcharges**:
- Geopolitical/Trade Risk: +15-30% for >30% export dependence or conflict zone operations
- High-Insolvency Sectors: +20-40% for automotive, construction, retail, consumer goods with elevated debt
- PFAS Exposure: +30-50% for manufacturing with PFAS use and inadequate disclosure
- AI-Washing Risk: +20-30% for technology companies with vague AI claims or poor disclosure
- Weak Cyber Governance: +15-25% for companies lacking board expertise or incident response plans
- Poor Emerging Risk Management: +15-25% for companies with weak disclosure or no horizon scanning

**Credits**:
- Strong Cyber Governance: -10-20% for board expertise, NIS2 compliance, documented response plans
- Robust Emerging Risk Management: -10-15% for task forces, external experts, proactive disclosure
- Geopolitical Risk Mitigation: -5-10% for scenario planning, board expertise, diversified operations

Apply surcharges and credits cumulatively. A company with multiple concerns can accumulate surcharges that effectively price them out of the market, which is the intended outcome. Conversely, a company with strong governance across multiple dimensions can earn meaningful credits that reward their risk management practices.

### **Portfolio Allocation Strategy**

Our target portfolio allocation for 2026 reflects our strategic priorities:

**60% GREEN accounts**: These are our core portfolio—companies with strong optionality, financial health, execution capability, and emerging risk management. Target premium growth of 15-25% through rate increases and modest exposure expansion. Focus on renewals with long-term relationships and new business in sectors with favorable risk profiles (established technology companies, professional services, healthcare with strong governance).

**30% YELLOW accounts**: These are acceptable risks with elevated pricing to reflect specific concerns. Target premium growth of 30-50% through rate increases and selective new business. Focus on companies where concerns are manageable and pricing adequately compensates for risk (e.g., companies with some AI dependency but strong financial health and proven execution).

**10% DECLINE**: Maintain ~75% decline rate on AI-exposed submissions and ~50% decline rate on overall financial lines submissions. This discipline is strategic—it protects loss ratio, differentiates our positioning, and allows growth through pricing rather than volume.

**Zero tolerance for RED accounts**: We will not write companies with any RED assessment or red flag trigger, regardless of pricing. This is a hard line that protects the portfolio from catastrophic losses.

### **Growth Target: $30M to $100M Through Quality, Not Volume**

Our growth target for 2026 is to expand the financial lines book from $30 million to $100 million in premium. This $70 million growth will come from three sources:

**Rate increases on renewals** ($25-30M): Apply minimum 5-10% increases across portfolio to reflect severity trends. On accounts with deteriorating risk profiles or market conditions, apply larger increases (15-30%). This disciplined approach to renewals will generate $25-30 million in additional premium without expanding exposure.

**Elevated pricing on new business** ($25-30M): Write new GREEN and YELLOW accounts at appropriate pricing that reflects risk quality. GREEN accounts at base + 10-25%, YELLOW accounts at base + 30-70%. By maintaining pricing discipline, we can write $25-30 million in new business premium while maintaining or improving loss ratio.

**Expansion of limits on existing accounts** ($15-20M): For GREEN accounts with strong relationships, offer increased limits at attractive pricing. Companies are increasingly purchasing additional D&O limits given elevated severity environment. This represents low-risk growth from known accounts with proven risk profiles.

**What we will NOT do**: We will not achieve growth by lowering underwriting standards, accepting RED risks at any price, or competing on price for marginal accounts. Growth through volume on poor risks is a recipe for loss ratio deterioration and ultimately undermines the franchise.

## Part 5: Operational Excellence and Team Development

Consistent application of the five-lens framework requires rigorous documentation. For every submission, complete the standardized assessment template documenting: Red flag check (list each of seven triggers and confirm none present, or identify which triggered and decline), Optionality assessment (STRONG/MODERATE/WEAK with supporting evidence), Financial health assessment (YES/TIGHT/NO with capex/OCF calculation), Execution capability assessment (PROVEN/UNCERTAIN/UNLIKELY with track record summary), Emerging risk management assessment (STRONG/MODERATE/WEAK with disclosure quality evaluation), Sector-specific overlay (if applicable—technology, manufacturing, exporter, high-insolvency sector), Final bucket determination (GREEN/YELLOW/RED), Pricing rationale (base rate, surcharges, credits, final premium).

This documentation serves multiple purposes. It ensures consistency across underwriters, provides clear rationale for pricing decisions that can be explained to brokers and clients, creates an audit trail for management review and regulatory examination, and enables portfolio analytics to identify trends and refine the framework over time.

### **Broker Education and Market Positioning**

Our brokers need to understand our strategic positioning and underwriting philosophy to bring us appropriate submissions. We will conduct quarterly broker education sessions covering: The five-lens framework and how we assess risk, Automatic decline triggers and why they're non-negotiable, Severity trends and why pricing is increasing, Real-world examples (CoreWeave, Oracle, Broadcom) that validate our approach, How to position Liberty as "the AI underwriters who know how to say no," What makes a "good" submission for Liberty (GREEN account characteristics).

The goal is to shift broker behavior toward bringing us higher-quality submissions rather than testing our appetite with marginal risks. Brokers who understand our framework can self-select appropriate submissions, reducing our decline rate on their business and building stronger relationships. We want to be known as disciplined but fair—we will decline risks that don't meet our standards, but we will provide clear explanations and pay appropriate premiums for risks that do.

### **Claims Collaboration and Early Warning Systems**

Close collaboration with our claims team is essential for early identification of emerging issues and continuous improvement of the underwriting framework. We will implement: Monthly claims review meetings to discuss new D&O claims, trends, and lessons learned, Early warning system for accounts showing distress signals (stock declines >20%, regulatory investigations, management turnover, earnings misses), Post-mortem analysis on any claim exceeding $10 million to identify whether underwriting could have predicted the issue, Feedback loop from claims to underwriting to refine red flag triggers and assessment criteria.

The CoreWeave, Oracle, and Broadcom examples demonstrate that market signals often precede formal claims by months. By monitoring our portfolio for similar signals (stock declines, analyst downgrades, regulatory investigations), we can take proactive action including non-renewal recommendations, limit reductions, or enhanced monitoring.

### **Continuous Learning and Framework Evolution**

The D&O landscape is evolving rapidly, and our framework must evolve with it. We will conduct: Quarterly framework reviews to assess whether red flag triggers remain appropriate, whether new triggers should be added (e.g., quantum computing risks, new regulatory regimes), and whether pricing guidance reflects current market conditions. Annual deep-dive research on emerging risks (PFAS, AI, geopolitical, cyber, climate) incorporating latest litigation trends, regulatory developments, and academic research. Semi-annual competitive intelligence gathering to understand how other carriers are approaching AI risks, what pricing levels are prevailing in the market, and where opportunities exist for differentiation.

The team should view the framework as a living document that improves through experience and market feedback. If we decline a risk that subsequently performs well, we should understand why our assessment was overly conservative. If we write a risk that subsequently deteriorates, we should understand what we missed. This continuous learning mindset is what separates good underwriting teams from great ones.

### **Individual Development and Expertise Building**

Each underwriter should develop deep expertise in at least one of the key risk areas: AI and technology risks (AI-washing, product liability, technology execution), Cyber governance and NIS2 compliance, Geopolitical risk and trade policy, PFAS and environmental liability, Bankruptcy and financial distress indicators. This specialization allows the team to consult each other on complex submissions and builds collective expertise that differentiates Liberty in the market.

We will support this development through: External training on specialized topics (e.g., NIS2 compliance seminar, PFAS litigation workshop), Industry conference attendance (e.g., PLUS D&O Symposium, RIMS), Expert speaker series (invite outside experts on geopolitical risk, AI regulation, cyber security), Cross-functional collaboration (work with Liberty's cyber, environmental, and professional liability teams).
