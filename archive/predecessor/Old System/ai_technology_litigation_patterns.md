# AI and Technology Sector Securities Litigation Patterns

## Source
DLA Piper - AI-related Securities Class Action Filings (September 2025)

## Filing Trends

- **2023**: 7 AI-related securities class actions filed
- **2024**: 14 cases filed (doubled from 2023)
- **2025 (through September)**: 12 cases filed
- **Total since 2020**: Over 50 AI-related securities class actions
- **Trend**: Rapidly increasing year-over-year

## Seven Major Hazard Categories for AI/Technology Companies

### 1. AI Washing - Exaggeration of AI Capabilities

**Hazard Description**: Companies overstate sophistication, effectiveness, or uniqueness of AI/ML technologies.

**Specific Circumstances That Trigger Lawsuits**:
- Claiming advanced AI platforms while actually relying on manual labor or offshore workers
- Marketing products as "AI-driven" when using basic algorithms or questionnaires
- Representing proprietary AI technology when using third-party tools or non-AI solutions
- Claiming AI can "reliably detect" or perform functions when testing shows significant failure rates
- Exaggerating ability to leverage AI in specific market opportunities (e.g., product upgrade cycles)

**Case Examples**:
- Innodata: Claimed advanced AI platforms but allegedly relied heavily on offshore manual labor with rudimentary AI
- Oddity Tech: Touted proprietary AI-driven product matching but allegedly used basic questionnaire, not "true AI"
- Evolv Technologies: Claimed AI could "reliably detect" weapons but allegedly failed to detect certain knives, bomb components, and micro-compact pistols
- Skyworks Solutions: Allegedly exaggerated ability to leverage AI within iPhone upgrade cycle

**Underwriting Red Flags**:
- Heavy marketing emphasis on AI capabilities without clear technical documentation
- Significant offshore labor force in companies claiming AI automation
- Recent pivot to "AI company" positioning
- Limited R&D spending relative to AI claims
- Small technical team size relative to claimed AI development

### 2. Misleading Statements About AI-Driven Revenue or Market Demand

**Hazard Description**: Companies attribute growth, customer retention, or competitive advantage to AI/ML when actual drivers are traditional methods.

**Specific Circumstances That Trigger Lawsuits**:
- Claiming AI drives revenue when growth actually comes from acquisitions or traditional services
- Attributing customer retention to AI when using aggressive sales tactics
- Claiming competitive advantage from AI when using standard marketing approaches
- Weak actual demand for AI features while claiming strong market traction
- AI offerings cannibalizing higher-margin traditional business lines

**Case Examples**:
- Tempus AI: Touted as AI healthcare company but allegedly had little history of AI revenues; most revenue from acquisitions, genomic testing, and data licensing
- Multiple companies: Alleged weak demand for AI features with AI shift cannibalizing higher-margin business

**Underwriting Red Flags**:
- Recent acquisitions accounting for majority of revenue growth
- Declining margins coinciding with AI product launches
- Customer churn increasing despite AI feature rollouts
- Revenue mix showing traditional services still dominating
- Market research showing weak demand for AI features in company's sector

### 3. Concealment of AI Limitations and Challenges

**Hazard Description**: Failure to disclose limitations, technical challenges, or delays in developing or deploying AI solutions.

**Specific Circumstances That Trigger Lawsuits**:
- Not revealing execution challenges in rolling out AI platforms
- Concealing technical limitations of AI systems
- Hiding competitive disadvantages in AI capabilities
- Failing to disclose delays in AI development timelines
- Omitting meaningful discussion of how AI was tested or benchmarked

**Underwriting Red Flags**:
- Aggressive AI deployment timelines without pilot programs
- Limited disclosure of testing methodologies
- No discussion of AI limitations in public statements
- Absence of third-party validation or benchmarking
- Rapid AI product launches without beta testing periods

### 4. False Claims of Third-Party Validation or Independence

**Hazard Description**: Companies falsely claim independent third-party validation of AI technologies or manipulate testing processes.

**Specific Circumstances That Trigger Lawsuits**:
- Claiming independent testing while being heavily involved in test design
- Manipulating test results or testing conditions
- Misrepresenting scope or rigor of third-party validation
- Omitting disclosure of company involvement in "independent" testing
- Cherry-picking favorable test results while hiding negative findings

**Case Example**:
- Evolv Technologies: Disclosed AI weapons detection validated by independent testing, but allegedly manipulated test results and was heavily involved in reporting process

**Underwriting Red Flags**:
- Vague references to "third-party testing" without specifics
- No publicly available validation reports
- Testing conducted by parties with financial relationships to company
- Limited peer review or academic validation
- Absence of industry-standard certification

### 5. Concealment of Increased Costs or Negative Financial Impact from AI

**Hazard Description**: Companies downplay or fail to disclose significant costs, margin pressures, or negative financial impacts from AI development, maintenance, or integration.

**Specific Circumstances That Trigger Lawsuits**:
- Understating negative impact of increased R&D expenses for AI competition
- Hiding margin compression from AI infrastructure costs
- Not disclosing integration costs for AI systems
- Concealing customer acquisition costs for AI products
- Failing to reveal maintenance and update costs for AI platforms

**Case Example**:
- Xiao-I: Allegedly understated negative impact of increased R&D expenses required to compete in AI industry

**Underwriting Red Flags**:
- R&D expenses increasing faster than revenue growth
- Gross margins declining after AI product launches
- Capital expenditures for AI infrastructure not disclosed
- Cloud computing costs increasing significantly
- Technical talent acquisition costs rising

### 6. Failure to Disclose Material Risks and Known Trends Related to AI

**Hazard Description**: Using "boilerplate" risk disclosures or failing to update risk factors when AI-related risks materialize.

**Specific Circumstances That Trigger Lawsuits**:
- Generic risk disclosures about AI not tailored to company's specific situation
- Couching risks as hypothetical when they have already occurred
- Failing to update risk factors as AI challenges emerge
- Not disclosing regulatory risks specific to AI applications
- Omitting competitive risks from AI technology changes

**Underwriting Red Flags**:
- Risk factors unchanged despite evolving AI landscape
- Generic AI risk language copied across multiple filings
- No discussion of sector-specific AI regulations
- Absence of competitive AI risk analysis
- Risk factors not updated after known AI incidents

### 7. Misleading Statements About AI Research, Development, and Investment

**Hazard Description**: Misrepresenting extent of AI investment, size and expertise of AI teams, or progress of AI projects.

**Specific Circumstances That Trigger Lawsuits**:
- Claiming significant AI R&D investment with minimal actual spending
- Overstating size or expertise of AI development teams
- Misrepresenting progress of AI projects or development milestones
- Claiming AI research capabilities without appropriate resources
- Exaggerating partnerships or collaborations with AI research institutions

**Case Example**:
- Innodata: Allegedly claimed AI development capabilities but had only eight employees and had not increased R&D spending on AI

**Underwriting Red Flags**:
- Small technical team relative to AI development claims
- R&D spending flat or declining despite AI initiatives
- Limited AI-related patent filings
- Few or no AI-specialized hires
- Absence of published research or technical papers
- No partnerships with academic institutions or AI research labs

## Additional Patterns

### Common Plaintiff Tactics
- Following short-seller reports with securities lawsuits
- Attacking business strategy disguised as securities claims
- Focusing on risk disclosures and financial result explanations
- Capitalizing on pressure companies face to demonstrate AI leadership

### Industry Context
- Companies across all sectors incorporating AI into products
- Pressure to demonstrate market leadership in AI space
- Many complaints follow familiar plaintiff playbooks
- Some cases focus on technology itself, many focus on revenue/demand claims

## Underwriting Implications by Company Profile

### Highest Risk Profile
- Recently rebranded as "AI company"
- Heavy AI marketing with limited technical substance
- Small technical team with big AI claims
- Flat R&D spending despite AI initiatives
- No third-party validation of AI capabilities
- Declining margins after AI product launches

### Moderate Risk Profile
- Legitimate AI development but aggressive marketing
- Some AI capabilities but also traditional business drivers
- Growing AI team but still building capabilities
- Increasing R&D investment in AI
- Early-stage AI products without full market validation

### Lower Risk Profile
- Established AI capabilities with track record
- Transparent about AI limitations and challenges
- Significant R&D investment with demonstrable results
- Third-party validated AI performance
- Balanced disclosure of AI opportunities and risks
- Technical team size matches development claims
