# AI COMPANY BUCKET STRATEGY FOR D&O UNDERWRITING

**Purpose**: Classification framework for AI-exposed companies to guide D&O underwriting decisions  
**Based on**: Howard Marks Oaktree Memo "Is It a Bubble?" (December 9, 2025)  
**Context**: AI investment exhibiting bubble characteristics with uncertain outcomes  
**Date**: December 2025

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The AI sector presents a paradox for D&O underwriters. While AI represents potentially transformative technology comparable to railroads or electricity ("inflection bubble"), current investment behavior shows concerning speculative characteristics including circular deals, excessive leverage, lottery-ticket valuations, and unpredictable demand trajectories. Howard Marks concludes that "irrational exuberance" is present, though whether it constitutes a destructive bubble remains unknowable until years hence.

**Key Insight for D&O Underwriting**: This is fundamentally an **equity story, not a debt story**. AI will likely produce winner-takes-all or winner-takes-most outcomes. Companies financing AI investments with significant debt face asymmetric risk - if they lose the competitive race, debt holders face impairment while equity in the winner generates massive returns. D&O litigation risk follows this pattern: heavily-leveraged AI "losers" will face securities litigation when projections fail, while equity-financed winners can pivot without triggering shareholder lawsuits.

**Strategic Recommendation**: **Selective participation** - target established cash-generative companies making prudent AI investments; avoid pre-revenue AI pure-plays and debt-financed infrastructure plays; approach with extreme caution any company exhibiting "circular deal" patterns or lottery-ticket financing.

---

## BUCKET 1: TARGET (Favorable for D&O Coverage)

### Definition
Established, profitable companies integrating AI to enhance existing business models, financed primarily through operating cash flow, with diversified revenue streams and proven management teams.

### Characteristics

**Financial Profile**:
- Market cap >$10B (large-cap or mega-cap)
- Profitable for 5+ consecutive years
- Strong operating cash flow (AI investments <30% of annual OCF)
- Minimal AI-specific debt (<10% of total debt attributable to AI)
- Debt/EBITDA <3x
- Investment-grade credit rating

**AI Investment Profile**:
- AI is enhancement to existing business, not core business model
- Clear use cases with measurable ROI
- Phased, disciplined capital deployment
- AI spending represents <20% of total capex
- Revenue from AI products already flowing (not purely speculative)

**Business Model**:
- Diversified revenue streams (AI <40% of total revenue)
- Existing customer base adopting AI features
- Proven ability to monetize technology investments historically
- Not dependent on "winner-takes-all" AI outcome

**Governance & Management**:
- Experienced management team (CEO tenure >3 years)
- Board with technology and financial expertise
- Conservative financial guidance practices
- History of meeting or beating guidance
- Transparent AI investment disclosures

**Examples** (Illustrative):
- Microsoft, Amazon, Google (established hyperscalers with diversified businesses)
- Enterprise software companies adding AI features (Salesforce, Adobe, ServiceNow)
- Financial services firms using AI for internal efficiency (JPMorgan, Goldman Sachs for own use)

### Underwriting Approach
- **Appetite**: Strong
- **Pricing**: Standard to slightly elevated (10-20% load for AI exposure)
- **Retention**: Standard ($1-5M depending on size)
- **Coverage**: Broad form acceptable
- **Key Focus**: Ensure AI disclosures are adequate; verify no circular deal relationships

## BUCKET 2: CAUTION (Selective - Requires Enhanced Due Diligence)

### Definition
Companies with significant AI exposure but mixed risk profile - may have revenue traction but also concerning leverage, valuation, or competitive positioning. Requires case-by-case underwriting judgment.

### Sub-Bucket 2A: Established AI Product Companies (Moderate Risk)

**Characteristics**:
- Market cap $1-10B (mid-cap)
- AI-native business model BUT with revenue traction
- Profitable or path to profitability visible within 12-18 months
- Some debt but manageable (Debt/EBITDA 3-5x)
- Competitive position uncertain but defensible
- Examples: Palantir, C3.ai, UiPath (companies with AI products generating revenue)

**Underwriting Approach**:
- **Appetite**: Moderate - selective participation
- **Pricing**: Elevated (30-50% load vs. non-AI tech)
- **Retention**: Higher ($3-10M)
- **Coverage**: Consider sublimits on certain representations (forward-looking statements)
- **Key Focus**: Revenue quality, customer concentration, competitive threats, path to profitability

### Sub-Bucket 2B: AI Infrastructure / Picks-and-Shovels (Moderate-High Risk)

**Characteristics**:
- Companies selling AI infrastructure (chips, data centers, cloud services)
- Benefiting from AI boom but exposed to demand uncertainty
- May have significant debt to finance infrastructure buildout
- Examples: Data center REITs, specialized AI chip designers, cloud infrastructure providers

**Underwriting Approach**:
- **Appetite**: Cautious - limited participation
- **Pricing**: Significantly elevated (50-75% load)
- **Retention**: High ($5-15M)
- **Coverage**: Restrictive - exclude coverage for debt financing representations
- **Key Focus**: Debt maturity profile, asset useful life assumptions, demand visibility, customer concentration

**Red Flags** (Move to Bucket 3 - Avoid):
- Debt/EBITDA >5x
- >50% of debt maturing within 3 years
- Customer concentration >40% in top 5 customers
- Evidence of circular deals
- Vendor financing >20% of revenue
- Coverage ratios <1.5x

### Sub-Bucket 2C: Traditional Companies with Heavy AI Pivot (High Risk)

**Characteristics**:
- Legacy business model pivoting heavily to AI
- AI investments represent >50% of capex
- May be issuing debt to finance AI transformation
- Competitive position in legacy business eroding
- Examples: Traditional software companies racing to add AI, legacy tech companies reinventing

**Underwriting Approach**:
- **Appetite**: Very cautious - minimal participation
- **Pricing**: Highly elevated (75-100% load)
- **Retention**: Very high ($10-25M)
- **Coverage**: Highly restrictive - exclude AI-related projections and forward-looking statements
- **Key Focus**: Execution risk, management credibility, financial flexibility, disclosure quality

**Red Flags** (Move to Bucket 3 - Avoid):
- Legacy business declining >20% annually
- Management making aggressive AI revenue projections (>100% growth)
- Debt issued specifically for AI investments
- History of missing guidance
- Recent management turnover

## BUCKET 3: AVOID (Decline Coverage)

### Definition
Companies exhibiting high-risk bubble characteristics identified in Howard Marks memo - speculative financing, lottery-ticket thinking, circular deals, or business models dependent on unprovable assumptions.

### Sub-Bucket 3A: Pre-Revenue AI Pure-Plays (DECLINE)

**Characteristics**:
- No meaningful revenue (<$10M annually)
- Valuation >$1B ("lottery ticket" thinking)
- Raised $500M+ in funding despite no product
- Cannot or will not explain business model clearly
- Examples: Thinking Machines Lab ($2B raised, $10B valuation, no product), Safe Superintelligence ($32B valuation, no released product)

**Rationale for Decline**:
- Valuation entirely speculative - no basis for D&O underwriting
- High probability of securities litigation if/when bubble pops
- Management credibility unknown
- No financial metrics to assess
- "Building airplane while in flight" - outcome purely conjectural

### Sub-Bucket 3B: Circular Deal Participants (DECLINE)

**Characteristics**:
- Revenue significantly derived from companies that are also investors
- Investment commitments to industry counterparties totaling >$500M
- Vendor financing >30% of revenue
- Examples: Companies with OpenAI-style circular arrangements (receiving billions from tech companies while sending billions back for services)

**Rationale for Decline**:
- Revenue quality highly suspect
- Potential for accounting manipulation
- "Perpetual motion machine" unsustainable
- High securities litigation risk when arrangements unwind
- Resembles 2000 telecom bust circular deals

### Sub-Bucket 3C: Debt-Financed AI Infrastructure with Thin Coverage (DECLINE)

**Characteristics**:
- Issued >$5B in debt specifically for AI investments
- Debt maturity <10 years but AI asset useful life uncertain
- Coverage ratios <1.2x
- Debt/EBITDA >6x
- Borrowing to build data centers for other startups (both losing cash)
- SPV structures hiding debt off balance sheet

**Rationale for Decline**:
- "Minsky moment" risk - credit expansion exhausting good projects
- Asset obsolescence risk (next-gen chips may obsolete current infrastructure)
- Lender incentives misaligned (make loans, no long-term exposure)
- High bankruptcy risk if AI demand disappoints
- D&O litigation certain if company fails to service debt

**Specific Red Flags**:
- 30-year bonds issued for technology with uncertain 5-year useful life
- Yields only 100bps above Treasuries despite technological uncertainty
- Startup borrowing to build data center for another startup
- Both parties losing "tremendous amounts of cash"
- No visibility into whether investments will pay off

### Sub-Bucket 3D: $1 Billion Seed Rounds / Extreme Valuations (DECLINE)

**Characteristics**:
- Seed or Series A funding >$500M
- Valuation >$5B pre-revenue or >20x revenue
- Founders refusing to explain product or business model
- "We're building AGI" without specifics
- Investor pitch emphasizes "best AI people" but no product roadmap

**Rationale for Decline**:
- Lottery-ticket thinking - probability of 8x+ return justifies investment
- No reasonable basis for valuation
- High probability of down-round or failure
- Securities litigation inevitable if company fails to achieve trillion-dollar outcome
- Management accountability unclear when outcome is purely speculative

## CROSS-CUTTING RED FLAGS (Move to Bucket 3 Regardless of Other Factors)

### Financial Red Flags
1. **Debt-for-AI**: Issuing >$1B in debt specifically for AI investments with maturity <10 years
2. **Vendor financing**: >30% of revenue from vendor financing arrangements
3. **Circular deals**: Material revenue from companies that are also investors/customers in circular pattern
4. **SPV structures**: Using special purpose vehicles to hide AI-related debt off balance sheet
5. **Coverage ratios**: Interest coverage <1.5x or debt service coverage <1.2x
6. **Extreme leverage**: Debt/EBITDA >6x with >50% attributable to AI investments

### Operational Red Flags
7. **No product**: Raised >$500M but no released product or service
8. **Refusal to explain**: Management cannot or will not clearly explain business model
9. **Building in flight**: "We're creating something that will change the world" without specifics
10. **Unpredictable demand**: Business model assumes AI demand growth that is admittedly unpredictable
11. **Asset life uncertainty**: Investing in assets with unknown useful life (AI chips, data centers)
12. **Winner-takes-all dependency**: Business model only works if company becomes #1 or #2 in market

### Valuation Red Flags
13. **Lottery ticket**: Valuation implies need for >10x return to justify investment
14. **$1B+ seed round**: Seed or Series A funding >$500M at >$5B valuation
15. **No comparables**: Valuation methodology relies entirely on "this time is different" logic
16. **Extreme multiples**: Trading at >50x revenue or >100x EBITDA with no clear path to margin improvement

### Management & Governance Red Flags
17. **Aggressive projections**: Guiding to >100% revenue growth in AI segment without historical precedent
18. **Guidance misses**: History of missing guidance by >20%
19. **Management turnover**: CFO or CEO turnover in past 12 months during AI pivot
20. **Disclosure gaps**: Inadequate disclosure of AI investment risks, asset useful life, or competitive threats
21. **Insider selling**: Heavy insider selling (>50% of holdings) during AI hype period

## INDUSTRY-SPECIFIC GUIDANCE

### AI Chip Designers / Semiconductor

**Target** (Bucket 1):
- Nvidia, AMD, Intel (established, diversified, cash-generative)
- Criteria: Diversified customer base, proven R&D track record, strong balance sheet

**Caution** (Bucket 2):
- Specialized AI chip startups with revenue traction (e.g., Cerebras, Groq if public)
- Criteria: Must have >$100M revenue, <3x Debt/EBITDA, clear competitive moat

**Avoid** (Bucket 3):
- Pre-revenue chip designers
- Companies with single customer >40% revenue
- Startups competing directly with Nvidia without clear differentiation
- Any company with "we'll be biggest if transformers go away" thesis

### AI Model Developers / LLM Companies

**Target** (Bucket 1):
- None currently - too early stage and speculative

**Caution** (Bucket 2):
- OpenAI (if/when public) - only if profitable and circular deals <20% revenue
- Anthropic (if/when public) - only if profitable and not dependent on circular funding

**Avoid** (Bucket 3):
- All pre-revenue LLM companies
- Any company with >$1B valuation and <$100M revenue
- Companies engaged in circular deals with investors
- "We're building AGI" companies without clear monetization

### Data Center Operators / Cloud Infrastructure

**Target** (Bucket 1):
- Established hyperscalers (AWS, Azure, Google Cloud) - part of larger profitable entities
- Criteria: AI workloads <30% of total, strong cash flow, investment-grade credit

**Caution** (Bucket 2):
- Specialized AI data center operators with long-term contracts
- Criteria: Must have >70% capacity contracted for >5 years, Debt/EBITDA <4x, investment-grade tenants

**Avoid** (Bucket 3):
- Data center operators with Debt/EBITDA >5x
- Operators with >50% exposure to AI startups (tenant credit risk)
- Any operator using SPVs to hide debt
- Operators with <50% contracted capacity
- "Build it and they will come" speculative data center development

### Enterprise AI Software

**Target** (Bucket 1):
- Established SaaS companies adding AI features (Salesforce, Adobe, ServiceNow, Workday)
- Criteria: AI <30% of revenue, profitable core business, clear AI monetization path

**Caution** (Bucket 2):
- AI-native enterprise software with >$50M revenue and >100 enterprise customers
- Criteria: Revenue retention >100%, gross margins >70%, path to profitability within 18 months

**Avoid** (Bucket 3):
- Pre-revenue enterprise AI startups
- Companies with <20 enterprise customers
- "We're replacing [established category] with AI" without proof
- Negative gross margins (paying more for AI inference than charging customers)

### AI Services / Consulting

**Target** (Bucket 1):
- Established IT services firms adding AI practices (Accenture, IBM Services, Cognizant)
- Criteria: AI <20% of revenue, profitable, diversified customer base

**Caution** (Bucket 2):
- AI-native services firms with >$100M revenue
- Criteria: Must have >50 customers, revenue retention >90%, profitable

**Avoid** (Bucket 3):
- Pure-play AI consulting with <$50M revenue
- Services firms dependent on single AI platform (e.g., only OpenAI integrations)
- Negative margins (hiring expensive AI talent faster than revenue growth)

## UNDERWRITING QUESTIONS - AI COMPANY CHECKLIST

### Financial Structure
1. What percentage of total debt was issued specifically for AI investments?
2. What is the maturity profile of AI-related debt?
3. What assumptions underlie the useful life of AI assets (chips, data centers)?
4. What is the company's interest coverage ratio and debt service coverage ratio?
5. Does the company use SPVs or off-balance-sheet structures for AI investments?

### Revenue Quality
6. What percentage of revenue comes from customers who are also investors?
7. What percentage of revenue involves vendor financing?
8. Are there circular deal arrangements with other AI companies?
9. What is customer concentration (top 5 customers as % of revenue)?
10. What is revenue retention rate for AI products?

### Business Model & Competitive Position
11. Is the company's business model dependent on becoming #1 or #2 in the market?
12. What is the competitive moat vs. risk of disruption from next-gen technology?
13. How predictable is demand for the company's AI products/services?
14. What happens to the business if AI demand grows 10x slower than projected?
15. What happens if a competitor offers similar services at 50% lower price?

### Management & Disclosure
16. Has the company clearly explained its AI business model and path to profitability?
17. What is the company's track record of meeting guidance?
18. Have there been recent CFO or CEO changes during the AI pivot?
19. Does the company adequately disclose AI investment risks and uncertainties?
20. Is management making aggressive AI revenue projections (>100% growth)?

### Governance & Insider Activity
21. What is the board's expertise in AI and technology?
22. Are there independent directors with financial/risk management expertise?
23. What is insider ownership level and recent insider trading activity?
24. Are executive compensation metrics tied to sustainable profitability or just revenue growth?
25. Has the company faced any regulatory scrutiny related to AI disclosures?

## PORTFOLIO CONSTRUCTION GUIDANCE

### Target Portfolio Composition (Path to $30M → $100M+ GWP)

**Bucket 1 (Target)**: 70-80% of AI-exposed premium
- Large-cap established tech companies with prudent AI investments
- Profitable, cash-generative, diversified businesses
- Lower risk, lower pricing, foundation of portfolio

**Bucket 2 (Caution)**: 20-25% of AI-exposed premium
- Selective participation in higher-risk/higher-premium opportunities
- Rigorous underwriting, enhanced due diligence
- Higher retentions, restrictive coverage terms
- Drives premium growth through higher rates

**Bucket 3 (Avoid)**: 0-5% of AI-exposed premium
- Generally decline
- Exception: Extremely compelling risk with extraordinary premium (3-5x standard rate)
- Very high retentions ($25M+), highly restrictive terms
- Only for relationship/strategic reasons

### Growth Strategy: $30M → $100M GWP

**Phase 1 (Year 1)**: Build Bucket 1 Foundation
- Target: Grow Bucket 1 from $20M → $50M GWP
- Focus: Established tech companies adding AI features
- Pricing: Standard + 10-20% AI load
- Approach: Relationship-driven, emphasize stability and expertise

**Phase 2 (Year 2)**: Selective Bucket 2 Expansion
- Target: Add $20-30M GWP from Bucket 2
- Focus: AI infrastructure and established AI product companies
- Pricing: 30-75% load vs. non-AI tech
- Approach: Differentiate through sophisticated risk assessment

**Phase 3 (Year 3)**: Maintain Discipline, Harvest Premium
- Target: Reach $100M+ GWP with 75% Bucket 1, 25% Bucket 2
- Avoid: Resist pressure to move down-market into Bucket 3 as competition intensifies
- Positioning: "Selective AI underwriter - we know how to say no"

### Competitive Positioning

**Differentiation Strategy**:
1. **Expertise-driven**: "We understand AI bubble dynamics - Howard Marks framework"
2. **Selective participation**: "We're not chasing every AI deal - only the right ones"
3. **Sophisticated risk assessment**: "We distinguish between inflection bubbles (good) and speculation (bad)"
4. **Long-term partnership**: "We'll be here after the bubble pops - stability matters"

**Avoid**:
- Racing to bottom on pricing to win AI deals
- Relaxing underwriting standards to hit growth targets
- Following market into Bucket 3 companies as competition intensifies

## MONITORING & EARLY WARNING INDICATORS

### Portfolio-Level Monitoring

**Quarterly Review**:
1. What percentage of portfolio is AI-exposed? (Target: <40%)
2. What percentage is Bucket 1 vs. Bucket 2 vs. Bucket 3? (Target: 75/25/0)
3. Are we seeing increased circular deal patterns in portfolio companies?
4. Are portfolio companies increasing AI-related debt levels?
5. Are we seeing guidance misses or downward revisions in AI segments?

### Market-Level Monitoring (Bubble Warning Signs)

**Credit Market Indicators**:
1. Are AI-related bond yields compressing further vs. Treasuries?
2. Are more companies issuing 30-year debt for AI investments?
3. Are SPV structures becoming more common?
4. Are lenders sweetening terms to keep deals flowing?

**Equity Market Indicators**:
5. Are seed round valuations continuing to climb (>$10B)?
6. Are more companies going public pre-revenue?
7. Are AI stock P/E multiples expanding beyond current levels?
8. Is retail investor participation increasing (meme stock behavior)?

**Operational Indicators**:
9. Are data center vacancy rates rising?
10. Are AI chip lead times shortening (supply catching up to demand)?
11. Are we seeing AI company bankruptcies or down-rounds?
12. Are hyperscalers slowing capex growth?

**"Minsky Moment" Triggers** (Immediate Portfolio Review):
- Credit expansion exhausts good projects, marginal deals proliferate
- First major AI company bankruptcy or debt default
- Hyperscaler announces material capex reduction
- Regulatory intervention in AI sector (antitrust, safety regulations)
- Technological breakthrough obsoletes current infrastructure (e.g., 10x more efficient chips)

## CONCLUSION & STRATEGIC RECOMMENDATION

The AI sector presents a classic underwriting dilemma: potentially transformative technology creating real value (inflection bubble) combined with speculative excess and unpredictable outcomes (mean-reversion bubble risk). Howard Marks' conclusion - "moderate position, applied with selectivity and prudence" - applies directly to D&O underwriting.

**Strategic Imperatives**:

1. **Participate selectively** - AI will transform the economy; complete avoidance is wrong
2. **Distinguish equity from debt stories** - favor companies financing AI with equity/cash flow over debt
3. **Avoid lottery tickets** - decline pre-revenue pure-plays and circular deal participants
4. **Demand transparency** - only underwrite companies that clearly explain their AI business model
5. **Monitor constantly** - bubble dynamics can shift rapidly; early warning indicators matter
6. **Maintain discipline** - resist competitive pressure to relax standards as market heats up

**The Bottom Line**: This bucket strategy provides a framework for **intelligent participation in AI without catastrophic exposure to bubble collapse**. By concentrating in Bucket 1 (established, cash-generative companies) and being highly selective in Bucket 2 (proven AI companies with manageable leverage), underwriters can capture AI-driven premium growth while avoiding the securities litigation tsunami that will inevitably follow if/when speculative excess unwinds.

**As Howard Marks concludes**: "Intelligent investment in data centers, and thus in AI – like everything else – requires sober, insightful judgment and skillful implementation." The same applies to D&O underwriting of AI companies.
