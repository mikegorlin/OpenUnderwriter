# Derivative Lawsuit Patterns and Hazards

## Source
Woodruff Sawyer - Five Types of Derivative Suits with Massive Settlements (2020)

## Overview
Approximately two-thirds of securities class action suits have related derivative suits, but most have no significant dollar settlement. However, certain categories consistently produce massive settlements.

## Five Major Derivative Lawsuit Categories

### 1. Board-Level Conflicts of Interest

**Description**: Directors or officers have personal financial interests that conflict with shareholders' interests, particularly in M&A transactions.

**Common Scenarios**:
- Acquisition of companies where directors/officers have ownership stakes
- Related-party transactions
- Deals that benefit executives while harming shareholders
- Self-dealing arrangements
- Narrowing definition of director independence (Delaware courts)

**Major Settlements**:
- **Tesla/SolarCity**: $60 million - Acquisition of SolarCity (founded by CEO Elon Musk's cousins) allegedly inappropriately benefited Musk and directors with SolarCity stakes
- **New Senior Investment Group**: $53 million - Acquisition of senior living properties allegedly benefited executives while shareholders lost $100+ million

**Underwriting Red Flags**:
- Related-party transactions disclosed in filings
- CEO/founder has ownership in potential acquisition targets
- Directors with financial interests in business partners
- Family relationships between executives and business counterparties
- Recent M&A activity involving related parties
- Weak board independence (Delaware standards)
- Lack of independent transaction committees

### 2. M&A Diligence Failures

**Description**: Discovery during acquiror's due diligence reveals problems that lead to diminished sale price, with shareholders claiming directors breached duties by failing to disclose or address issues.

**Common Scenarios**:
- Data breaches discovered during due diligence reducing purchase price
- Financial misstatements found during M&A process
- Errors in financial modeling for deals
- Undisclosed liabilities affecting valuation
- Failure to implement security measures before sale

**Major Settlements**:
- **Yahoo**: $29 million - First-ever monetary settlement in data breach derivative suit; directors knew of breach but failed to report or implement security measures, leading Verizon to cut purchase price by $350 million
- **Freeport-McMoRan**: $138 million - Conflicts of interest plus alleged errors by Credit Suisse in financial modeling for deal

**Underwriting Red Flags**:
- Company in active M&A discussions or sale process
- Recent cybersecurity incidents or data breaches
- Known financial restatements or accounting issues
- Pending regulatory investigations
- Weak internal controls disclosed in audits
- Third-party valuation disputes
- Due diligence findings requiring price adjustments

### 3. Egregious Behavior Directed at Consumers or Employees

**Description**: Headline-grabbing bad behavior impacting individuals, including sexual misconduct (#MeToo), massive data breaches, or systematic fraud against customers.

**Common Scenarios**:
- Sexual harassment or misconduct by executives
- Systematic fraud against customers (fake accounts, unauthorized charges)
- Massive data breaches compromising customer information
- Widespread employee misconduct to meet sales targets
- Failure to stop known harmful practices
- Culture of misconduct enabled by leadership

**Major Settlements**:
- **Wells Fargo**: $240 million - Directors breached fiduciary duties by failing to stop employees from creating fake customer accounts to boost sales figures and qualify for bonuses
- **Wynn Resorts**: $41 million - Sexual misconduct by Steve Wynn over 20-year period (#MeToo case)
- **Equifax**: $32.5 million - Cyber breach compromising 140+ million Americans' personal information, plus related insider trading allegations

**Underwriting Red Flags**:
- History of employee misconduct or whistleblower complaints
- Aggressive sales culture with commission-based compensation
- Previous data breach incidents
- Weak cybersecurity controls
- #MeToo allegations or sexual harassment claims
- High employee turnover in compliance functions
- Regulatory investigations into consumer practices
- Pattern of customer complaints
- Inadequate background checks for executives
- Weak tone at the top regarding ethical behavior

### 4. Health and Human Safety (Caremark Claims)

**Description**: Death or physical harm to the public due to inadequate board oversight and failure to address red flags about safety risks.

**Common Scenarios**:
- Product safety failures causing death or injury
- Infrastructure failures (pipeline explosions, building collapses)
- Distribution of dangerous products (opioids)
- Ignoring multiple red flags about safety issues
- Failure to maintain safety systems or records
- Inadequate risk oversight by board

**Major Settlements**:
- **McKesson Corp**: $175 million - Board didn't have adequate oversight and ignored red flags over suspicious opioid distribution, fueling opioid crisis
- **PG&E**: $90 million - Natural gas pipeline explosion and fire killed 8 people, injured many, destroyed 38 homes; directors failed to maintain pipeline safety or keep accurate records

**Legal Development**: This category may see increased activity following Delaware cases:
- *Marchand v. Barnhill* (2019) - "Bluebell Ice Cream" case
- *Clovis Oncology, Inc. Derivative Litigation*
- *Hughes v. Hu* (Kandi Technologies)

These cases underscore board's role in corporate risk oversight.

**Underwriting Red Flags**:
- Products with potential for physical harm (pharmaceuticals, medical devices, chemicals)
- Critical infrastructure operations (utilities, pipelines, transportation)
- History of safety incidents or near-misses
- Regulatory citations for safety violations
- Inadequate safety monitoring systems
- No board-level risk oversight committee
- Limited safety training or protocols
- Deferred maintenance on critical systems
- Known product defects not addressed
- Distribution of controlled substances
- Operations in high-risk industries (energy, healthcare, manufacturing)

### 5. Massive Fraud

**Description**: Egregious intentional fraud to deceive the market and artificially inflate prices. Unlike typical securities cases, these derivative suits result in significant settlements when fraud is particularly severe.

**Common Scenarios**:
- Intentional fraud in financial statements
- Systematic deception of market participants
- Artificial inflation of stock prices
- Accounting fraud resulting in criminal charges
- Executives personally enriching themselves through fraud

**Major Settlements**:
- **American Realty Capital Properties**: $287 million derivative settlement (related securities class action settled for $1+ billion) - Intentional fraud to deceive market and artificially inflate prices; former CFO sentenced to jail

**Underwriting Red Flags**:
- Financial restatements, especially multiple restatements
- SEC investigations or enforcement actions
- Criminal investigations of executives
- Whistleblower allegations of fraud
- Unusual related-party transactions
- Complex corporate structures obscuring financial reality
- Aggressive revenue recognition practices
- Frequent changes in auditors
- Qualified audit opinions
- Executives selling large amounts of stock before bad news
- Insider trading allegations
- Anonymous short-seller reports alleging fraud

## Key Underwriting Insights

### Derivative vs. Securities Class Actions
- About 2/3 of securities class actions have related derivative suits
- Most derivative suits have no significant settlement
- Exceptions: The five categories above consistently produce large settlements
- Derivative suits can exceed securities settlements in fraud cases

### Settlement Patterns
- Board-level conflicts: $50-60M typical for major cases
- M&A diligence failures: $30-140M range
- Egregious behavior: $30-240M depending on scope
- Health/safety (Caremark): $90-175M for major incidents
- Massive fraud: $200M+ possible, can exceed securities settlements

### Delaware Court Influence
- Delaware Court of Chancery sets standards for director duties
- Definition of director independence continues to narrow
- Caremark claims (oversight failures) seeing renewed attention
- Board risk oversight responsibilities expanding

### Insurance Implications
- Side A D&O coverage critical for these claims
- Settlement amounts can be substantial even without securities case
- Trend toward more aggressive derivative litigation
- Board composition and independence increasingly scrutinized

## General D&O Claim Types (Beyond Derivative Suits)

Based on search results, common D&O claims include:

1. **Breach of Fiduciary Duty** - Most common D&O claim type
2. **Misrepresentation or Fraud** - False statements to investors/stakeholders
3. **Employment Practices Claims** - Wrongful termination, discrimination, harassment
4. **Shareholder Actions** - Both direct and derivative suits
5. **Regulatory and Compliance Violations** - Failure to comply with laws/regulations
6. **Corporate Waste** - Improper or excessive executive compensation
7. **Failure to Pursue Legal Claims** - Not enforcing company rights
8. **Misappropriation of Assets** - Self-dealing or theft
9. **Non-Compliance with Corporate Formalities** - Governance failures

## Underwriting Priorities by Industry

**High-Risk Industries for Derivative Suits**:
- Financial Services (conflicts, fraud potential)
- Healthcare/Pharmaceuticals (safety, opioid-related)
- Utilities/Energy (safety, environmental)
- Technology (data breaches, cybersecurity)
- Real Estate Investment (conflicts in acquisitions)
- Retail/Consumer (customer fraud, data breaches)

**Key Risk Factors Across Industries**:
- Weak board independence
- Related-party transactions
- History of safety incidents
- Data breach vulnerabilities
- Aggressive sales cultures
- Complex M&A activity
- Regulatory scrutiny
- Prior misconduct allegations
