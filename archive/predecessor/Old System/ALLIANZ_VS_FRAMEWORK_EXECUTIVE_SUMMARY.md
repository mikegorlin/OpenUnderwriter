# Allianz D&O Insights 2026 vs. Our AI Framework
## Executive Summary & Underwriting Insights

## 1. KEY SUMMARY POINTS FROM ALLIANZ

### **The Big Picture**

Allianz's 2026 D&O outlook reveals a complex risk landscape where **geopolitical uncertainty, cyber threats, AI risks, and emerging liabilities** are converging to create heightened D&O exposure. While securities class action frequency is stabilizing (229 in 2024, expected 216 in 2025), **severity is surging** with average settlements jumping 27% to US$56 million in the first half of 2025.

The document emphasizes a fundamental shift in director responsibilities: **"The duty of a director now is greater than ever before. They need to be able to look forward, as well as back, when it comes to assessing risks to their company."** This forward-looking requirement challenges traditional risk management approaches that focus primarily on historical performance and foreseeable risks.

### **Top D&O Risk Drivers for 2026**

**Geopolitical Uncertainty** emerges as the dominant theme, with 45 armed conflicts in the Middle East and North Africa alone, combined with fluctuating tariffs and accelerating deglobalization. These factors are forcing supply chain restructuring, increasing litigation risk for companies expanding their US footprint, and creating regulatory compliance challenges. Directors face liability for failing to anticipate and adapt to geopolitical changes that impact operations, financial performance, and legal/regulatory requirements across different jurisdictions.

**Cyber risk** maintains its position as the number one risk for the fourth consecutive year in Allianz's Risk Barometer. Ransomware attacks account for 60% of the value of large cyber insurance claims, while data exfiltration incidents have doubled to represent 40% of large claims. The EU's NIS2 directive significantly increases personal accountability for D&Os, who can now be held directly responsible for overseeing cyber security, risk management, and incident response preparedness. Fines can reach €10 million or 2% of global turnover.

**Artificial intelligence** is generating a new wave of securities litigation, with 53 lawsuits filed since March 2020 and 12 in the first half of 2025 alone. The SEC's April 2025 enforcement action against AI-startup Nate, Inc for misleading investors demonstrates regulatory willingness to pursue AI-washing claims. Companies face D&O exposure from two angles: overstating AI benefits to investors and understating AI risks to profitability.

**PFAS (forever chemicals)** represents a massive emerging liability, with Verisk estimating potential US litigation costs of up to $165 billion. Thirty US states have initiated litigation against PFAS manufacturers, and 3M has already settled for $10.3 billion. The D&O exposure arises from securities litigation where shareholders allege directors failed to identify and adequately disclose PFAS liability risks.

**Bankruptcy risk** is accelerating, with global business insolvencies expected to rise 6% in 2025 and 5% in 2026, marking five consecutive years of increases and reaching 24% above pre-pandemic averages. The US has seen 32 "mega bankruptcies" (companies with over $1 billion in assets) in the past 12 months, well above the historical average of 23. Insolvency risks are particularly concentrated in automotive, construction, retail, and consumer goods sectors.

**Trade tariffs** create additional D&O exposure, with 42% of exporting companies expecting turnover to decline by 2-10% over the next 12 months. Directors face potential regulatory investigations and enforcement actions for tariff evasion or misleading disclosures, with the US government actively using the False Claims Act to enforce tariff compliance.

## 2. WHAT OUR FRAMEWORK IS MISSING OR NEEDS TO ADJUST

### **Critical Gaps**

**PFAS (Forever Chemicals) Risk** is entirely absent from our framework despite representing potentially $165 billion in litigation exposure. The Allianz document identifies this as a major emerging D&O risk, with 30 US states having initiated litigation and 3M settling for $10.3 billion. We should add PFAS exposure assessment as either Red Flag Category 8 or integrate it into our Product Liability assessment with specific questions about PFAS use in products, environmental contamination potential, and disclosure practices.

**Geopolitical Risk Assessment** is not systematically evaluated in our current framework, yet Allianz identifies it as a primary driver of D&O exposure. We need to add geopolitical exposure assessment to the Execution Capability lens, examining geographic concentration, tariff exposure, supply chain dependencies, and the company's scenario planning processes for geopolitical disruptions.

**Horizon Scanning and Emerging Risk Disclosure** represents a fundamental gap. Our framework is primarily backward-looking, focusing on historical track record and proven execution. However, Allianz emphasizes that "directors need to look forward, as well as back." We should add assessment of the company's emerging risk identification processes, task force structures, and disclosure practices for risks that haven't yet materialized but may occur.

**Cyber Governance** is mentioned in our framework but lacks detailed assessment criteria. Given that cyber is the #1 risk and NIS2 creates personal accountability for EU directors, we need specific questions about board-level cyber expertise, incident response plans, NIS2 compliance status, and investment levels in cyber security infrastructure.

**Bankruptcy Risk Indicators** are not systematically assessed. Our Financial Health lens focuses on AI investment affordability but doesn't evaluate general insolvency risk. With bankruptcies surging 24% above pre-pandemic levels and concentrated in specific sectors, we should add bankruptcy risk indicators including debt ratios, interest coverage, sector exposure, and financial distress signals.

**Trade and Tariff Exposure** is not addressed despite 42% of exporters expecting revenue declines and active FCA enforcement against tariff violations. We should add trade exposure assessment examining export dependence, tariff sensitivity, compliance processes, and geographic diversification of revenue.

### **Information Differences and Refinements**

**Securities Class Action Trends**: While our framework mentions securities litigation generally, Allianz provides specific data showing frequency stabilizing (229 in 2024, expected 216 in 2025) but severity surging (+27% to $56 million average settlement). This suggests we should price for severity risk, not just frequency, and recognize that even declining claim counts don't necessarily reduce exposure.

**AI Litigation Categories**: Our framework focuses on AI product liability (Character.AI example) but Allianz identifies two distinct categories of AI securities litigation: AI-washing (overstating benefits to investors) and understating AI risks to profitability. We should expand our AI assessment to include securities litigation risk from misleading AI-related disclosures, not just product liability from harmful AI products.

**Cyber Claims Breakdown**: Allianz provides granular data showing ransomware represents 60% of large claims value, data exfiltration has doubled to 40%, non-attack incidents account for 28%, and supply chain/CBI incidents have increased to 15%. This nuance suggests cyber risk is more complex than our framework currently captures, requiring assessment of multiple cyber exposure categories.

**Insolvency Sector Concentration**: Allianz identifies automotive, construction, retail, and consumer goods as highest-risk sectors for insolvency. Our framework should incorporate sector-specific overlays that apply enhanced financial scrutiny to companies in these industries.

## 3. UNDERWRITING INSIGHTS FOR 2026

### **Strategic Positioning**

The D&O market is experiencing a fundamental shift where **severity is rising despite stable or declining frequency**. Average settlements increased 27% to $56 million in the first half of 2025, even as total claim counts are expected to decline slightly. This pattern demands a pricing strategy focused on severity exposure rather than frequency trends. Underwriters should recognize that a company with one severe claim can generate more loss than multiple smaller claims, making quality of risk selection more critical than volume.

**Regional market dynamics** vary significantly. The US market is stabilizing but remains competitive, particularly for private D&O, with some insureds in better positions to purchase additional limits. Asia faces intense competition and rate erosion, with clients cutting insurance spending and driving broker competition. European markets show mixed signals: France is experiencing increased bankruptcy claims from both large corporations and middle-market companies; Germany is stabilizing with cyber and bankruptcy as top claims sources; the UK maintains a soft market with long-term agreements but faces escalating regulatory risk from HMRC, SFO, and FCA enforcement. Latin America represents a high-growth opportunity driven by regulatory progress on anti-corruption and ESG, with medium-sized companies offering significant expansion potential.

### **Enhanced Underwriting Assessment Framework**

**Geopolitical Risk Assessment** should become a standard component of underwriting evaluation. Key questions include: What is the company's geographic revenue concentration? What percentage of revenue comes from regions with active armed conflicts or high tariff exposure? How dependent is the company on cross-border supply chains that could be disrupted by geopolitical events? Does the company conduct scenario planning for geopolitical disruptions? Has the company appointed external advisors or board members with geopolitical expertise? How does the company monitor and disclose geopolitical risks to investors?

**Cyber Governance Review** requires deeper scrutiny given NIS2 personal accountability requirements and the 60% of large claims attributable to ransomware. Underwriters should assess: Does the board include members with cyber security expertise? Has the company implemented a documented incident response plan and business continuity plan? For EU companies, what is the status of NIS2 compliance? What is the company's annual investment in cyber security as a percentage of IT budget? Has the company experienced cyber incidents in the past three years, and how were they managed? Does the company maintain cyber insurance, and at what limits?

**Bankruptcy Risk Indicators** deserve heightened attention given the 24% increase above pre-pandemic insolvency levels. Critical metrics include: Debt-to-equity ratio and interest coverage ratio; Operating cash flow trends and ability to service debt from operations; Sector exposure (automotive, construction, retail, consumer goods carry elevated risk); Customer concentration and exposure to financially distressed customers; Liquidity position and access to credit facilities; Management's track record navigating economic downturns.

**Emerging Risk Disclosure Practices** should be evaluated to assess forward-looking risk management. Questions include: Does the company maintain an emerging risks task force or horizon scanning process? How does the company identify and evaluate risks that haven't yet materialized (PFAS, AI, new regulations)? What is the quality and timeliness of risk disclosures in SEC filings or equivalent? Has the company been subject to securities litigation for failure to disclose emerging risks? Does the board receive regular briefings on emerging risks from external experts?

### **Sector-Specific Underwriting Overlays**

**Technology and AI Companies** require assessment of AI-washing risk and disclosure quality. Underwriters should examine: What percentage of revenue or market valuation is attributed to AI capabilities? How specific and verifiable are the company's AI-related claims to investors? Has the company disclosed potential AI risks including regulatory uncertainty, competition, and technology limitations? Does the company have processes to validate AI performance claims before public disclosure? Has the company been subject to SEC inquiries or securities litigation related to AI disclosures?

**Manufacturing and Industrial Companies** face PFAS exposure that could generate massive liability. Key assessment areas include: Does the company manufacture or use PFAS chemicals in products or operations? What is the company's environmental contamination history and remediation track record? How has the company disclosed PFAS exposure and potential liability to investors? Has the company been named in PFAS-related litigation or regulatory investigations? What insurance coverage does the company maintain for environmental and product liability?

**Exporters and Trade-Dependent Companies** confront tariff compliance risk and revenue volatility. Underwriters should evaluate: What percentage of revenue comes from exports, and to which countries? How sensitive is the company's business model to tariff changes? What compliance processes does the company maintain for tariff classification and payment? Has the company been subject to customs investigations or FCA complaints? How does the company scenario plan for trade policy changes?

**Automotive, Construction, Retail, and Consumer Goods Sectors** carry elevated insolvency risk requiring enhanced financial scrutiny. Additional diligence should include: Detailed cash flow analysis and working capital trends; Debt maturity schedules and refinancing risk; Customer payment trends and days sales outstanding; Supplier concentration and supply chain stability; Management's contingency plans for revenue declines or margin compression.

### **Pricing and Terms Guidance**

**Severity-Driven Pricing**: With average settlements at $56 million and rising, underwriters should model pricing based on severity exposure rather than frequency alone. Companies with characteristics associated with high-severity claims (large market capitalization, high-profile AI or technology claims, PFAS exposure, cyber incidents with data exfiltration) warrant premium loading even if overall claim frequency is declining.

**Geopolitical and Trade Risk Surcharges**: Companies with significant export dependence (>30% of revenue), high tariff exposure, or operations in conflict zones should receive surcharges of 15-30% to reflect elevated regulatory investigation and securities litigation risk.

**Cyber Governance Credits**: Companies demonstrating strong cyber governance (board expertise, NIS2 compliance, documented incident response, adequate cyber insurance) may warrant credits of 10-20%, as Allianz data shows proactive management significantly reduces litigation and regulatory action risk.

**Sector-Specific Adjustments**: Apply surcharges of 20-40% for companies in high-insolvency sectors (automotive, construction, retail, consumer goods) unless strong financial metrics and liquidity position justify lower pricing. Manufacturing companies with PFAS exposure may warrant surcharges of 30-50% or specific PFAS exclusions depending on disclosure quality and litigation status.

**Emerging Risk Disclosure Quality**: Companies with robust emerging risk identification processes, task forces, and high-quality disclosure practices may warrant credits of 10-15%, as these practices reduce securities litigation risk from failure to disclose emerging liabilities.

### **Coverage Considerations**

**Side A DIC Coverage**: Given heightened insolvency risk and the potential for company insolvency to leave directors without indemnification, underwriters should actively market Side A difference-in-conditions coverage, particularly for companies in high-insolvency sectors or with elevated debt levels.

**PFAS Exclusions**: For manufacturing companies with known PFAS exposure and inadequate disclosure, consider PFAS-specific exclusions or sublimits to manage potentially catastrophic exposure. However, recognize that D&O policies covering securities litigation for failure to disclose PFAS risks may still respond even with product liability exclusions.

**Cyber-Related D&O Coverage**: Clarify the interaction between D&O and cyber insurance policies, particularly for claims arising from cyber incidents. Ensure coordination between D&O and cyber underwriters to avoid gaps or disputes over which policy responds to shareholder litigation following ransomware attacks or data breaches.

**Geographic Scope**: For companies with significant international operations, carefully evaluate whether policy territory should be worldwide or limited based on litigation risk in specific jurisdictions. US exposure remains highest-risk given litigious environment and escalating settlement values.

## 4. ACTIONABLE RECOMMENDATIONS

### **Immediate Framework Enhancements**

**Add Red Flag Category 8: PFAS Exposure**: Create automatic decline or elevated pricing triggers for companies with significant PFAS manufacturing, use in products, or environmental contamination history without adequate disclosure and insurance coverage.

**Expand Lens 1 (Red Flags) to Include Geopolitical Red Flags**: Add automatic decline triggers for companies with >50% revenue concentration in active conflict zones, companies under active tariff-related FCA investigation, or companies with documented tariff evasion.

**Enhance Lens 3 (Financial Health) with Bankruptcy Indicators**: Add specific assessment of debt-to-equity ratio, interest coverage, and sector-specific insolvency risk. Companies in automotive, construction, retail, or consumer goods with debt-to-equity >300% or interest coverage <2x should receive elevated scrutiny.

**Upgrade Lens 4 (Execution Capability) with Cyber Governance Assessment**: Add detailed questions about board cyber expertise, NIS2 compliance status, incident response plans, and cyber security investment levels. Companies lacking board cyber expertise or documented incident response plans should receive surcharges.

**Create Lens 5: Emerging Risk Management**: Add new lens evaluating the company's forward-looking risk identification processes, including horizon scanning, task forces, external expert engagement, and disclosure quality for emerging risks (PFAS, AI, geopolitical, new regulations).

### **Underwriting Process Changes**

**Implement Sector-Specific Overlays**: Develop detailed underwriting guidelines for high-risk sectors including technology/AI (AI-washing assessment), manufacturing (PFAS exposure), exporters (tariff compliance), and high-insolvency sectors (enhanced financial review).

**Require Geopolitical Risk Questionnaire**: For all companies with >20% international revenue, require completion of geopolitical risk questionnaire assessing geographic concentration, tariff exposure, supply chain dependencies, and scenario planning processes.

**Mandate Cyber Governance Review**: For all companies, require assessment of board cyber expertise, incident response plans, and (for EU companies) NIS2 compliance status. Companies failing minimum cyber governance standards should receive surcharges or coverage limitations.

**Establish Emerging Risk Disclosure Scoring**: Develop scoring methodology evaluating quality of company's emerging risk identification and disclosure practices. Use scores to inform pricing and terms, with high-quality disclosure practices warranting credits and poor practices warranting surcharges.

### **Market Positioning and Communication**

**Position as Forward-Looking Underwriter**: Differentiate by emphasizing assessment of emerging risks and forward-looking risk management, not just historical performance. Use Allianz's "directors need to look forward, as well as back" messaging to explain enhanced underwriting requirements.

**Educate Brokers and Clients on Severity Trends**: Communicate that while claim frequency may be stabilizing, severity is surging (+27% to $56 million average), requiring pricing adjustments even for companies without recent claims history.

**Highlight Geopolitical and Cyber as Top Priorities**: Use Allianz data (45 armed conflicts, cyber as #1 risk for 4th consecutive year) to justify enhanced underwriting scrutiny in these areas and explain surcharges or coverage limitations for high-risk exposures.

**Offer Cyber Governance Consulting**: Differentiate by offering cyber governance consulting services to help companies improve board expertise, implement incident response plans, and achieve NIS2 compliance, potentially qualifying for pricing credits.

## 5. CONCLUSION

The Allianz D&O Insights 2026 document validates the core structure of our AI underwriting framework—focusing on optionality, financial health, and execution capability—while revealing critical gaps in geopolitical risk, PFAS exposure, bankruptcy indicators, cyber governance, and emerging risk disclosure. The fundamental insight is that **director duties are expanding from backward-looking oversight to forward-looking horizon scanning**, requiring underwriters to assess not just historical performance but also the quality of emerging risk identification and disclosure processes.

The market environment for 2026 is characterized by **rising severity despite stable frequency**, with average settlements increasing 27% even as total claim counts decline slightly. This pattern demands pricing discipline focused on severity exposure and careful risk selection to avoid high-severity claims from geopolitical disruptions, cyber incidents, AI-washing allegations, PFAS litigation, or bankruptcy-related director liability.

Our framework is strong but needs to become more comprehensive and forward-looking. By adding PFAS assessment, geopolitical risk evaluation, bankruptcy indicators, cyber governance review, and emerging risk disclosure scoring, we can position ourselves as sophisticated underwriters who understand the evolving D&O landscape and can accurately price for 2026's complex risk environment.

The CoreWeave, Oracle, and Broadcom examples from recent market events provide real-time validation of our framework's core principles while highlighting the importance of the enhancements recommended in this analysis. Companies that cannot afford AI investments from operating cash flow, maintain excessive customer concentration, attempt circular deals, or lack robust governance face severe market consequences—and corresponding D&O exposure—exactly as our framework predicts.
