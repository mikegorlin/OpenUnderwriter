- In re Vicuron Pharmas. (E.D. Pa. 2005): Allowed claims re positive statements about Phase III results where court was convinced defendant actually knew clinical results were problematic and approval was unlikely

### C. THE RISKIEST AREA: Disclosures Made Relative to FDA Feedback
One category of statements sticks out as PARTICULARLY TROUBLESOME for defendants: alleged misrepresentations concerning feedback from or interactions with the FDA.

On one hand, courts have held: "[N]umerous courts have concluded that a defendant pharmaceutical company does not have a duty to reveal interim FDA criticism regarding study design or methodology. Indeed, such courts frequently reason that interim FDA feedback is not material because dialogue between the FDA and pharmaceutical companies remain ongoing throughout the licensing process, rendering such criticism subject to change and not binding in regards to ultimate licensing approval." - Vallabhaneni v. Endocyte (S.D. Ind. 2016)

Tongue v. Sanofi (2d Cir. 2016): "Reasonable investors understand that dialogue with the FDA is an integral part of the drug approval process, and no sophisticated investor familiar with standard FDA practice would expect that every view of the data taken by Defendants was shared by the FDA."

BUT: When companies AFFIRMATIVELY MISCHARACTERIZE specific FDA feedback, courts are much less forgiving. This is the SINGLE BIGGEST RED FLAG for underwriters.

### PRACTICE TIP FOR UNDERWRITERS:
Best practice for biotech companies: accurately disclose the details of their clinical trial methodology and underlying data along with the company's interpretation of that data, to avoid plausible claims of subterfuge later on.

### C (continued): THE CRITICAL DISTINCTION - FDA Feedback Types

Courts carefully distinguish between:
1. **Optimistic projections regarding approval** = opinions/protected forward-looking statements (SAFE)
2. **Statements regarding past FDA interactions or feedback** = verifiable historical facts (DANGEROUS)

Key case: In re Mannkind Sec. Actions (C.D. Cal. 2011): Court REFUSED to dismiss where defendants repeatedly assured FDA had "blessed," "approved," "accepted," and "agreed to" the company's methodological approach, when it later became clear the FDA had done no such thing.

Court quote: "Courts must of course be careful to distinguish between forward-looking statements later deemed to be unduly optimistic, and statements of historical fact later shown to be FALSE when made.... [S]tatements touting the merits of the bioequivalency studies, can be fairly read as misguided opinion or 'corporate optimism,' [but] it is harder to escape the conclusion that Defendants' statements concerning the FDA cross the line from exaggeration and 'corporate optimism' into outright misstatement of historical fact."

### SPECIAL PROTOCOL ASSESSMENT (SPA) RISK:
In re Cell Therapeutics (W.D. Wa. 2011): Court allowed claims to move forward regarding defendants' repeated statements that its SPA—an agreement with the FDA that the drug would be approved if the company followed the agreed-upon protocol—was still in effect even after defendants knew they had invalidated the SPA.

Additional cases where FDA feedback mischaracterization survived:
- Tomaszewski v. Trevena (E.D. Pa. 2020): Declined to dismiss claims premised on omission of specific negative FDA feedback where defendant subsequently represented that the FDA "approved" of the study design
- Frater v. Hemispherx (E.D. Pa. 2014): Declined to dismiss where company (1) omitted FDA statements indicating it probably would not be receptive to company's intended clinical approach, and (2) incorrectly stated the FDA had withdrawn its request for a new clinical trial

### UNDERWRITER RED FLAG CHECKLIST - FDA Communications:
The question for underwriters is: Does the company make SPECIFIC FACTUAL CLAIMS about what the FDA said/did, or does it express OPINIONS about likely approval? The former is far more dangerous.

### PRACTICE TIPS FOR COMPANIES (AND UNDERWRITING SIGNALS):

1. **Companies need to be careful not to mislead.** Selective disclosure of some facts but not others can create difficulties. If a company chooses to disclose interim FDA feedback, it should do so fairly, reporting both positive AND significant negative components of that feedback at the same time.

2. **Companies should be careful not to overstate or misconstrue FDA opinions.** These can later be contradicted by the agency when an approval decision is made. A biotech company most often will be best served by couching any optimism in terms of THE COMPANY'S opinions and expectations—rather than positively characterizing the FDA's feelings or intentions—and sticking to accurate, factual accounts of FDA feedback.

### V. CONCLUSION
Both the original study and updated analysis show that, contrary to popular belief, development-stage biotech companies actually have LESS to fear from federal securities cases than do many other types of corporate defendants that have a far easier time securing insurance coverage. Over the last decade and a half, these cases have been dismissed at a high rate early in the litigation process. Courts are sympathetic to the inherent risks of the industry and seem primed to dismiss these suits when defendants can present a credible narrative of good faith conduct.
