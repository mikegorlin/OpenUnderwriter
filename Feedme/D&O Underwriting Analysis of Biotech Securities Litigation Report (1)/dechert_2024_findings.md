# Dechert Survey 2024: Securities Fraud Class Actions Against U.S. Life Sciences Companies

## Source: Dechert LLP, March 2025

### Filing Volume
- 50 securities class action lawsuits against life sciences companies in 2024
- Over 1 in 5 of ALL securities class actions (22.2%)
- Increase of over 15% from prior year
- 2023: 43 of 215 (20%), 2022: 43 of 197 (21.8%), 2021: 59 of 212, 2020: 80 of 317

### Types of Claims Filed in 2024 (of 50 complaints):
- 52% (26 of 50): Product efficacy and safety misrepresentations
- 34% (17 of 50): Regulatory hurdles, FDA timing, application sufficiency
- 34% (17 of 50): Financial reporting misrepresentations
- 20% (10 of 50): Mergers, sales, IPOs, offerings, other transactions
- 14% (7 of 50): Non-U.S. issuers incorporated abroad
- 6% (3 of 50): COVID-19 related

### Market Capitalization Breakdown (Figure 3):
- $0-250M: 20 lawsuits (MOST FREQUENT - small cap)
- $250-500M: 5 lawsuits
- $500-750M: 6 lawsuits
- $750M-1B: 0 lawsuits
- $1-2B: 5 lawsuits
- $2-5B: 6 lawsuits
- $5-10B: 2 lawsuits
- $10B+: 6 lawsuits

### KEY FINDING FOR UNDERWRITING: Market Cap Distribution
- Small cap companies ($0-$2B) account for the MAJORITY of filings
- But large cap companies ($10B+) had 6 filings - significant concentration
- Moderate decrease in large cap claims from prior year
- Slight INCREASE in small cap claims

### Plaintiff Law Firm Concentration:
- Pomerantz LLP: 22 complaints (44% of all life sciences filings!)
- Levi & Korsinsky: 9 complaints
- The Schall Law Firm: 7 complaints
- The Rosen Law Firm: 6 complaints
- These 4 firms account for ~75% of first-filed complaints

### Geographic Concentration:
- Ninth Circuit saw most filings in 2024 (shift from Third Circuit in 2023)
- Southern District of New York: highest number of filings within any single district court


### Causes of Action Categories (Detailed):
1. Product Efficacy and Safety (52%): Largest category. Misrepresentations regarding product efficacy/safety that could impact FDA approval likelihood.
2. Regulatory Hurdles (34%): FDA timing, application sufficiency, regulatory hurdles
3. Financial Reporting (34%): Internal controls, restatements, material weakness
4. Transactions (20%): Mergers, acquisitions, IPOs, offerings
5. COVID-19 (6%): Declining trend, expected to continue decreasing
6. Non-U.S. Issuers (14%): Companies incorporated abroad but listed in U.S.

### Key Case Examples (2024):
- Caribou Biosciences: Clinical-stage gene-edited allogeneic cell therapy company. Sued for overstating CB-010 clinical results and commercial prospects. Stock dropped after discontinuing preclinical research and reducing workforce 12%.
- Moderna: Sued for overstating mRNA-1345 efficacy and failing to disclose true clinical performance. Stock fell after FDA approval revealed lower-than-expected efficacy.
- Ardelyx: Biotech focused on CKD. Sued after disclosing it had not intended to apply for critical Medicare adjustment, causing 30% stock drop.
- BioNTech: Sued for overstating Comirnaty demand and commercial prospects, failing to disclose excess inventory and write-off risk.
- AstraZeneca: Sued for failing to disclose insurance fraud in China, legal risks, and detention of China President.
- Lifecore Biomedical: Material weakness in internal controls, restatement, 16 disclosures in 18 months leading to multiple stock declines.
- Orthofix Medical: Merger-related claims. Executives terminated for cause after investigation revealed code of conduct violations.

### KEY INSIGHT: Life sciences companies face DUAL exposure:
1. Industry-specific issues (clinical trials, product safety, FDA approval)
2. General corporate issues (financial reporting, M&A, internal controls)
Both categories generate significant litigation.


### 2024 Court Decisions Analysis (Dechert)

#### Decision Volume:
- 19 opinions in 2024 (significant decrease from 49 in 2023)
- Three broad categories of decisions:
  1. Claims during development phase (stock drop after clinical trial failure, overly optimistic FDA approval statements)
  2. Claims independent of or after development process
  3. Financial management/general mismanagement claims

#### Product Development Decisions (8 of 19):
- Courts granted 4 of 8 motions to dismiss in entirety
- 4 motions denied in part
- Defendants challenge and defeat claims in approximately HALF of these actions
- Key case: Fernandes v. Centessa Pharmaceuticals - dismissed because "nearly all challenged statements were statements of opinion"
- Key case: In re Spero Therapeutics - dismissed on scienter grounds. Court found plaintiffs failed to show defendants' decision to enroll fewer patients was made with defrauding intent (was due to COVID-19 operational challenges)

#### Post-Development Decisions (11 of 19):
- 6 motions dismissed in whole
- 3 motions denied
- 2 dismissed in part
- Key case: Ortmann v. Aurinia Pharms - dismissed with prejudice. Court found amended complaint failed to assert any particular statement was false despite alleging "materially false and misleading statements and omissions"

#### Financial Management Decisions (14 of 19):
- 9 cases dismissed in whole in favor of defendants
- 3 cases plaintiffs prevailed
- 2 dismissed in part
- Key case: In re BioLineRx - dismissed. Court rejected plaintiffs' theory that company lied about having sufficient funds. Court found raising capital doesn't prove prior statements about funds were false.

### KEY DECHERT INSIGHT FOR UNDERWRITERS:
The Dechert data confirms that courts are applying PSLRA standards rigorously. Even when plaintiffs can allege falsity, courts are independently analyzing scienter and often finding it insufficient. This is a STRUCTURAL ADVANTAGE for biotech defendants.

### Insider Trading as Scienter Evidence:
- In re Spero Therapeutics: Plaintiffs alleged insider transactions and increased executive compensation as evidence of motive
- Court found this INSUFFICIENT to establish scienter
- BUT: insider trading remains a key factor courts examine - it just needs to be more than routine
